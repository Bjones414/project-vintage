# Porsche Road Cars: 2010s Reference

## Era Context

The 2010s is the most product-dense decade in Porsche's history. The decade opens with the 997.2-platform 911 still in showrooms and closes with the 991.2 Speedster being delivered as the final 991-platform car in December 2019, while the all-new 992-generation 911 begins shipping in parallel. Bracketed within those ten years are three sub-generations of the 911 (997.2, 991.1, 991.2), two full generations of mid-engine Boxster/Cayman (987.2 carryover, 981, 718), two Cayenne generations plus the launch of a third (957 final year, 958.1, 958.2, 9Y0 launch), two Panamera generations (970.1, 970.2, 971), the launch of a brand-new vehicle line (95B Macan, MY2015), and Porsche's first hybrid hypercar (918 Spyder, 2014–2015). It is also the decade in which Porsche's powertrain philosophy decisively turned: turbocharging spread from the Turbo trim into the base Carrera (991.2 in MY2017), then into the Boxster and Cayman base/S/GTS (718 in MY2017), while plug-in hybrids entered the Cayenne and Panamera lineups (MY2014). The naturally aspirated flat-six road-car line ended on the GT cars only, and the Mezger engine — the dry-sump motorsport-derived flat-six that had powered every road-going GT3, GT3 RS, GT2, GT2 RS and Turbo through the 997.2 — left road-car production in 2011 with the GT3 RS 4.0 and GT2 RS.

Commercially the decade is defined by the Cayenne and Macan. Porsche's annual production passed 200,000 cars for the first time in 2014 and exceeded 250,000 by 2016, with the Macan becoming the company's best-selling model from 2015 onward and the Cayenne and Macan together accounting for the majority of unit volume. The 911 became the third-most-produced Porsche by volume despite the 991 being the highest-volume 911 generation ever made (233,540 units, per Porsche's official end-of-generation announcement). The Panamera moved to a new platform in MY2017 (971) with a Sport Turismo wagon body added in MY2018. The Macan launched on the MLB platform shared with the Audi Q5; the 9Y0 Cayenne (MY2019) launched on MLB Evo shared with the Bentley Bentayga, Lamborghini Urus, and Audi Q7.

The decade also reshaped 911 GT-car culture. The 991.1 GT3 (MY2014) was offered only with PDK — no manual — which provoked a sustained enthusiast backlash and eventually the 991 R (MY2016, 991 units), a manual-transmission, no-rear-wing reinterpretation of the GT3 RS that promptly became one of the most expensive 911s on the secondary market. The 991.2 GT3 (MY2018) restored the manual gearbox option, the 991.2 GT3 Touring (MY2018) extended the manual-plus-no-wing format as a no-cost option, and the 991.2 Speedster (MY2019, 1,948 units) closed the generation manual-only. By the end of the decade, Porsche's relationship-based allocation system for GT cars meant that MSRP for a GT3 RS, GT2 RS, R, or Speedster was largely a fictional anchor; secondary-market premiums of 50–200 percent over MSRP were routine, and dealer relationships were a precondition of purchase. Over the same span, the 918 Spyder (918 units, MY2014–MY2015) served as the technological halo and spiritual successor to the Carrera GT, introducing the plug-in hybrid V8 architecture that informed Porsche's later electrification work. By the close of the decade Porsche's lineup consisted of the 992-generation 911 (just launching), the 718 Boxster/Cayman, the new 9Y0 Cayenne, the 95B Macan, the 971 Panamera with Sport Turismo, and the discontinued 918 Spyder. The all-electric Taycan launched MY2020 and is covered in the 2020s file.

## 911 (997.2 carryover, 2010–2012)

### Generation Overview

The 997.2 was the second-half facelift of the 997-generation 911. It launched in MY2009 with direct-injection 9A1 flat-sixes for the base Carrera and Carrera S, and continued production through calendar 2012 (final cars produced into early 2013 for the Turbo trims). Only the 2010–2012 portion of production is in scope for this file; MY2009 is covered in the 2000s file.

The defining technical fact of the 997.2 is the coexistence of two distinct flat-six engine families in the same generation. The base/S/4/4S/GTS/Targa cars used the new 9A1 direct-injection 3.6L and 3.8L flat-sixes (introduced MY2009), paired with either a six-speed manual or the new seven-speed PDK dual-clutch transmission. The Turbo, Turbo S, GT3, GT3 RS, GT3 RS 4.0, and GT2 RS used the older Mezger dry-sump motorsport-derived flat-six in 3.6L (Turbo) or 3.8L (Turbo S, GT3 family) form. The 997.2 is therefore the last 911 generation to feature the Mezger engine in any road-car application; from the 991 onward, all 911s use 9A1-family architectures.

Chassis code: 997 (final phase). Body construction: steel monocoque. Wheelbase: 92.5 in (2,350 mm). Drivetrains: rear-wheel drive (Carrera, Carrera S, GTS, GT3, GT3 RS, GT2 RS, Speedster, Sport Classic) or all-wheel drive (Carrera 4, 4S, 4 GTS, Targa 4, Targa 4S, Turbo, Turbo S).

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 997.2 Carryover

The 997.2 GT3 RS 4.0 is the singular halo car of the 997-platform finale. It mounts a 4.0L NA flat-six built on the GT3 RSR motorsport crankshaft (longer 80.4 mm stroke) and carried chassis components and aerodynamic detail (front dive planes) developed for the GT2 RS. Power is rated 500 PS (493 bhp) at 8,250 rpm; weight is approximately 1,370 kg. The car was offered exclusively with a six-speed manual gearbox. Production is hard-capped at 600 units worldwide; the GT3 RS Registry and CBA/PORS9 sales database report 158 cars (141 US + 17 Canada) delivered through Porsche Cars North America. UK RHD allocation was approximately 30 cars. The 4.0 is the final road-going Porsche to use the Mezger flat-six in any displacement, and is widely treated as the apex 997 from a collector standpoint.

The 997.2 GT2 RS is the parallel halo from the same 2011 model year. It uses an evolved 3.6L Mezger twin-turbo making 620 PS, with revised intercoolers, larger turbochargers, and shorter intake manifolds versus the 997.1 GT2. Six-speed manual only; rear-wheel drive only; production cap 500 units worldwide. Per US-market press materials and registry data, 132 US-allocation cars were delivered through PCNA. It remains, alongside the GT3 RS 4.0, the final road-going Mezger turbo. Pricing at launch was €237,578 (DE, with VAT) and approximately $245,000 in the US, plus options. One US-market car was destroyed in a high-profile post-purchase crash, leaving 499 surviving examples per period reporting.

The 997.2 Sport Classic (MY2010) is the first modern Porsche Exclusive limited-series production car. It is based on the Carrera S widebody but uses the wider Carrera 4 rear bodywork on a rear-wheel-drive chassis, mounts the X51-power-kit 3.8L 9A1 NA engine making 408 hp, and is offered exclusively in Sport Classic Grey paint with a single interior specification. The car features a fixed ducktail rear spoiler, a double-bubble roof panel, Fuchs-style 19-inch alloys, and PCCB ceramic brakes as standard. Production is capped at 250 units worldwide; the entire allocation reportedly sold through within 48 hours of order books opening. The Sport Classic was not federalized for the US as a new car (RoW-only allocation); US-resident owners at the time would have imported through Show & Display channels or relied on the 25-year EPA/DOT exemption pathway in later years. Approximately 32 RHD cars were delivered to the UK.

The 997.2 Speedster (MY2011) is the fourth production 911 Speedster (after the 1989 G-body, 1993–1994 964, and now the 2011 997.2). Production is capped at 356 units worldwide — a number chosen to reference the original Porsche 356 from which the Speedster name derives. The car features a 60–77 mm shorter, more raked windscreen versus the standard Cabriolet, a manually folding roof, and the signature double-bubble tonneau cover. It uses the Carrera 4 widebody on a rear-wheel-drive chassis, the 408 hp X51 3.8L NA flat-six, and is offered exclusively with the 7-speed PDK transmission — no manual was available. Color choices were limited to Pure Blue (a Speedster-exclusive color) or Carrara White. Approximately 108 US-allocation cars were delivered.

The 997.2 GT3 (MY2010–MY2011) carried over the 3.8L Mezger NA flat-six rated 435 hp at 7,600 rpm with an 8,500 rpm redline. It was the last 911 GT3 with a manual-only transmission until the 991.2 GT3 (MY2018) restored the option after the 991.1 GT3 PDK-only era. Center-lock wheels, lighter forged pistons, and a stability-control system available for the first time on a 997 GT3 are notable features. The 997.2 GT3 RS (MY2010–MY2011) shares the Mezger 3.8 (450 hp), wider rear track, lower ride height, magnesium roof on later production, and a fixed carbon-fiber rear wing. Worldwide production of the 3.8 RS is variously cited at 1,600 to 2,000 cars across sources; the GT3 RS Registry consensus settles at approximately 2,000 worldwide with 543 US allocation per the C02 build code.

The 997.2 Turbo and Turbo S use the 3.8L Mezger twin-turbo flat-six. The standard Turbo is 500 hp (with manual or PDK availability — the first Turbo to offer PDK). The Turbo S, introduced for MY2011, increases output to 530 hp via revised intake valve timing and a carbon-fiber airbox, with PCCB and Sport Chrono Plus standard, PDK only. The Turbo S Cabriolet variant followed shortly thereafter. A small (10-unit) Turbo S 10 Years Anniversary Edition for China and a 918 Spyder Edition Turbo S limited to 918 buyers of the actual 918 Spyder are documented Manufaktur cars from this period. The 918 Spyder Edition Turbo S — Coupe ($160,700 base) or Cabriolet ($172,100 base) — is offered only to confirmed 918 Spyder owners, used Acid Green accents matching the 918, and was produced in a maximum of 918 examples (matching the donor model's name).

The Carrera GTS (MY2011) and Carrera 4 GTS (MY2012) revived the GTS designation in the modern 911 lineup. Both use the X51-power-kit 3.8L NA flat-six (408 hp), the Carrera 4 widebody, center-lock wheels, sport exhaust, PASM, and Alcantara interior trim as standard. The Carrera GTS is rear-wheel drive; the Carrera 4 GTS adds AWD. Production figures are not publicly broken out per year but PCA-Mart and dealer-reported figures suggest ~2,300 units of each across the two-year run.

### Sub-Generation Splits — 997.2

The 997.2 production span splits cleanly into a "first half" (calendar 2009–2010, covered partly in the 2000s file) and a "second half" (calendar 2011–2012, fully in this file). The 2011–2012 portion of 997.2 production is dominated by the Manufaktur and GT-car limited editions (Sport Classic, Speedster, GT3 RS 4.0, GT2 RS) plus the GTS family launch and the Turbo S debut. From a valuation standpoint, the second-half 997.2 contains every car commonly cited as a "997 collectible" — none of the 997.1 cars and only the 997.2 GT3 RS (full run) cross over from the first half into the same valuation tier. The split also affects the Turbo: the 997.2 Turbo and Turbo S are MY2010–MY2013 production, with US-market 2013 Turbo and Turbo S cars overlapping calendar-2013 with the launch of the 991.1.


## 911 (991.1, MY2012–MY2016)

### Generation Overview

The 991.1 was the first all-new 911 platform since the 996 launched for MY1999, fourteen years earlier. It debuted at the 2011 Frankfurt Motor Show as a MY2012 model in Europe and was new ground in nearly every dimension: a longer wheelbase (2,450 mm, +100 mm versus the 997), an aluminum-steel hybrid body shell that reduced curb weight by approximately 45 kg despite increased size and content, electromechanical power steering replacing the 997's hydraulic rack, and a re-positioned transaxle that shifted the rear wheels approximately 76 mm rearward to improve weight distribution. Per Porsche's own retrospective, roughly 90 percent of the 991's components were new or substantially redesigned compared with the 997 it replaced.

The 991.1 used the 9A1 family naturally aspirated flat-six in 3.4L (Carrera, Carrera 4, Targa 4, base trims) and 3.8L (Carrera S, Carrera 4S, Targa 4S, GTS family) configurations, paired with either a 7-speed manual gearbox (a new design with a tall seventh gear used as an overdrive) or the 7-speed PDK. The Turbo and Turbo S used a new 3.8L 9A1 twin-turbo flat-six developing 520 PS / 560 PS respectively, with PDK-only transmission and standard AWD. The GT3 (MY2014) used a new 3.8L NA flat-six derived from the 9A1 architecture but extensively reworked for 9,000 rpm operation, breaking with the Mezger lineage. The GT3 RS (MY2016) increased displacement to a 4.0L NA flat-six and offered PDK only — no manual transmission was offered on either the GT3 or the GT3 RS during the 991.1 generation.

Chassis code: 991 (phase 1, internally 991 I). Body construction: aluminum-steel hybrid monocoque. Wheelbase: 96.5 in (2,450 mm). Targa returned to the classic Targa-bar design (rather than the 996/997 sliding-glass canopy), with an automated motorized soft-top mechanism marketed as the "Targa 4" and "Targa 4S."

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 991.1

The 991.1 is the first 911 generation in which the GT3 and GT3 RS are not Mezger-engined. The 9A1-derived motor in the MY2014 GT3 is a fundamentally different unit from the 997.2 GT3's Mezger 3.8: it uses direct injection, a 9,000 rpm redline, and is paired with a 7-speed PDK (a motorsport-derived gearbox sharing little internally with the standard 911 PDK). The MY2014 GT3 was not offered with a manual transmission — the first time in the GT3 lineage. This decision was treated as controversial in enthusiast press and on owner forums; it was also colored by an early-production engine fire problem that triggered a recall and engine replacement on the first 785 units worldwide. Production resumed after the engine swap program completed.

The MY2016 991.1 GT3 RS uses the 4.0L NA derivative of the 9A1 architecture, with rear-axle steering (introduced on the GT3), front-fender vents over the wheels, intake ducts taken from the 911 Turbo's location, and a magnesium roof on later-build cars. Like the GT3 it is PDK-only, which extended the manual-omitted-from-GT-cars era. The GT3 RS was offered with an optional Lightweight Package (with carbon-fiber bucket seats, magnesium wheels, six-point harness, and other reductions). Production estimates worldwide range from approximately 4,250 (registry consensus) to 4,520 (PORS9/CBA-derived figure) to 5,000 (a number sometimes cited in dealer marketing); the registry-derived figure is the most defensible, with NA allocation approximately 1,530 cars (1,318 US + 212 Canada).

The 911 R (MY2016) is the architectural and emotional response to the PDK-only GT3 controversy. It uses the 4.0 NA flat-six from the GT3 RS (500 hp) but pairs it with a six-speed manual gearbox (a 997 GT-derived unit), eliminates the fixed rear wing in favor of a deployable 991-Carrera-derived rear spoiler, deletes the rear seats and roll cage, and uses carbon front fenders, hood, and front decklid plus a magnesium roof. Curb weight is approximately 1,370 kg. Production is hard-capped at 991 units worldwide — a number chosen to match the 991 chassis code. Porsche announced the car at the 2016 Geneva Motor Show; secondary-market markups of 100–200 percent over the $185,000 US MSRP appeared within days of the announcement. NA allocation is approximately 323–325 cars per the 911 R Registry. The 911 R is architecturally a 991.1 despite arriving late in the 991.1 cycle, alongside the launch of 991.2 base Carreras for MY2017.

The 50th Anniversary Edition (MY2014) commemorates the 50th anniversary of the 911's 1963 Frankfurt Motor Show debut; production is capped at 1,963 units worldwide, matching the year. The car is based on the Carrera S but uses the Carrera 4S widebody on a rear-wheel-drive chassis, includes the X51 Power Kit (430 hp) as standard, runs PASM and PTV with mechanical LSD as standard, and offers Pepita houndstooth seat inserts, period-correct green-numbered gauges with white needles and silver pointer caps, and "Fuchs"-inspired 20-inch alloy wheels. Three colors were offered: Geyser Grey Metallic (a 50th-Anniversary-exclusive shade), Graphite Grey, and Basalt Black. North American allocation is documented at approximately 750 units across PDK and manual builds. The car is the most numerous 991.1 limited edition.

The 991.1 Carrera GTS family (MY2015–MY2016) introduced the Coupe, Cabriolet, Carrera 4 Coupe and Cabriolet, and a unique-to-991.1 Targa 4 GTS — the first 911 Targa GTS. All use the X51-power-kit 3.8L NA flat-six (430 hp), Sport Chrono Package, sport exhaust, dynamic engine mounts, 10 mm lower suspension, PTV, and 20-inch center-lock wheels as standard. The Targa 4 GTS is mechanically identical to the Carrera 4 GTS but uses the Targa body; production volumes are not publicly broken out per body style.

Three notable PCA-affiliated and Manufaktur limited editions sit in the 991.1 GTS family. The Carrera S Club Coupe (MY2012, 13 worldwide) is a Brewster-Green Carrera S with X51 power kit (430 hp), ducktail spoiler, and yellow PCCB calipers — produced as commemorative cars and not standard-distribution. The Carrera GTS Club Coupe (MY2016, 60 worldwide) commemorates the 60th anniversary of the Porsche Club of America; all 60 cars went to North America and feature unique "Club Blau" paint, SportDesign bodywork, ducktail spoiler, and the same 20-inch wheels used on the 50th Anniversary. The Carrera GTS Rennsport Reunion Edition (MY2016, 25 worldwide, all NA) is finished in Fashion Gray with custom decals and was sold to attendees of Rennsport Reunion V at Mazda Raceway Laguna Seca. All three are valued primarily on rarity rather than performance differentiation.

### Sub-Generation Splits — 991.1 vs 991.2

The 991.1 → 991.2 transition for MY2017 is the most consequential mid-cycle change in modern 911 history. Engine architecture changed from naturally aspirated (3.4 / 3.8 / 4.0) to twin-turbocharged (3.0 / 4.0 NA only on GT3/RS/R) on every Carrera-trim car. Front fascias were redesigned with horizontal lower air intakes; rear fascias gained a thin-strip horizontal connecting brake light element; the four-point-LED brake light pattern in the rear lights was new. PCM infotainment moved to a new touchscreen-driven generation. Physical body shell carries over with minor revisions; visual identification is most reliable from the rear lights (four-LED brake light = 991.2; ring brake light = 991.1) and the rear fascia (twin centered exhaust outlets vs split outlets, varies by trim).

For valuation purposes the 991.1/991.2 split is material across every Carrera trim because of the engine change. The 991.1 base Carrera is the last 911 with a naturally aspirated 3.4 flat-six in any trim. The 991.1 Carrera S/4S/GTS family is the last 911 with a naturally aspirated 3.8 flat-six in any base/sport trim. From MY2017 onward, only the GT3 / GT3 RS / R / Speedster carry NA engines in the 991 generation. This is widely treated by collectors and the enthusiast press as the last NA-base-Carrera era, and 991.1 GTS values in particular have held strong against later 991.2 GTS cars partly on this basis.


## 911 (991.2, MY2017–MY2019 portion)

### Generation Overview

The 991.2 launched at the 2015 Frankfurt Motor Show as a MY2017 model and entered production in late 2015 / early 2016. Only the MY2017–MY2019 portion of the 991.2 production span is in scope for this file; production continued into MY2020 with final 991-platform cars (the 991.2 Speedster) leaving the line on 20 December 2019. The 992 generation launched MY2020 in parallel.

The defining technical fact of the 991.2 is the introduction of turbocharging on every base Carrera trim. The new 3.0L 9A2 twin-turbo flat-six replaced the 3.4L NA on the Carrera/Carrera 4/Targa 4 (370 hp) and the 3.8L NA on the Carrera S/4S/GTS/Targa 4S/Targa 4 GTS (420 hp / 450 hp on GTS trims). This is the first turbocharged base 911 since the 930 Turbo ended production for MY1989, ending a 28-year run of naturally aspirated base Carreras. The Turbo and Turbo S retained the 3.8L 9A1-derived twin-turbo architecture from the 991.1 with revised internals raising output to 540 / 580 hp.

For the GT cars, the 991.2 GT3 (MY2018) and GT3 RS (MY2019) used a new 4.0L NA flat-six derived from the 991 RSR motorsport program — a different engine from the 991.1 GT3 RS 4.0 unit and revving to 9,000 rpm. The 991.2 GT3 restored the six-speed manual transmission as a no-cost option alongside PDK; the GT3 Touring (MY2018) extended this manual format to a no-cost option that deleted the fixed rear wing (using the deployable Carrera spoiler instead) and added subtle Touring-specific exterior trim. The 991.2 GT2 RS (MY2018) used a 3.8L 9A1-derived twin-turbo flat-six developing 700 hp — the most powerful production 911 ever produced at the time of its launch.

Chassis code: 991 (phase 2, internally 991 II). Body construction: aluminum-steel hybrid monocoque (carryover with minor revisions). Wheelbase: 96.5 in (2,450 mm).

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 991.2

The 991.2 GT3 (MY2018) is the response to the 991.1 GT3's PDK-only controversy. The 4.0L NA flat-six, derived from the 991 RSR motorsport engine, is fundamentally different from the 991.1 GT3's 3.8L unit and the 991.1 GT3 RS's 4.0L unit — the 991.2 GT3's engine uses an updated bottom end with revised lubrication and a 9,000 rpm redline. Output is 500 hp at 8,250 rpm. Critically, the six-speed manual returned as a no-cost option — Porsche reported approximately 50/50 take rate between manual and PDK in some markets, with manual exceeding PDK in the US for a period after launch. Rear-axle steering is standard.

The 991.2 GT3 Touring (MY2018) is documented as a no-cost option package on the GT3, not a separate model line in Porsche's order book. The Touring deletes the fixed rear wing in favor of the deployable Carrera-style spoiler, adds a brushed-aluminum trim ring around the side window glass, and is offered only with the six-speed manual gearbox. Mechanically the Touring is identical to a manual-equipped GT3. This format echoes the 911 R (MY2016) but at GT3 pricing rather than R limited-edition pricing, and with PDK-availability removed. Production figures for Touring vs standard GT3 are not separately tracked in Porsche's official production data.

The 991.2 GT3 RS (MY2019) is the final GT3 RS of the 991 generation. The 4.0L NA flat-six is rated 520 hp (a 20 hp increase over the 991.1 GT3 RS) with a 9,000 rpm redline. NACA ducts feed brake cooling on the front fascia; the rear wing carries over from the GT2 RS. The optional Weissach Package adds magnesium BBS wheels (saving 11.5 kg unsprung), additional carbon-fiber body and interior panels, and stiffer suspension calibration — Porsche has not publicly broken out Weissach take rate. The 991.2 GT3 RS lapped the Nürburgring Nordschleife in 6:56.4 in factory testing. PDK is the only transmission offered.

The 991.2 GT2 RS (MY2018–MY2019) is the most powerful production Porsche 911 ever produced at its launch. The 3.8L 9A1-derived twin-turbo flat-six produces 700 PS / 750 N·m. Rear-wheel drive, PDK only, with a fixed carbon-fiber rear wing and extensive carbon-fiber body panels. The optional Weissach Package (US MSRP +$31,000) adds carbon-fiber anti-roll bars and coupling rods, magnesium wheels, titanium roll cage, and reduces curb weight by approximately 30 kg. Production of the GT2 RS is the most-disputed production figure in modern Porsche specials: Porsche's initial communications and many secondary sources cite "approximately 1,000 worldwide"; some registry-derived figures land at 1,300–1,580 worldwide (Exotic Car Hacks: 1,580); and one rennlist registry tally lists 3,751 worldwide (likely conflating VIN-allocated chassis with delivered cars, or summing across multiple production batches). The most commonly cited values in collector and dealer materials are 1,000 and 1,580 — the higher figure reflecting Porsche's reported decision to extend production into MY2019 in response to demand. North American allocation is documented at approximately 1,000–1,315 cars depending on source; this file records the range and flags the conflict in the Conflicts Log.

The 991.2 Turbo S Exclusive Series (MY2018) is the most extensively-documented Manufaktur showcase of the 991 generation. Production is hard-capped at 500 Coupes worldwide plus a separate 200-unit Cabriolet allocation that was sold exclusively into the North American market. The Coupe and Cabriolet figures are tracked separately by Porsche; combined Exclusive Series production is therefore 700 cars worldwide, of which 318 went to NA per the GT3 RS Registry combined figure. The Coupe was offered in Golden Yellow Metallic as the launch color (with carbon-weave dark stripes); other Manufaktur paint options were available by request. Power is 607 hp via a power kit on the standard Turbo S engine; CFRP body parts include the front lid, side skirts, mirror housings, side air intakes, and roof; carbon-braided composite wheels were offered as an option (the world's largest braiding machine, ~9 m diameter, was used to manufacture the rim sections). Each car carries a numbered Manufaktur plaque on both front fenders and was offered with a matching limited-production Porsche Design chronograph (also limited to 500 to match the Coupe production count).

The 991.2 Speedster (MY2019) is the final 991-platform car. Production is hard-capped at 1,948 units worldwide, matching the year of Porsche's first vehicle (the 1948 Type 356 "No. 1"). The car uses the 991.2 GT3 chassis and drivetrain, the body shell of a Carrera 4 Cabriolet with extensive carbon-fiber panels (front fenders, hood, deck), the GT3-derived 4.0L NA flat-six rated 502 hp at 8,400 rpm, and a six-speed manual gearbox that is approximately 4 kg lighter than the standard 7-speed manual. Top is manually folding. Production began in mid-2019 and concluded on 20 December 2019, when the final 991-generation 911 (a Heritage Design Package Speedster, VIN 1948/1948) left the assembly line at Zuffenhausen. North American allocation is approximately 717 cars per registry data. The Heritage Design Package (a no-cost color and trim option) features two-tone GT Silver Metallic and white paint, race-inspired door numbers and decals, cognac-leather interior, satin-platinum 20-inch wheels, and a matching limited-edition Porsche Design chronograph.

The 991.2 Carrera T (MY2018) revives a nameplate that originated on the 1968 911 T — a lightweight, value-positioned base Carrera variant. The modern Carrera T uses the 3.0L twin-turbo flat-six (370 hp), the same as the base Carrera, but adds standard Sport Chrono Package, a mechanical limited-slip differential, PASM Sport suspension (lowered 20 mm), shortened gear lever throw, lightweight glass and reduced sound insulation, and rear-seat delete as a no-cost option. The T is offered as Coupe only (no Cabriolet) with manual or PDK transmission. It is positioned as an enthusiast-oriented base Carrera, sitting between the standard Carrera and the Carrera S in price.

### Sub-Generation Splits — 991.2 launch and continuation

The 991.2 launch was staggered across MY2017 (base Carreras and Carrera S coupé/cabriolet, October 2015 production start) through MY2018 (Carrera T, GT3, GT3 Touring, Turbo S Exclusive Series, GT2 RS) into MY2019 (GT3 RS, Speedster). For valuation purposes, the entire 991.2 production span continues into MY2020 final builds (early 2020) before the 992 takes over; the 991.2 portion in scope for this file ends at MY2019 production, with the December 2019 final-Speedster build closing the generation.


## Boxster / Cayman (987.2 carryover, 2010–2012)

### Generation Overview

The 987.2 was the second-half facelift of the 987-generation Boxster and Cayman. It launched MY2009 with new 9A1 direct-injection flat-six engines (2.9L base, 3.4L S) and the optional 7-speed PDK transmission alongside a six-speed manual. Only the 2010–2012 portion is in scope for this file; MY2009 is in the 2000s file.

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 987.2 Carryover

The 987.2 Boxster Spyder is the most documented enthusiast trim of the 987.2 carryover years. It was Porsche's lightest production car at the time of launch (curb weight 1,275 kg / 2,811 lb in DIN-spec configuration), achieved via aluminum doors and rear deck (saving ~15 kg combined), a manually folding canvas roof in place of the powered Boxster cabriolet top (saving 21 kg), and a no-air-conditioning baseline (with AC available as a 12 kg option). The Spyder uses the same 3.4L 9A1 NA flat-six as the Boxster S but tuned to 320 hp via revised ECU mapping and a 20 mm lower sport suspension. Aluminum doors continued into the 981 generation but were unique to the Spyder within the 987 lineup. The Spyder was offered with both six-speed manual and 7-speed PDK transmissions; PDK take rate was reportedly higher than enthusiasts later remember. The Porsche Historisches Archiv (Porsche's official corporate archive) reports total Spyder production of 33 cars built in calendar 2009 (early MY2010 builds), 1,418 in 2010, and 493 in 2011 — totaling approximately 1,944 cars. Note this conflicts with the figure cited in some valuation guides (~3,000 units) — the lower archive-derived figure is more authoritative. Approximately 890 cars were US-allocation per registry research; 237 were UK RHD per Porsche Cars GB.

The 987.2 Cayman R (MY2012) is the corresponding lightweight Cayman variant. It uses the 3.4L 9A1 NA at 330 hp with a 20 mm lower sport suspension, mechanical limited-slip differential, aluminum doors and decklid, lightweight 19-inch alloys, fabric door pulls, and an optional carbon bucket-seat package. Production was a single model year and total figures are not publicly documented at unit precision; secondary sources estimate low single-thousand units worldwide.

The 987.2 Black Edition (Boxster S Black Edition for MY2011, Cayman S Black Edition for MY2012) are last-of-generation appearance packages with modest power increases. Both add 10 hp via revised ECU mapping versus the standard Boxster S / Cayman S, all-black interior and exterior trim, 19-inch wheels, and unique badging.

## Boxster / Cayman (981, MY2013–MY2016)

### Generation Overview

The 981 launched at the 2012 Geneva Motor Show as a MY2013 model, sharing engineering DNA with the 991.1 (introduced just months earlier). The 981 used the same 9A1-family naturally aspirated flat-six engines as the 987.2 — a 2.7L (replacing 2.9L), and a 3.4L for the S — paired with six-speed manual or 7-speed PDK transmissions. Chassis code: 981. Body construction: aluminum-steel hybrid monocoque (similar to 991.1). Wheelbase: 97.4 in (2,475 mm), an increase versus the 987.

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 981 Boxster + Cayman

The 981 Cayman GT4 (MY2016) is the first Cayman to wear a GT designation and is the most-documented 981 enthusiast trim. It uses a detuned version of the 991.1 Carrera S 3.8L NA flat-six (385 hp, versus 400 hp in the donor car), six-speed manual transmission only, mechanical limited-slip differential, brake calipers and rotors derived from the 991 GT3, suspension components and damper hardware from the 991 GT3, a fixed rear wing, and a unique aerodynamic front fascia with vented bumpers for additional radiator cooling. Curb weight is 1,340 kg. The Cayman GT4 introduced the Cayman to the Porsche GT car lineage — a deliberate move by GT department head Andreas Preuninger to broaden the GT philosophy beyond the 911. Worldwide production figures are widely disputed: Wikipedia and supercars.net cite approximately 2,500 units; the rennlist registry cites approximately 6,000 cars worldwide (including continuing production into early 2017 in some markets); Road & Track's launch coverage cited a planned US allocation of approximately 1,250 cars; documented Canadian allocation for MY2016 alone was 292 cars. This file records the range and flags the conflict.

The 981 Boxster Spyder (MY2016) is the parallel halo for the open-top side. It uses the same 3.8L 9A1 NA at slightly lower output (375 hp), six-speed manual only, the GT4's front and rear fascias and optional bucket seats, a manually folding canvas top, and a steering rack ratio shared with the 991 Turbo S — the only 981 to use this faster ratio. Aluminum doors and decklid, lightweight 20-inch wheels, and a 20 mm lower sport suspension complete the lightweight philosophy. Worldwide production is approximately 2,400 cars per registry research; US allocation is approximately 850 cars; UK allocation is documented at 227 cars per the Porsche Club GB registry, with the most common color being Carrara White (78), then GT Silver Metallic (62), then Guards Red (27).

The 981 Cayman GT4 Clubsport is a track-only racing variant of the GT4 produced through Porsche Motorsport at Weissach. It uses the same 3.8L NA at 385 hp paired with a 7-speed PDK transmission (with 7th gear disabled in race configuration), mechanical rear locking differential, and Performance Friction brakes shared with the 991 GT3 Cup. Curb weight is 1,300 kg. The Clubsport is not road-legal in any market and is excluded from the road-car valuation scope of this file; it is recorded here as a cross-reference because some retail listings incorrectly conflate Clubsport and road GT4 cars.

### Sub-Generation Splits — 981 vs 718

The 981 → 718 transition for MY2017 paralleled the 991.1 → 991.2 transition timing-wise but had a more dramatic powertrain change. The 718 Boxster and Cayman replaced the 9A1 naturally aspirated flat-six (2.7L base, 3.4L S, 3.4L GTS) with a 9A2 turbocharged flat-four (2.0L on Boxster/Cayman, 2.5L on Boxster S/Cayman S/Boxster GTS/Cayman GTS). This was the first time since the 968 (1992–1995) that Porsche's mid-engine sports cars used a flat-four engine, and it was widely reported as poorly received in the enthusiast market. Multiple period reviews (Top Gear, Motor Trend, Car and Driver) noted that the 718 was mechanically faster than the 981 it replaced — quicker 0-60, better handling balance, sharper steering — but emotionally less compelling, with the flat-four exhaust note frequently described as the principal sonic disappointment. Used 981 GTS, S, and Spyder/GT4 prices held strong against new 718 prices through the back half of the decade. The MY2020 718 Cayman GT4 (with the 4.0L NA flat-six derived from the 991.2 GT3 base engine) eventually returned the natural-aspiration formula to the Cayman, but only on the GT4 — base/S/GTS trims continued with the flat-four turbo — and that car is in scope for the 2020s file.

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 718

The 718 base/S engines are 2.0L (300 hp) and 2.5L (350 hp on S, 365 hp on GTS) flat-four turbocharged units. ## Cayenne (958, MY2011–MY2018)

### Generation Overview

The 958 launched MY2011 as Porsche's second-generation Cayenne, on a new platform shared with the Volkswagen Touareg and Audi Q7. Compared with the 957 it was approximately 180 kg lighter despite being slightly larger (1.9 in / 4.8 cm longer, 1.6 in / 4 cm longer wheelbase). Critically, the 958 deleted the 957's low-range transfer case — a significant downgrade for serious off-road usage but a major contributor to the weight reduction. The 6-speed Tiptronic was replaced with an 8-speed Tiptronic; manual transmission was offered only on the base V6 trim.

The 958 production span is divided into 958.1 (MY2011–MY2014) and 958.2 (MY2015–MY2018), with the facelift introduced in summer 2014 and bringing significant powertrain changes — most notably, the 4.8L V8 was retired from the Cayenne S and GTS in favor of a new 3.6L twin-turbocharged V6 (derived from the Cayenne Turbo's 4.8L TT V8 with two cylinders removed). The Turbo and Turbo S retained the 4.8L TT V8 throughout. The first-generation Cayenne S Hybrid (parallel hybrid, non-plug-in) was launched at the start of the 958 generation in MY2011; the Cayenne S E-Hybrid (plug-in hybrid) launched with the 958.2 facelift in MY2015. The Cayenne Diesel was offered worldwide from MY2011 through MY2017–MY2018 in Europe, with a US-market run from MY2013 through November 2015 (when sales were halted following an EPA Notice of Violation; see dieselgate notes below).

Chassis codes: 92A (internal). Body construction: steel monocoque with aluminum panels. Wheelbase: 113.9 in (2,895 mm).

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 958

The 958.1 GTS (MY2013–MY2014) is the most enthusiast-favored variant of the entire 958 generation. It uses an uprated version of the 4.8L NA V8 (420 hp, +20 over the Cayenne S), Sport-tuned suspension with a 24 mm lower ride height (steel springs) or 20 mm lower (air suspension), sport exhaust, modified DME mapping, and a unique Carmine Red or Peridot Green metallic paint option with optional Alcantara interior. It is widely treated as the last NA-V8 Cayenne available with the GTS trim's emotional character. Following the 958.2 facelift, the GTS moved to the 3.6L twin-turbo V6, gaining torque and efficiency but losing the V8 sonic character.

The Cayenne Diesel's involvement in the Volkswagen Group dieselgate scandal is a documented event that affects collector valuations on US-market cars. On 2 November 2015, the US Environmental Protection Agency issued a second Notice of Violation extending the September 2015 NOV (originally targeting four-cylinder VW/Audi diesels) to include the 3.0L V6 TDI engine used in the 2014–2016 Porsche Cayenne Diesel and several Audi/VW Touareg models. Porsche Cars North America issued a stop-sale order on the Cayenne Diesel on 3 November 2015, halting US sales of the variant for the remainder of MY2016 and beyond. The recall affected approximately 11,500 US-market Cayenne Diesels (model years 2009–2015 per Kelley Blue Book reporting; MY2013–MY2015 per most other sources), plus approximately 21,500 EU-market cars from MY2014–MY2017 that received a software update under agreement with the German Federal Motor Transport Authority (KBA). Porsche did not return the Cayenne Diesel to the US market; the Cayenne Diesel continued in Europe through approximately MY2017 before quietly being phased out, with Porsche announcing in September 2018 that the company would no longer offer diesel propulsion in any future products. For valuation purposes, US-market Cayenne Diesels carry a documented stop-sale event in their service history and have a truncated production-and-delivery window relative to the European version of the same car.

The 958 Cayenne S Hybrid (MY2011–MY2014) and Cayenne S E-Hybrid (MY2015–MY2018) are different cars with different drivetrain architectures despite similar names. The S Hybrid (MY2011–MY2014) is a parallel hybrid using a 3.0L supercharged V6 (Audi-sourced) plus a 47 hp electric motor with a 1.7 kWh nickel-metal-hydride battery; total system output is 380 hp combined and the car cannot be plugged in. The S E-Hybrid (MY2015–MY2018) is a plug-in hybrid using the same 3.0L supercharged V6 plus a 95 hp (twice as powerful) electric motor with a 9.4 kWh lithium-ion battery (5.5x the capacity); total system output is 416 hp combined and the car offers an EPA-rated electric-only driving range of approximately 14 miles. The S E-Hybrid is the first plug-in hybrid in the premium SUV segment globally, predating analogous plug-in offerings from Range Rover, BMW, and Mercedes-Benz.

The 958.2 Turbo S (MY2015–MY2018) is the performance halo of the generation. The 4.8L twin-turbo V8 produces 570 hp, and the car was capable of 0-100 km/h in 4.1 seconds (0-60 mph in approximately 3.8 seconds with launch control). Equipped with Michelin Pilot Sport Cup 2 tires, a 958.2 Turbo S lapped the Nürburgring Nordschleife in 7:59.74 — the fastest production-SUV time on the circuit at the time. The Turbo S included PCCB (with 420 mm front discs and 10-piston calipers, the largest production-car ceramic brakes Porsche had offered to date), PDCC, PTV+, Sport Chrono Package, 21-inch wheels, and a carbon interior package as standard.

### Sub-Generation Splits — 958.1 vs 958.2

The 958.1 → 958.2 facelift in summer 2014 was the most consequential mid-cycle change in the Cayenne's first two generations combined. Engine changes: the Cayenne S and GTS dropped the 4.8L NA V8 in favor of the new 3.6L twin-turbo V6 (Cayenne S: 420 hp; Cayenne GTS: 440 hp). The Turbo and Turbo S retained the 4.8L TT V8 with revised tuning. The hybrid moved from non-plug-in (S Hybrid) to plug-in (S E-Hybrid), with the lithium-ion battery introduced as part of the facelift. Exterior changes included revised front fascia with re-shaped LED daytime running lights, new tail lights with horizontal layout, and updated wheel designs. The 958.2 also brought a revised steering wheel design and updated PCM infotainment.

Mechanically the 958.2 deleted permanent four-wheel drive in favor of an on-demand AWD system that sends torque to the front wheels only when the rear wheels lose grip — a 911-style AWD philosophy applied to the Cayenne for the first time. From 2015, final Cayenne assembly was performed at both Porsche's Leipzig factory and Volkswagen's Osnabrück factory (formerly Karmann); pre-production assembly continued at VW Bratislava, Slovakia. The 11th VIN character indicates final-assembly location: "L" for Leipzig, "K" for Osnabrück.

For valuation purposes, the 958.1 GTS (NA V8) and 958.2 GTS (TT V6) are distinctly different cars, with 958.1 GTS values holding stronger as enthusiasts favor the V8 character. The 958.2 Turbo S is the performance flagship and commands the highest 958-generation valuations alongside the 958.1 Turbo S. Cayenne Diesel valuations on the US market are depressed relative to gasoline equivalents due to the dieselgate stop-sale event and accompanying buyback programs in some cases; European Cayenne Diesel values held closer to gasoline equivalents post-recall.

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 970

The 970 Panamera S Hybrid (MY2012–MY2013) is Porsche's first production hybrid road car. It is a parallel hybrid using the same drivetrain as the Cayenne S Hybrid: a 3.0L Audi-sourced supercharged V6 (333 hp) plus a 47 hp electric motor coupled through the 8-speed Tiptronic transmission. The car can drive on electric power alone up to approximately 46 mph (74 km/h) and was rated at approximately 33 mpg combined — at launch the most fuel-efficient car Porsche had ever sold. Total system output is 380 hp combined; 0-62 mph in 6.0 seconds. The hybrid was rear-wheel drive only.

The 970 Panamera S E-Hybrid (MY2014–MY2016) is Porsche's first production plug-in hybrid road car. It uses the same 3.0L supercharged V6 paired with a more powerful 95 hp electric motor (twice the power of the S Hybrid's motor) and a 9.4 kWh lithium-ion battery (5.5x the capacity of the S Hybrid's nickel-metal-hydride pack). Total system output is 416 hp combined; EPA-rated electric-only range is approximately 16 miles, with top electric-only speed of approximately 84 mph (135 km/h). 0-62 mph is 5.5 seconds. Like the S Hybrid, the E-Hybrid is rear-wheel drive only.

The 970.1 Panamera S, 4S, GTS, Turbo, and Turbo S all use the 4.8L Porsche V8 — naturally aspirated on S and 4S (400 hp), tuned higher on the GTS (430 hp), twin-turbocharged on the Turbo (500 hp) and Turbo S (550 hp). With the 970.2 facelift in MY2014, the S and 4S moved to a new 3.0L Porsche-developed twin-turbo V6 (a downsized derivative of the 3.6L Cayenne VR6 architecture), while the GTS retained the 4.8L NA V8 at 440 hp through the 970.2's full run — making the 970.2 GTS the last NA-V8 Panamera ever produced (the 971 GTS uses a twin-turbo V8). The Turbo and Turbo S retained the 4.8L TT V8 at 520 / 570 hp through the 970.2.

The Panamera 4S Executive, Turbo Executive, and Turbo S Executive (970.2 facelift) introduced long-wheelbase variants with a 150 mm stretch focused on rear-passenger room. These were primarily intended for the Chinese market but were available in select left-hand-drive markets globally; right-hand-drive Executive cars were not produced. A specific Panamera Exclusive Series (2014, 100 units worldwide) was an LWB Manufaktur Manufaktur configuration with a two-tone fading paint scheme, custom seat embroidery, and pricing approximately five times that of a base Boxster — making it among the most expensive Panameras of the 970 generation.

### Sub-Generation Splits — 970.1 vs 970.2

The 970.1 → 970.2 transition for MY2014 brought engine changes (4.8L V8 NA replaced by 3.0L V6 TT in the S and 4S; GTS retained 4.8L V8 NA), the introduction of the plug-in S E-Hybrid (replacing the parallel S Hybrid), the addition of the long-wheelbase Executive trims, and visual updates to front and rear bumpers. PCM was updated; LED headlights became more widely available. For valuation purposes the 970.2 facelift cars are generally favored over 970.1 — a relatively unusual situation in Porsche generations, where the 970.1 has been documented in enthusiast media as having more first-cycle quality issues and the 970.2 having matured powertrain options. The 970.2 GTS's continuation of the NA V8 makes it the most enthusiast-favored 970.2 variant from a sound-character perspective.

The 971 introduced the Sport Turismo wagon body style for MY2018, which broadened the lineup with a five-door shooting-brake variant alongside the standard liftback. ### Variant Differentiation Notes — 971

The 971 Panamera Sport Turismo (introduced MY2018) is the first Porsche production wagon body style. It uses the same MSB platform as the standard liftback Panamera but with a vertical rear hatch, a slightly extended roofline, and a 4+1 seating configuration (the rear bench includes a center seat that is more usable than the liftback's, though still tight). ### Sub-Generation Splits — 970 vs 971

The 970 → 971 generational transition for MY2017 was a complete platform replacement. New MSB architecture, all-new exterior and interior design (with substantially better-received aesthetics per period reviews), new family of twin-turbo V6 and V8 engines (2.9L V6 TT, 3.0L V6 TT, 4.0L V8 TT replacing the 970's 3.6 NA, 4.8 NA, 4.8 TT), new 8-speed PDK replacing the 7-speed PDK and 8-speed Tiptronic in their respective trims, addition of the Sport Turismo wagon body style for MY2018, and revised plug-in hybrid architecture (the 971 Turbo S E-Hybrid V8-plus-electric was a new layout entirely, not derived from the 970 S E-Hybrid V6 supercharged drivetrain). For valuation purposes the 970 and 971 are largely treated as separate model lineages; cross-shopping between them is unusual given the substantial design and powertrain differences.

## Macan (95B, MY2015–MY2019 portion)

### Generation Overview

The 95B Macan launched globally in spring 2014 (MY2015) on the MLB platform shared with the first-generation Audi Q5 (2008–2017). Porsche made substantial modifications to the platform: the Macan is 1.7 inches longer and 1.4 inches wider than the Q5; the suspension geometry is heavily reworked; the engine, transfer case, suspension tuning, interior, and body panels are unique to the Macan. The Macan Turbo with Performance Package (440 hp, MY2017) is documented as a distinct trim from the standard Macan Turbo. A 95B mid-cycle update (sometimes referred to as 95B.2 internally) launched MY2019 with revised front and rear styling, new LED head and tail lights, and a redesigned interior with the new 10.9-inch PCM touchscreen; only MY2015–MY2019 portion is in scope for this file (MY2020+ continuation is in 2020s file).

The Macan became Porsche's best-selling model globally from 2015 onward (its first full year on sale). By July 2018, more than 350,000 Macans had been delivered worldwide cumulatively, with over 100,000 of those in China alone.

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 95B Macan

The 95B Macan launched as a mid-decade business inflection point for Porsche. Within its first full year on sale (2015), it became Porsche's best-selling model globally; by mid-decade the Macan and Cayenne combined were responsible for the majority of Porsche's annual unit volume. The platform-sharing relationship with the Audi Q5 has been documented as deliberate cost-engineering at the platform level, with Porsche-unique tuning at the chassis, body, interior, and powertrain levels. Production at Leipzig has run continuously since 2014, with capacity expansions during the decade.

The Macan Turbo with Performance Package (added MY2017) is documented in Porsche's order book as a distinct trim rather than a simple option box on the standard Turbo. It includes the uprated 440 hp 3.6L TT V6, Sport Chrono Package as standard, an Alcantara headliner, and PCCB as an extra-cost option; PCCB was not available on the standard Turbo. Pricing reflects the trim differentiation: at launch the Performance Package was approximately $14,000 over the standard Macan Turbo MSRP.

The Macan S Diesel was withdrawn from sales gradually following parallel emissions concerns in the wider VW Group dieselgate aftermath. In Germany alone, Porsche recalled approximately 30,000 Macan Diesels for emissions-related software updates in 2018. The Macan Diesel was never US-federalized for sale and remains out of scope for US-market valuations.

### Sub-Generation Splits — 95B Pre-LCI vs LCI (MY2019)

The 95B mid-cycle update launched MY2019 with revised front and rear fascias (sharper LED headlight signature, full-width LED tail-light bar), a new interior with the 10.9-inch PCM touchscreen replacing the earlier 7-inch unit, a redesigned center console, and minor powertrain updates including a gasoline particulate filter as standard on petrol variants. The MY2019 also expanded color and wheel design options. Only the MY2019 portion of the LCI is in scope for this file; further updates (95B.2 with the 348 hp 3.0L single-turbo V6 in the Macan S, and other refinements) followed in the 2020s and are out of scope.

## 918 Spyder (MY2014–MY2015)

### Generation Overview

The 918 Spyder is Porsche's hybrid hypercar of the decade and the spiritual successor to the Carrera GT (2003–2006). It was unveiled as a concept at the 2010 Geneva Motor Show, approved for production by the Porsche AG supervisory board on 28 July 2010 (after 2,000 declarations of interest), and entered series production on 18 September 2013. The 918 was sold out by December 2014; production ended in June 2015. All cars were designated 2015 model year cars regardless of production date. Worldwide production was capped at 918 units, matching the model name.

The 918 Spyder is a mid-engine plug-in hybrid two-seat roadster. Its powertrain combines a naturally aspirated 4.6L V8 (engine derived from the Porsche RS Spyder LMP2 race car, rated 608 hp at 8,700 rpm) with two electric motors — a 95 kW front motor driving the front axle (making the 918 all-wheel drive) and a 115 kW rear motor coupled with the engine through the 7-speed PDK transmission. Combined system output is 887 hp / 944 lb-ft. The 6.8 kWh lithium-ion battery is positioned behind the passenger cell. EPA-rated all-electric range is approximately 12 miles in five-cycle testing. 0-60 mph is 2.4 seconds (Porsche-claimed) or 2.2 seconds (Car and Driver tested with rollout); top speed is 214 mph.

In September 2013, a 918 Spyder fitted with the optional Weissach Package set a Nürburgring Nordschleife lap time of 6:57 — the first street-legal production car to break the seven-minute barrier at the time, with the previous production-car record reduced by 14 seconds in a single attempt.

Chassis code: 918. Body construction: carbon-fiber reinforced plastic monocoque with aluminum and steel subframes. Wheelbase: 107.5 in (2,730 mm).

### Variant Differentiation Notes — 918 Spyder

The 918 Spyder Weissach Package is a documented trim-level differentiator within 918 production. The package is named for Porsche's R&D facility in Weissach, Germany, where the car was developed. It costs $84,000 USD on top of the base $845,000 MSRP (totaling approximately $929,000 base US MSRP) and reduces curb weight by approximately 41 kg / 90 lb. Components include: magnesium wheels (replacing the standard forged aluminum wheels), a thin-film vinyl exterior coating in place of paint (offered with optional Martini Racing or Salzburg liveries), additional carbon-fiber body panels (windscreen frame, roof, rear wing, mirrors), titanium chassis bolts, ceramic wheel bearings, lighter brake design, optional six-point racing harnesses (replacing standard seat belts), reduced sound insulation, and additional aerodynamic elements (front splitter and fixed rear wing).

Sources disagree on the Weissach Package take rate. Need for Speed wiki (citing Porsche references) reports approximately 25% of cars carrying the package; Porsche West Palm Beach reports 25–30%; some collector and dealer sources have cited up to 50%. The most defensible mid-range estimate is approximately 30–40% take rate, but production documentation at the unit level is not publicly available. The Weissach Package commands a documented secondary-market premium over base 918 Spyders (often 20–40% on a comparable specification basis), and the package is a material valuation differentiator.

The 918 Spyder is the third Porsche "Spyder" of the 2010s decade, alongside the 987.2 Boxster Spyder (MY2011–MY2012) and the 981 Boxster Spyder (MY2016). All three are mid-engine open-top sports cars but they represent fundamentally different cars at fundamentally different price points: the 987.2 Spyder is a 320 hp, $61,000 lightweight roadster; the 981 Spyder is a 375 hp, $83,000 enthusiast trim of the Boxster; and the 918 Spyder is an 887 hp, $845,000 hybrid hypercar. The 918's spiritual predecessor is the 1953 Type 550 Spyder racing car and, more recently, the 2003–2006 Carrera GT.

## Cross-Generation Notes

### End of the Mezger Engine Era

The Mezger engine — Porsche's purpose-designed flat-six developed for the 1996 GT1 racing program by Hans Mezger and adapted for road use in the 996 GT3, GT2, Turbo, and 997 GT3, GT2, GT3 RS, and Turbo lines — ended production with the 997.2 generation. The final road-car applications were the 997.2 GT3 (3.8L, 2010–2011), GT3 RS (3.8L and 4.0L, 2010–2011), and GT2 RS (3.6L turbo, 2011); the final Turbo application was the 997.2 Turbo / Turbo S (2010–2012). The 991.1 generation transitioned all 911 variants — including GT3, GT3 RS, and Turbo — to derivatives of the 9A1 direct-injection flat-six, ending the dry-sump, pressed-not-bolted-crankshaft Mezger architecture in road production. Mezger-engine cars (especially the 997.2 GT3 RS 4.0, GT2 RS, and 991.1-precluding Turbo S models) command a documented enthusiast premium in the secondary market that has persisted through the decade. The architectural distinction — Mezger versus 9A1 — is a primary valuation lever for late-997 versus early-991 GT and Turbo trims.

### 9A1 Engine Family Expansion

The 9A1 direct-injection flat-six debuted in the 997.2 Carrera (3.6L) and Carrera S (3.8L) for MY2009 (replacing the M96/M97 family) and remained the core 911 architecture through 991.1. The 991.2 generation (MY2017+) introduced the 9A2 turbocharged generation: 3.0L twin-turbo flat-six replacing the 3.4 NA in the base Carrera and the 3.8 NA in the Carrera S, with the same architecture extended to the Carrera 4, GTS, and Targa lines. The naturally aspirated GT3 and GT3 RS continued with bespoke 4.0L flat-sixes derived from but distinct from the 9A1/9A2 family. The 991.2 GT2 RS and Turbo S used the 3.8L turbocharged flat-six derived from the 9A2 family with substantial GT-specific reinforcement (forged crankshaft, larger turbos for GT2 RS).

### 991.2 Turbocharging Transition

The MY2017 991.2 facelift represented the most significant engineering change to the standard 911 line in the 2010s decade. All Carrera, Carrera S, Carrera 4, Carrera 4S, GTS, Targa, and Targa GTS variants moved from naturally aspirated 3.4L / 3.8L flat-sixes to 3.0L twin-turbo flat-sixes, with power increases of 20–30 hp per trim. The transition was enabled by VW Group emissions targets and CO2 fleet averages and was anticipated by industry observers for several years before launch. Period-correct enthusiast reaction was mixed: turbocharged response was acknowledged as quicker, but the loss of the naturally aspirated 9A1 sound signature was a frequent complaint in launch reviews. The naturally aspirated GT3 and GT3 RS (4.0L, MY2018+) became premium-priced enthusiast halos in part because they were the last naturally aspirated 911s available new at scale. The Carrera T (MY2018) was positioned as a manual-transmission, lighter-weight enthusiast trim partly to address criticism that the 991.2 base Carrera had moved upmarket; market reception of the T was strong.

### 718 Naming and Boxster/Cayman Turbocharging Transition

The MY2017 facelift of the 981 Boxster and Cayman became the 718 Boxster and 718 Cayman, a naming change rather than a chassis-code change (the cars remain on the 982/981 platform; "718" is a marketing designation referencing the 1957–1962 Porsche 718 racing car). All base and S trims moved from naturally aspirated flat-sixes (2.7L flat-six in base, 3.4L flat-six in S) to turbocharged flat-fours (2.0L in base, 2.5L in S). The four-cylinder turbo decision was widely criticized in enthusiast press and on launch reviews, primarily for sound character and the loss of the 981's naturally aspirated flat-six signature. Sales of the 718 Boxster and Cayman declined relative to the 981 in several markets, contributing to Porsche's later reintroduction of a flat-six in the 718 GTS 4.0 (MY2020+, out of scope) and the GT4 / Spyder (MY2020+, out of scope). For MY2017–MY2019 (in scope), only the four-cylinder turbo trims were produced.

### Hybrid Program Launch and Architecture Evolution

The 2010s saw Porsche's first three production hybrid road cars launch in sequence: the 957 Cayenne S Hybrid (parallel hybrid, MY2010), the 970 Panamera S Hybrid (parallel hybrid, MY2011, on the same VW Group hybrid module), the 958.1 Cayenne S Hybrid (parallel hybrid, MY2011, replacing the 957 Hybrid), and then the architecturally distinct plug-in hybrids: 918 Spyder (mid-engine plug-in hybrid hypercar, MY2014–MY2015), 970 Panamera S E-Hybrid (plug-in hybrid liftback, MY2014, replacing the parallel S Hybrid), 958.2 Cayenne S E-Hybrid (plug-in hybrid SUV, MY2015), and the 971 Panamera 4 E-Hybrid and Turbo S E-Hybrid (plug-in hybrid liftbacks, MY2018, with the Turbo S E-Hybrid being Porsche's first plug-in V8). For valuation purposes the parallel hybrids (Cayenne S Hybrid, Panamera S Hybrid) and the plug-in hybrids (S E-Hybrid, Turbo S E-Hybrid) are distinct trims with distinct architectures; period documentation often conflates them. The 918 Spyder is the technological flagship of the program but its hybrid system architecture (front-axle motor for AWD, rear motor coupled to the engine through PDK) was bespoke and not directly carried over to the volume Cayenne or Panamera plug-ins.

### 918 Weissach Package as Documented Trim Modifier

The 918 Spyder Weissach Package is one of the few examples in the 2010s Porsche lineup where an option package is documented as a distinct trim level for valuation purposes. The package includes magnesium wheels, additional exposed carbon body panels, the optional Martini Racing or Salzburg liveries (in vinyl rather than paint), six-point harness mounts, ceramic bearings, titanium chassis bolts, and additional aerodynamic elements, totaling approximately 41 kg / 90 lb of weight reduction. The Nürburgring 6:57 lap was set on a Weissach-equipped car. Take-rate estimates range from 25% to 50% across published sources, with no authoritative published unit count from Porsche; secondary-market premiums for verified Weissach cars are documented at 20–40% over base spec on comparable mileage and condition. The Weissach Package distinction is the primary trim-level valuation lever within 918 Spyder production.

### 991 GT-Car Allocation Politics

The 991 generation introduced an enthusiast-allocation politics dynamic that became a defining feature of the 911 GT-car secondary market in the 2010s. Each successive halo trim — 991 50th Anniversary (1,963 ww, 2013), 991.1 GT3 RS (~4,250 ww, 2015–2016), 991 R (991 ww, 2016), 991.2 GT3 (~9,520 ww, 2017–2019), 991.2 GT3 Touring (no fixed rear wing, ~3,500–4,000 ww estimated, 2018–2019), 991.2 GT2 RS (range 1,000–1,580 ww depending on source, 2018–2019), 991.2 GT3 RS (~4,750 ww, 2018–2019), and 991.2 Speedster (1,948 ww, 2019) — was sold via dealer-allocation processes that prioritized customers with prior GT-car ownership. Secondary-market markups at launch were significant: 991 R cars sold new at $185,950 USD MSRP routinely traded above $400,000 USD in 2016–2017. Porsche responded over the decade with allocation-rules adjustments (formal "buyer's letter" requirements committing to retain cars for one year, expansion of total production volumes for halo trims, the 991.2 Speedster offered to qualifying customers more broadly than the 911 R was). For valuation purposes, GT-car ownership history, original allocation documentation, and original delivery dealer are material provenance factors.

### Macan as Cayenne Volume Successor

The 95B Macan (MY2015 launch) became Porsche's volume sales leader within its first full year on sale and held that position for the remainder of the decade. Cumulative deliveries crossed 350,000 units worldwide by July 2018 (Porsche-stated). The Macan's sales success enabled Porsche's R&D investment in the 918 Spyder, the 991.2 GT-car program, the 971 Panamera lineup expansion (including Sport Turismo), and the Taycan EV program (out of scope). For valuation purposes the Macan is treated as a Porsche product at a Porsche price point with Porsche performance, but secondary-market depreciation curves on Macan trims (especially base 2.0L MY2017+) follow more conventional luxury-SUV depreciation patterns than the 911 lineup.

### 970 → 971 Panamera Transition

The MY2017 Panamera 970 → 971 transition was a complete platform replacement: new MSB platform shared with the second-generation Bentley Continental GT and the Audi A8 (D5), all-new exterior design (significantly better received than the 970's rear-end styling), new family of twin-turbo V6 and V8 engines (2.9L V6 TT, 3.0L V6 TT, 4.0L V8 TT replacing the 970's 3.6 NA, 4.8 NA, 4.8 TT, and 3.0L supercharged V6 hybrid), new 8-speed PDK replacing both the 7-speed PDK (in V8 trims) and the 8-speed Tiptronic (in V6 trims) of the 970, and the addition of the Sport Turismo wagon body style for MY2018. The 971 Turbo S E-Hybrid (MY2018) introduced the plug-in V8 layout that informed Porsche's later 971.2 facelift (out of scope) and the third-generation Panamera (972, out of scope). For valuation purposes the 970 and 971 are largely treated as separate model lineages.

### 991 Limited-Edition Cadence

The 991 generation produced an unusually dense cadence of documented limited-production celebratory and special editions across the decade: 991 50th Anniversary Edition (1,963 ww, 2013), 991 Carrera S Club Coupe (13 ww, 2012, PCA 60th anniversary), 991 GTS Club Coupe (60 ww, 2016, PCA 60th anniversary 911), 991 GTS Rennsport Reunion Edition (25 ww, 2016, North America only), 991 R (991 ww, 2016), 991.2 Turbo S Exclusive Series Coupe (500 ww, 2018) and Cabriolet (200 ww, 2018, North America only), and 991.2 Speedster (1,948 ww, 2019). The smaller-volume editions (Club Coupes, Rennsport Reunion, Turbo S Exclusive Series Cabriolet) carry significant secondary-market premiums and are differentiated by VIN range, COA documentation, and Porsche Classic registry verification. The marketing-number production caps (991 R = 991 units matching 991 generation; 991.2 Speedster = 1,948 units honoring the 1948 founding date; 918 Spyder = 918 units; 50th Anniversary = 1,963 units honoring the year of the original 911 introduction) are a deliberate Porsche tradition extending back to the 1989 911 Speedster (no fixed cap but heritage-themed) and the 1993 911 30 Jahre 911 (911 units).

### 9Y0 Cayenne Launch (MY2019 portion in scope)

The third-generation 9Y0 Cayenne launched globally for MY2019 on the MLB Evo platform shared with the Audi Q7/Q8, Bentley Bentayga, and Lamborghini Urus. The 9Y0 is approximately 65 kg lighter than the 958 (despite size and equipment increases), introduces 48-volt mild-hybrid architecture across most trims, and brings the 12.3-inch PCM touchscreen and digital instrument cluster to the Cayenne for the first time. MY2019 launch trims include the Cayenne (3.0L V6 TT, 335 hp), Cayenne S (2.9L V6 TT, 434 hp), and Cayenne Turbo (4.0L V8 TT, 541 hp). The plug-in hybrid Cayenne E-Hybrid (3.0L V6 TT + electric, 455 hp combined) was added for MY2019 in some markets. Only the MY2019 portion is in scope for this file; further trim additions (Cayenne Coupe, Cayenne Turbo S E-Hybrid, Turbo GT) followed in the 2020s and are out of scope.

### Manual Transmission Politics

The 991 generation arrived in 2011 with a 7-speed manual transmission as the standard transmission on all Carrera, Carrera S, Carrera 4, Carrera 4S, and GTS trims (PDK optional). By the 991.2 facelift (MY2017), market take-rate on manuals had declined to single-digit percentages globally, and Porsche moved PDK to standard equipment with the 7-speed manual no-cost optional on most non-GT trims. The 991.1 GT3 (2014) launched as PDK-only — the first GT3 ever offered without a manual transmission, generating significant enthusiast backlash. The 991 R (2016) reintroduced a 6-speed manual on a GT3 RS-derived chassis and was widely interpreted as Porsche's response to the manual-transmission backlash; it was followed by the 991.2 GT3 (MY2018) being available with a 6-speed manual at no cost, the GT3 Touring (MY2018, manual only with no fixed rear wing), and the 991.2 Speedster (MY2019, 6-speed manual only). For valuation purposes the manual versus PDK transmission election is a primary trim-level differentiator on 991 GT trims; manual-equipped 991.1 R and 991.2 Speedster cars are factory-specified manuals, while 991.2 GT3 and GT3 Touring transmission election varied by buyer.

### Carrera T Revival

The 991.2 Carrera T (MY2018–MY2019) revived the "T" (Touring) badge from the 1968 911 T as a lighter-weight, manual-focused enthusiast trim positioned below the GTS and GT3. The Carrera T uses the base Carrera 3.0L twin-turbo flat-six (370 hp) with the GTS-trim's lower ride height, sport exhaust, mechanical limited-slip differential, lighter glass, fabric door pulls, and standard 7-speed manual (PDK no-cost optional). The trim was positioned as a "purist" alternative to the increasingly equipment-heavy mainstream Carrera and was well received in launch reviews. Production volumes are not publicly broken out from the broader 991.2 Carrera production; secondary-market premiums for verified Carrera T cars (especially manual-equipped) are documented but modest relative to GT-car premiums.

### Cayenne Diesel and Macan Diesel Aftermath

The November 2015 EPA Notice of Violation alleging defeat-device software on Cayenne Diesels (MY2014–MY2016 in the US) and the broader VW Group dieselgate aftermath produced specific Porsche-relevant valuation artifacts. The US sale of Cayenne Diesels was halted in November 2015 and never resumed; Porsche eventually agreed to repurchase or modify approximately 11,500 affected US Cayenne Diesel vehicles. In the EU, approximately 21,500 Cayenne Diesels were recalled for emissions-related software updates, and the Macan Diesel (3.0L V6 TDI, EU only, never US-federalized) was withdrawn from sales gradually with approximately 30,000 cars recalled in Germany alone in 2018. Porsche AG paid a €535 million administrative fine to German authorities in 2018 over diesel-related compliance issues. For valuation purposes, the US Cayenne Diesel is generally treated as a non-marketable trim (most cars were repurchased and crushed or modified-and-resold with branded titles); the EU Cayenne Diesel and EU Macan Diesel remain in the secondary market but with documented title-history complications and environmental-zone restrictions in some German cities. None of the diesel trims are US-legal for new sale in this scope.

## Confidence and Conflicts Log

This log documents source disagreements and confidence flags by row of the production tables above. Items are listed in approximate order of significance to valuation outcomes.

### High-Significance Conflicts

**991.2 GT2 RS worldwide production:** Sources disagree by a factor of roughly 3× on this figure. The Porsche initial-launch communication suggested an approximate cap of 1,000 units worldwide. Some marque-specialist sources have cited 1,580 units worldwide. One rennlist registry thread cites 3,751 units worldwide, but this figure appears to be a registry tally that may include international VINs not initially counted in the cap and is treated here as the upper-bound outlier. The North America total is documented in registry data as in the range 1,000–1,315 cars. **Recommended valuation working figure:** range 1,000–1,580 ww with a most-likely point estimate of approximately 1,500 worldwide. Confidence: low. This figure should be flagged for downstream review and possible recategorization as the 991.2 GT2 RS continues to age into the secondary market.

**981 Cayman GT4 worldwide production:** Sources vary substantially. Wikipedia historically cited approximately 2,500 units worldwide; rennlist registry threads cite approximately 6,000 units worldwide; Road & Track US allocation reporting cites approximately 1,250 units planned for the US. Canada MY2016 import records show 292 units. **Recommended valuation working figure:** range 2,500–6,000 ww with a most-likely point estimate of approximately 3,500–4,500 worldwide. Confidence: low. The 981 GT4 is the highest-volume halo trim in the 981 lineup and a primary 981-generation valuation lever; this figure should be researched further.

**987.2 Boxster Spyder worldwide production:** Porsche Historisches Archiv records (the most authoritative source) show 1,944 units worldwide (33 CY2009 + 1,418 CY2010 + 493 CY2011 + 0 CY2012). This conflicts with widely-circulated estimates of approximately 3,000 units worldwide that appear in some collector publications and dealer marketing copy. The 1,944 figure is the higher-confidence source and is used as the primary working figure. Confidence: high (Porsche archive). The 3,000 estimate appears to be erroneous and may have arisen from confusion with related model production. North America total is approximately 890 US plus an unspecified Canadian volume; UK records show 237 units.

**918 Spyder Weissach Package take rate:** Sources disagree on the take rate of the Weissach Package within the 918-unit production. Need for Speed wiki and Porsche West Palm Beach cite approximately 25–30%; some collector and dealer sources cite up to 50%. There is no authoritative Porsche-published unit count for Weissach-equipped cars. **Recommended valuation working figure:** range 230–460 ww (25–50% of 918), with a most-likely point estimate of approximately 30–40% take rate (275–365 ww). Confidence: medium. This figure has direct valuation impact because the Weissach Package commands a documented 20–40% secondary-market premium.

**997.2 GT3 RS (3.8L) worldwide production:** Sources cite a range of 1,600–2,000 units worldwide across the production run (2009–2011). The 1,800–2,000 range is most commonly cited but no single authoritative source resolves the figure to a precise number. **Recommended valuation working figure:** approximately 1,800 ww with documented range 1,600–2,000. Confidence: medium-low. North America total (3.8 RS) is approximately 600 units (estimated from registry data, not Porsche-confirmed).

### Caveats on Methodology

This file's production figures were compiled from a combination of Porsche official sources (where available), marque-specialist publications and enthusiast registries (for limited-edition trims and GT cars), and aggregate registration data (for high-volume mainstream trims). Where Porsche has published a cap or a Historisches Archiv figure, that figure is used as the primary working number. Where multiple non-Porsche sources disagree, the range is reported and a most-likely point estimate is offered with explicit confidence flag.

The production-figure rounding convention used throughout (round down to nearest 10 except for sub-50 caps, which are reported exact; preserve marketing-number caps such as 918, 991, 1,948, 1,963, 250, 356, 500, 600 exactly) is applied consistently.

Where source disagreement exceeds approximately 5%, a documented range is reported. Where source disagreement is within 5%, a single rounded figure is reported.

This document is intended as a reference for downstream valuation system input and should be reviewed and re-baselined annually as registry data matures and as Porsche or its marque-specialist publications release additional documentation.
