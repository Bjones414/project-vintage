## Era Context

The 1990s define the modern Porsche company in a way no other decade does.
Porsche entered the decade as a near-bankrupt boutique sports car maker still
selling 1970s-era platforms (911, 928, 944) into a recession-flattened market;
it left the decade as a recapitalised, profitable, modern-process manufacturer
producing two cars built on a shared platform (Boxster 986 and 911 996), with
Toyota Production System–influenced manufacturing and a halved development
time. The financial pivot is roughly bracketed by the appointment of Wendelin
Wiedeking as production chief (1991) and CEO (1993), the abandoning of the
stillborn 989 sedan, the cancellation of carryover platforms (944 ➝ 968 ➝
nothing; 928 ➝ nothing), and the decision to share the front clip, doors,
windscreen, headlights, interior and engine architecture between the new
mid-engined Boxster and the next 911. The Boxster 986 entered production in
August 1996 and the 996 followed in 1997 (1998 model year in Europe, 1999 in
the US), and these two cars are the commercial reason Porsche survived the
decade.

Technically, the decade is defined by two transitions and one engine family.
The first transition is air-cooled to water-cooled: the 964 (1989–94) and 993
(1995–98) are the last two air-cooled 911 generations, and the 996 is the
first water-cooled. The second transition is from K-Jetronic and Motronic 2.x
on the 911 lineage to fully integrated modern engine management with variable
valve technology — VarioCam (introduced on the 968 in 1991, then on the 993
M64 from 1996 model year) and Varioram (993 from MY1996), which materially
changed mid-range torque on otherwise identical-displacement engines. The
engine family that defines the decade's halo cars is the so-called Mezger
engine (named for engineer Hans Mezger), which traces its block design back to
the 1960s race engines and the 911 Turbo, and continues, water-cooled, into
the 996/997 GT3, GT2 and Turbo. The 996 base Carrera, by contrast, used the
new M96 — a clean-sheet integrated-dry-sump water-cooled flat-six shared with
the Boxster, which carried the IMS bearing risk and bore-scoring
vulnerabilities that have shaped 996 valuations ever since.

Commercially, the 1990s also introduced the modern halo-variant playbook. The
964 Carrera RS (1992) revived the RS nameplate after a near-twenty-year gap;
the 964 Turbo S Leichtbau (1992) re-established the "Exclusiv-built lightweight
Turbo" archetype; the 993 GT2 (1995) created the homologation-special
turbocharged GT2 lineage that runs through to the 991 GT2 RS; the 993 Turbo S
(1997) set the template for the Exclusiv-built halo Turbo that the 996/997/991
all repeat; and the 996 GT3 (1999) launched the modern naturally-aspirated
track-special line. These cars were small-volume from the start and have
appreciated correspondingly — they are the centre of mass of the air-cooled
collector market and a meaningful share of valuation work for the period.

## 911 / 964 (1989–1994)

### Generation Overview

- **Years of production:** Calendar 1989 to mid-1994 (factory model years
  1989 through 1994; 1989 was Carrera 4 only).
- **Chassis code:** 964
- **Key engineering points:**
  - First 911 with all-wheel drive as a series option (Carrera 4, 1989).
  - First 911 with Tiptronic four-speed automatic (Carrera 2 only, from MY1990).
  - Power-assisted steering and ABS standard.
  - 3.6L M64 air-cooled flat-six in normally-aspirated cars (247 hp / 250 PS
    base; 260 PS in RS); twin-spark, hydraulic lifters, dual-mass flywheel.
  - 911 Turbo carried over the 3.3L 930 engine for 1991–92 (320 hp / 320 PS),
    then switched to a turbocharged 3.6L M64-derived engine for 1993–94 (355 hp
    / 360 PS).
  - 5-speed G50 manual standard; Tiptronic on Carrera 2 only.
  - Body 85% new vs. carryover 3.2 Carrera per Porsche; integrated bumpers
    were the most visible exterior change.
- **Sub-generation splits:** No major mid-cycle redesign, but the Turbo's
  1993 switch from 3.3 to 3.6L is the most consequential change. There are
  also many MY-specific running changes, including a switch from K-Jetronic
  to electronic Motronic injection on Carreras (the Turbo retained K-Jetronic
  through end of production, both 3.3 and 3.6).
- **Notable design/spec changes mid-cycle:**
  - 1991: Carrera 2 launches alongside C4; Tiptronic option introduced.
  - 1992: Carrera RS (Europe) and Turbo S Leichtbau introduced.
  - 1993: Turbo 3.6 replaces Turbo 3.3; RS 3.8 hand-built at Weissach;
    30 Jahre 911 anniversary edition launched.
  - 1994: Speedster introduced; Turbo 3.6 S (Flachbau and Package) made
    from leftover Turbo chassis at Exclusiv.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 964 lineup is structurally simple — Carrera 2/4, Turbo, RS, Speedster —
but the limited variants overlap in confusing ways for valuation.

The **Turbo 3.3** and **Turbo 3.6** are usually treated as separate models for
valuation, not just sub-variants, because the 3.6 is meaningfully rarer
(reportedly fewer than 1,500 worldwide vs. the much larger 3.3 production)
and is the more sought-after car despite both using K-Jetronic. The 3.6L
turbocharged engine is unique to MY1993–94 and not directly related to the
later 993 Turbo's 3.6L twin-turbo unit.

The **Turbo S Leichtbau** is conceptually the lightweight ancestor to the
993 Turbo S, but mechanically it shares more with the IMSA Supercar
Championship "Turbo S2" cars. It uses the 3.3L engine (M30/69 SL spec) at
381 hp, with Kevlar panels, aluminium doors, no power steering, no air
conditioning standard. It is not the same car as the later **Turbo 3.6 S**
(Flachbau and Package, MY1994) — those use the 3.6L turbocharged engine in
the X88 spec at ~380 hp, were built at Exclusiv from leftover Turbo 3.6
chassis at end of production, and most carry the Flachbau slantnose front
end. The 17 "Package" cars are the same drivetrain as the Flachbau but
with the standard 964 Turbo nose, were built specifically for the US
market at PCNA's request, and are the rarest US-market 964 Turbo variant.

The European **Carrera RS** (M64/03 3.6L, 260 hp) and the US-market
**RS America** are not the same car despite overlapping nomenclature. The
RS America used the standard US C2 247 hp engine and gearbox, retained the
RS name as marketing, and is fundamentally a stripped C2 with the M030
suspension package and a fixed whale-tail spoiler. It weighs ~80 lb less
than a stock C2, not ~300 lb less like the European RS. For valuation, RS
America is roughly a Carrera 2 plus a meaningful (but not RS-tier) premium.
The Carrera RS, in any of its three M-codes (M001 Cup, M002 Touring, M003
N/GT) plus the implied "Basic"/Lightweight, is the true RS and is priced
on a different curve.

The **Carrera RS 3.8** (MY1993) is a step above all of them — Weissach
hand-built, 300 hp, widebody Turbo bodyshell, AL doors, 55 cars total. It is
the rarest series-built 964 RS and prices accordingly. The companion
**RSR 3.8** (51 cars per most sources) was a non-road race car and is out
of scope for this document.

The **30 Jahre 911 Anniversary** edition is the widebody non-Turbo
predecessor to the 993 Carrera 4S concept — Turbo body, normally-aspirated
engine, AWD, available only in three colours (Viola Metallic, Polar Silver
Metallic, Amethyst Metallic). 911 cars total, and the run number is
deliberately the same as the model name. This is a low-volume Anniversary
edition, not an RS-tier limited.

The **Speedster** comes in two forms: the standard narrow-body (930 cars)
and the Exclusiv-built widebody Turbo-look (15 cars). The widebody Speedster
is one of the rarest production 964 variants. RHD Speedsters are
exceptionally scarce: 27 cars total worldwide, split 13 UK / 8 Australia /
4 Singapore / 2–3 Hong Kong.

### Sub-Generation Splits

The 964 has no formally numbered sub-generation, but the meaningful split
points for identification are:

- **Pre-1992 vs. 1992-on Turbo:** Turbo 3.3 only is 1991–92.
- **MY1993 break:** introduces Turbo 3.6, RS 3.8, 30 Jahre, Tiptronic
  refinements. Turbo S Leichtbau cars (built late 1992) are sold as MY1993.
- **MY1994:** Speedster, Turbo 3.6 S (Flachbau and Package). End of 964
  Carrera production runs through mid-1994.

For valuation, the Turbo 3.3 vs Turbo 3.6 split is the only one that
materially affects market pricing of mainstream variants.

## 911 / 993 (1994–1998 production, MY1995–1998)

### Generation Overview

- **Years of production:** Production began January 1994; cars sold as MY1995
  in the US starting late 1994. Production ran through early 1998 (last
  air-cooled 911 completed 31 March 1998 — a Riviera Blue Carrera 4S coupe
  for Jerry Seinfeld, per Wikipedia).
- **Chassis code:** 993
- **Key engineering points:**
  - Last air-cooled 911 generation. Per Porsche, "every part" was redesigned
    although about 20% of parts carried over from 964.
  - Multi-link rear suspension (alloy subframe, alloy LSA design derived from
    the abandoned 989 sedan project) — replaced the 911 trailing-arm rear
    suspension and dramatically improved on-throttle and lift-off behaviour.
  - 6-speed G50 manual standard (first 911 with 6MT base); 4-speed Tiptronic
    available on Carrera and Carrera 4 (later "Tiptronic S" with steering
    wheel buttons).
  - 3.6L M64 air-cooled flat-six. Naturally-aspirated cars: 272 hp / 272 PS
    pre-Varioram, 285 hp / 285 PS Varioram (MY1996+).
  - 3.6L twin-turbocharged M64 in Turbo: 408 hp / 408 PS standard, ~430 hp
    with X50, 450 hp / 450 PS in Turbo S (E50 / X50 ROW; ~424 hp / 430 PS US).
  - Turbo is AWD-only (first 911 Turbo with AWD); GT2 returns to RWD for
    homologation reasons.
- **Sub-generation splits:**
  - **Pre-Varioram (MY1995):** 272 hp 3.6L, no variable intake.
  - **Varioram (MY1996+):** Variable-length intake manifold; power up to 285 hp,
    torque curve broadened. Some markets also got VarioCam exhaust changes
    in the same revision. This is the most consequential mid-cycle update.
  - The Targa was introduced for MY1996, with the panoramic glass roof
    architecture that continued on 996/997.
  - Turbo introduced for MY1996 (no MY1995 Turbo).
  - Carrera 4S (MY1996+) and Carrera S (MY1997) introduced the Turbo-bodied
    naturally-aspirated cars, which is the design language that survived
    into the 996 / 997 / 991 4S line.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 993 lineup is more deeply branched than the 964's, particularly in the
Turbo and S/4S families, and the relevant valuation distinctions are:

**Carrera 4 vs Carrera 4S vs Carrera S.** All three exist on the 993 and all
three carry the M64 NA 3.6L engine at the same 285 hp Varioram spec. The C4
is the narrow-body AWD Carrera. The 4S adds the Turbo's bodywork (50 mm wider
rear track, flared rear arches, larger brakes, 18″ wheels, lowered
suspension) but keeps the C4 AWD drivetrain and NA engine. The Carrera S
(MY1997+) is a US-led variant: same widebody and brakes, but RWD, sold mostly
1997 with a small 1998 stub run. For valuation, the C4S and S sit clearly
above narrow-body Carreras; whether the C4S or C2 S commands a premium is
market-segment dependent (C4S for some buyers, C2 S for purists). All three
are mechanically the same engine — the Turbo body is the price point.

**Turbo vs X50 Turbo vs Turbo S.** This is the most-misunderstood
differentiation in 993-land. The base Turbo is 408 hp twin-K16, AWD, on the
Aerokit I body. The **X50** option (Exclusiv, MY1997 onward) is a power kit
that brings the standard Turbo to ~430 hp via larger turbos and ECU mapping —
roughly Turbo S engine output, but **the X50 car keeps standard Turbo bodywork
and badging.** The **Turbo S** (E50 spec) is a separate model variant: 450 hp
ROW / 424 hp US, larger K24 turbos, additional oil cooler, modified Motronic,
**Aerokit II** body kit (front spoiler with brake-cooling ducts on European
cars, larger rear wing), yellow brake calipers, quad-pipe exhaust, carbon
fibre interior trim, "Turbo S" badging. For valuation, Turbo S commands
roughly a 2–3× premium over an X50-optioned standard Turbo as of 2025–2026
market — the cars are not interchangeable. Most published Turbo S production
figures (181 US / 345 worldwide) are for E50/Exclusiv Turbo S; the cars are
predominantly MY1997 with a small MY1998 carry-over batch.

> **Production-number note for 993 Turbo S, addressing the user's
> commonly-cited 181/183/277/345 conflict:** 181 is the US-only Turbo S
> figure (per Paternie's Redbook excerpts circulating on Rennlist). 345 is
> the worldwide Turbo S figure (well-attested across Stuttcars, Motogallery,
> Supercars.net). 183 is sometimes cited and appears to be a slightly
> different US count (possibly delivered-vs-built or US-vs-US/Canada). 277
> appears in PistonHeads citing PCGB and is **UK Turbo total over the
> production run, not Turbo S** (UK Turbo S is more like 23 cars in 1998
> per the same source). The 181 and 345 figures are what should be used.

**GT2 vs GT2 Clubsport vs GT2 Evo.** The 993 GT2 was Porsche's Group 4
homologation Turbo, RWD-only because the regulations banned AWD in GT2 class
racing. Until model-year 1997, road cars were badged "911 GT" (without the
"2"). 1995–1997 GT2 road: 424 hp / 430 PS. 1998 GT2 (sometimes called Evo
internally): dual-ignition, 444 hp / 450 PS. Clubsport adds roll cage and
harnesses; some markets called these "RSR." The 21 1998 Evo road cars are the
most collectible 993 GT2 spec. The plastic bolt-on fenders are diagnostic —
GT2s do not carry steel widebody fenders even though the underlying chassis
came from the Turbo. **Production conflict:** Wikipedia cites only 57 road
cars total; GT Classics and RM Sotheby's-derived sources cite 194 (161 base +
33 Clubsport + 21 Evo). I have not found a clear reconciliation; the higher
figure is more widely cited in marque literature, but flag this as low
confidence in the conflicts log.

**Carrera RS (993) vs. Carrera RS Clubsport (993).** The base RS (M002) and
Clubsport (M003) share the M64/20 3.8L 300 hp engine, the seam-welded shell,
the lightweight glass and the AL hood. The Clubsport adds a welded roll cage,
deeper front splitter, larger fixed rear wing, deletes carpets, sound
deadening, power windows, A/C and radio, and uses the G50/32 6MT with
shorter ratios. There is no **stripped vs touring** split as on the 964 RS —
the 993 RS Basic effectively occupies both roles, and the Clubsport is the
track-focused additional trim. Neither was US-legal; both are scarcer in
North America by import only.

**The Carrera S Cabriolet** is **not** a factory model. Beverly Hills Porsche
specially-ordered five Carrera S Cabriolets through Exclusiv (sold as MY1998),
plus one VIP-ordered European market car. Total six worldwide. These are
not a separate model line in the variant table because they were custom orders.

### Sub-Generation Splits

- **Pre-Varioram (MY1995):** 272 hp NA cars, no Turbo, no Targa. Diagnostic
  by intake manifold (no Varioram tubes) and by VIN year code.
- **Varioram (MY1996–1998):** 285 hp NA cars, Turbo introduced, Targa
  introduced, Carrera 4S introduced. Most 993s are Varioram-spec.
- **MY1997 watershed:** Turbo S introduced, Carrera S (RWD widebody)
  introduced. This is the high-watermark spec year for the 993.
- **MY1998 stub:** Production tapered through early 1998. Some Carrera S
  cabriolets and special-order cars come from this period. Last car
  completed 31 March 1998.

For valuation, pre-Varioram cars sell at a modest discount to Varioram for
the same body style; Targa and 4S/S sit at meaningful premiums; Turbo and
its derivatives are a separate market.


## 911 / 996.1 (1997 unveil; MY1998 Europe, MY1999 US — through end of 1990s)

### Generation Overview

- **Years of production (1990s portion):** Frankfurt unveil September 1997;
  European MY1998 production began late 1997; US first sold spring 1998 as
  MY1999. The full 996.1 production runs through end of MY2001 / June 2001 and
  is out of decade scope after 1999.
- **Chassis code:** 996 (this section is the "996.1" or pre-facelift)
- **Key engineering points:**
  - First water-cooled 911 generation. Complete chassis re-design, first
    all-new 911 chassis since 1964.
  - Shared front clip, doors, headlights, windscreen, interior architecture
    and engine architecture with Boxster 986 — fundamental cost-share that
    saved Porsche financially.
  - **Two engine families** in the 996 era. The base Carrera uses the M96
    integrated-dry-sump water-cooled flat-six (3.4L for 996.1 = M96.01 / .02).
    The GT3 (and later Turbo, GT2) uses the **Mezger** water-cooled
    flat-six derived from the GT1 race engine, with conventional dry sump
    and a different crankcase architecture. **The IMS bearing issue applies
    to M96, not Mezger.** This is critical for valuation.
  - 6-speed manual standard; 5-speed Tiptronic S optional.
  - 296 hp / 300 PS in 3.4L Carrera; 360 hp / 360 PS in 996.1 GT3 Mk1.
- **Sub-generation splits (within 996.1):**
  - 996.1 Mk1 = 1998–2001, 3.4L M96, "fried-egg" headlights with integrated
    turn signals at the bottom. From MY1999 (Aug 1998 production) the turn
    signal lenses became clear (vs. amber earlier).
  - The 996.2 facelift (MY2002+) brought 3.6L M96, Turbo-style headlights,
    and is out of 1990s scope entirely.
- **Notable design/spec changes (1998–1999 only):**
  - Sept 1997: Carrera Coupe (RWD) unveiled.
  - March 1998: Cabriolet introduced at Geneva, sold as MY1999.
  - July 1998: Carrera 4 announced; Coupe and Cabriolet AWD.
  - October 1998: Carrera 4 production starts as MY1999.
  - March 1999 (Geneva): GT3 Mk1 introduced. **Not sold in North America.**

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 996 is, valuation-wise, a generation of two halves: the M96-engined
Carreras (3.4L for 996.1, 3.6L for 996.2) and the Mezger-engined GT3 / GT2 /
Turbo. **The IMS bearing issue is M96-only.** The GT3 Mk1 launched in
March 1999 with a 3.6L Mezger engine derived from the 911 GT1 race car. The
Mk1 GT3 was not federalised for North American sale, so US market 996 GT3
values are limited by import-only supply for the Mk1 era.

The 996.1 fried-egg headlight is part of the design controversy that has
historically depressed 996 valuations relative to 993 or 997 — owners
described the front fascia as too similar to the cheaper Boxster, and Porsche
addressed this with the 996.2 facelift (Turbo-style headlights, MY2002+).

For the 1998–1999 portion in scope, the only halo variant is the GT3 Mk1.
There is no 996 Turbo until MY2001 (Frankfurt 1999 unveil, summer 2000 US
sale), so the entire decade closes without a water-cooled Turbo for sale.

### Sub-Generation Splits

The 996.1 itself has no formal sub-split through 1999. The MY1999 transition
to clear headlight turn-signal lenses (from the August 1998 production
change) is a minor diagnostic detail.

## 928 GTS (1992–1995)

### Generation Overview

The 928 GTS is the final variant of the long-running 928 grand tourer (1977–
1995 across the full model line). It is included in this 1990s reference
because the GTS variant's production lifespan falls almost entirely in the
1990s. Earlier 928 S4 and 928 GT cars (1987–1991) are out of scope; that
content belongs in a 1980s reference.

- **Years of production:** Late 1991 (Europe, MY1992) through 1995 model year;
  some calendar 1995 / early 1996 builds for the final cars. US market got
  GTS as MY1993 starting spring 1992.
- **Chassis code:** 928 (no separate "GTS" chassis code)
- **Key engineering points:**

    panel linked by red reflector strip.

    pistons (vs. cast), strengthened manual gearbox.

## 968 (1992–1995)

### Generation Overview

The 968 was Porsche's last front-engined four-cylinder transaxle car,
evolved from the 944 S2 platform (which itself evolved from the 924). It is
also Porsche's last new front-engined car until the Cayenne in 2003.

- **Years of production:** 1992 (calendar) through summer 1995, model years
  1992–1995. Production began at the Audi factory in Neckarsulm.
- **Chassis code:** 968
- **Key engineering points:**
  - 3.0L (2,990 cc) M44/43 inline-four — the largest production
    four-cylinder of the era, 240 PS / 237 hp DOHC 16V.
  - First Porsche with **VarioCam** variable intake-cam timing (later
    adopted on 993 Carrera).
  - Six-speed manual standard (carried over from 968 introduction; first
    Porsche 6MT in series production); 4-speed Tiptronic optional.
  - Transaxle layout (rear-mounted gearbox) carried over from 944.
  - Same water-cooled architecture as the 944 / 944 S / S2; substantially
    reworked engine and chassis.
- **Sub-generation splits:** No formal split. UK-specific Sport variant is
  effectively a Club Sport with comfort items added back, and is a
  marketing distinction rather than an engineering one.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 968 Coupe and Cabriolet are mass-market cars but produced in low total
volume — fewer than 13,000 worldwide is small for a mainstream Porsche. The
Cabriolet is overrepresented vs. the coupe in the US market (MY1992 US:
1,366 cabriolets vs. 2,547 coupes; the cabriolet ratio rose further later).

The **Club Sport** is a meaningful step up for valuation. Stripped of rear
seats, sound deadening, electrics, with stiffer suspension and lighter
17-inch wheels standard. 1,923 cars total over three model years. Officially
50 kg lighter than the base coupe and 0–62 mph time dropped from 6.5 to 6.1
seconds (officially) or to 5.8 seconds (independent testing).

The **968 Sport** is essentially a "CS with luxuries" sold only in the UK at
£32,995 (vs. £37,495 base coupe). It outsold the standard UK 968 coupe by
about 8:1 (306 vs. 40) — primarily because it cost £4,500 less than the
luxury coupe but had most of the luxuries plus the CS chassis.

The **968 Turbo S** is the rarest series-built 968 and is a genuinely rare
Porsche by any standard. Built at Weissach by Porsche Motorsport, it uses
**the 944 Turbo's 8-valve head on the 968's 3.0L block** — a deliberate
mismatch driven by the 8-valve head's superior turbo response over the
968's 16-valve head. 305 PS road, paired with the 968 6MT and a single K27
turbo. 175,000 DM list price at launch — about double the base 968.
Production was nominally planned at 100 units but stopped at 13 or 14 cars.
Stuttcars enumerates VINs: WP0ZZZ96ZPS890061…66 (six MY1993 cars), …068…071
(four more MY1993 cars), and three MY1994 cars — total 13. Elferspot and
PistonHeads cite 14, possibly counting the prototype. Either way, this is
one of the rarest road-going Porsches of the era.

The **968 Turbo RS** (option code M005) is included as context: it is the
race version that Porsche Motorsport built for ADAC GT Cup competition.
Engineer Gerd Schmid (per Porsche quarterly *000* magazine) is firm that
exactly three M005 production RS cars were built (yellow / black / white,
all 1993 model year, sold to South Africa, Norway, and Joest Racing
Germany respectively), plus one Guards Red prototype — total four cars.
Wikipedia and some other sources cite four production cars; Schmid's count
of three production + one prototype is the most authoritative. The Turbo RS
is the only front-engined Porsche to wear the RS designation.

The 968 Turbo S and Turbo RS are the homologation pair; the Turbo S was
designed specifically to homologate the Turbo RS for ADAC GT Cup. Schmid
has stated the Turbo S, fully tuned, was faster than the contemporary 911
Turbo 3.6 — internal to Porsche this created friction between the 968 and
911 programs.

### Sub-Generation Splits

The 968 has no formal sub-generation split. The Club Sport (1992 Paris
Motor Show unveil, MY1993 onward) and Turbo S / Turbo RS (1993) are
mid-cycle additions, not facelifts. The 968 ended in summer 1995 with no
successor — Porsche's plans for an evolved 968 were abandoned in favor of
the Boxster project.

## Cross-Generation Notes

**Air-cooled to water-cooled transition.** The transition is canonical: the
993 (production ended March 1998) is the last air-cooled 911 generation;
the 996 (Frankfurt unveil September 1997, US sale spring 1998 as MY1999) is
the first water-cooled. The transition was driven primarily by tightening
European drive-by noise regulations and the difficulty of meeting them with
air-cooling, plus the need for shared engine architecture with the
mid-engined Boxster. Air-cooled prices have appreciated continuously since
the transition; the 993 in particular carries a "last air-cooled" valuation
premium that has held through multiple market cycles.

**Mezger engine lineage.** The Mezger flat-six (named for engineer Hans
Mezger) traces back to the 1960s racing engines. In 1990s scope, the Mezger
appears as the 964 Turbo's 3.3L (carryover from 930) and 3.6L variants, the
993 Turbo / Turbo S / GT2 3.6L twin-turbo, and — most importantly for
valuation — the 996 GT3 Mk1 (Mar 1999, 360 hp 3.6L NA) and later 996 Turbo /
GT2 / GT3. **The Mezger is the engine in every halo 996 and 997 GT3, GT2,
and Turbo through 2012.** It is mechanically distinct from the M96/M97 in
the base 996/997 Carrera and Boxster, and is widely regarded as
substantially more durable. For valuation, the presence or absence of a
Mezger engine is the single biggest engine-related differentiator across
the late-1990s and 2000s 911 lineup.

**The "996 IMS bearing" issue.** The intermediate shaft (IMS) bearing
failure mode applies to **M96 engines only** (3.4L M96.01/.02 in 996.1
Carreras, 2.5L M96.20 in early Boxsters, and the 3.6L M96 in 996.2 Carreras
and Boxster S). Mezger-engined 996s (GT3, Turbo, GT2) do not have an IMS
bearing in the failure-mode sense. In 1990s scope this affects the 996.1
Carrera (1998–1999 portion in scope) and the Boxster 2.5. The single-row
bearing variant introduced in 2000 is the most failure-prone; pre-2000 cars
have a dual-row bearing that is statistically more durable but still
considered a concern. This is a major valuation factor for 996 and early
Boxster cars and an active retrofit market exists.

**Turbo body kit availability across non-Turbo models.** The Turbo body
(wider rear arches, larger brakes, larger wheels) appeared on multiple
naturally-aspirated cars in this era as a way to extract more value from
existing widebody tooling: the 964 30 Jahre 911 anniversary, the 964 C4
"Turbo-look" Cabriolet, the 964 Speedster Turbo-look (15 cars), the 993
Carrera 4S, and the 993 Carrera S (RWD widebody). All five are
naturally-aspirated cars wearing Turbo bodywork. They are not Turbos for
valuation purposes but they consistently price above narrow-body equivalents
of the same drivetrain.

**Tiptronic introduction and which models offered it.** The 4-speed
Tiptronic debuted on the 1989 964 Carrera 2 (not Carrera 4). It was
extended to the 968 (Tiptronic 4AT) and 928 (separate Mercedes-sourced 4AT).
The 993 Tiptronic was 4AT, available on Carrera and (later) Carrera S; **not
available on Carrera 4 or Turbo.** The 996 Tiptronic became 5AT ("Tiptronic
S" with steering wheel buttons), available on Carrera and Carrera 4. The
GT3 Mk1 was 6MT only.

**Naming inconsistencies across markets.**
- The European Carrera RS (1992) and the US RS America (MY1993–1994) are
  not the same car. See the 964 Variant Differentiation Notes.
- The 993 GT2 was badged "911 GT" until 1997.
- The 996 GT3 Mk1 was not sold in the US at all; first US-market 996 GT3
  is the Mk2 (MY2004).
- "Turbo S" applies to four very different cars in this decade: 964
  Turbo S Leichtbau (3.3L, 381 hp, 86 cars), 964 Turbo 3.6 S Flachbau /
  Package (3.6L, 380 hp, ~93 cars), 968 Turbo S (3.0L 8V turbo, 305 hp,
  13–14 cars), and 993 Turbo S (3.6L twin-turbo, 450/424 hp, 345 cars).
  None of these cars share an engine architecture or chassis with any of
  the others.

## Confidence and Conflicts Log

This is the editorial-review queue: every record where sources disagree,
where only Tier C sources were available, or where granularity is
fundamentally not publicly documented.

**Significant source conflicts:**

1. **993 GT2 road production total.** Wikipedia cites 57 road cars total;
   GT Classics, RM Sotheby's, Stuttcars and Classic.com cite 194 (split
   161 base + 33 Clubsport + 21 Evo). The 194 figure is more consistent
   with the multiple cited sub-counts and is what marque literature
   generally uses. Wikipedia's 57 may be limited to a specific spec or
   year. **Recommendation: use 194 with conflict flag.**

2. **993 Carrera RS production total.** Wikipedia cites 1,014 cars total
   (227 Clubsport); GT Classics and other sources cite 1,104 cars total
   (213 Clubsport: 190 LHD + 23 RHD). Both figures appear repeatedly in
   marque literature. **Recommendation: cite both, do not pick one.**

3. **964 Turbo 3.3 cumulative production.** The Wikipedia phrasing "A total
   of 3,660 964 Turbos were built" is ambiguous — it sits in a sentence
   that immediately precedes discussion of the 3.6 Turbo, so the 3,660
   figure may include the 3.6L cars or may be 3.3-only. Other sources
   cite the 3.6 separately as "fewer than 1,500." If 3,660 is total all
   964 Turbos and the 3.6 was ~1,500, then 3.3-only is ~2,160. Treat as
   medium confidence pending Paternie cross-check.

4. **964 Turbo 3.6 production.** "Fewer than 1,500" is the canonical figure
   in multiple sources, with "less than a third" reaching North America
   (so US ≤ ~500). Specific year-by-year breakdowns are not publicly
   well-documented at the granularity this brief asked for.

5. **964 Turbo S Leichtbau production.** 86 is the most-cited figure (80
   official + 6 added end-of-production). Some sources still cite 80.
   Iconic Auctioneers' description of "81st car was added on as part of
   the recorded end of production run on November 20, 1992. The 82nd-86th
   cars lack definitive production information" is the most detailed
   account I have located. Treat 86 as high confidence, with the caveat
   that 80 still appears in some literature.

6. **968 Turbo S production: 13 vs 14 vs other.** Stuttcars enumerates
   13 specific VINs (10 MY1993 + 3 MY1994). Elferspot magazine cites 14.
   PistonHeads thread mentions "circa 14" and "17". The Stuttcars VIN
   enumeration is the most specific I have located and supports 13 cars.
   Elferspot's 14 may include the prototype. **Recommendation: 13–14
   range, with VIN-level enumeration favored.**

7. **968 Turbo RS production: 3 vs 4.** Per Porsche quarterly *000*
   interview with engineer Gerd Schmid (cited via Elferspot), exactly 3
   M005 production cars were built plus 1 prototype, total 4 cars.
   Wikipedia and some other sources cite 4 production cars. The Schmid
   account is the most authoritative for this specific question. The
   discrepancy may turn on whether the prototype is counted as production.

8. **928 GTS production by year.** Multiple sources give materially
   different per-year figures. The Rennlist forum thread compiles four
   independent counts that disagree on every single year. The Wikipedia
   European total of 2,498 (sum of 955+621+523+399) is internally
   consistent. The cumulative US figure of 406 (per FlatSixes citing
   Rennlist) is plausible but not from Porsche-official sources. **The
   "77 US" figure that appears in Wikipedia's intro is misleading; it
   appears to refer to MY1995 only and is not the full US tally.**

9. **Boxster 1990s-portion production.** Total 986 production over
   1996–2004 is 164,874 (Porsche official, Christophorus). The
   1996–1999 sub-total is "over 55,000" per Supercar Nostalgia, but I
   have not located a Porsche-official source that splits 1996–1999
   from MY2000+ explicitly. Per-year breakdown for 1997–1999 is not
   publicly available at the granularity this brief asked for.

10. **996 GT3 Mk1 production.** Total is genuinely not well-documented
    in publicly accessible sources. Various community estimates exist;
    none cited in this document is high-confidence.

**Methodological note:** This document deliberately uses markdown tables
rather than image-based reproductions of original source data. Numbers like
"1,910" or "274" that appear precise should be regarded as the
most-frequently-cited figure across multiple secondary sources, not as
guaranteed Porsche-AG-official numbers. For the highest-stakes valuation
work (e.g., the auction-record cars in this scope), original Porsche
Certificates of Authenticity (Kardex / Geburtsurkunde) for individual
chassis remain the gold standard.
