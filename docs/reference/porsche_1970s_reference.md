# Porsche Road Cars: 1970s Reference

## Era Context

The 1970s reshaped Porsche from a small specialist building two model lines (911 and 914) at the start of the decade into a four-line manufacturer (911, 924, 928, plus the 911 Turbo halo) by the end. Two events bracket the decade: the 1973 Carrera RS 2.7 closed the long-hood 911 era at its commercial and engineering peak, and the 928 launch in 1977 — Porsche's first clean-sheet, in-house, water-cooled, front-engined design — declared a strategic intent to move beyond the air-cooled rear-engined formula that had defined the company since 1948. Between those bookends sat the model year 1974 transition to the impact-bumper "G-body" 911 (driven by U.S. NHTSA 5-mph crash standards), the progressive rollout of hot-dip galvanized chassis (introduced model year 1976 for full-shell coverage and substantially extending 911 service life), and the 1975 launch of the 911 Turbo, the first turbocharged production car from a major sports-car manufacturer and the founding text for the high-performance road-car category that defined the rest of the era.

The technical through-line of the decade was emissions and fuel-economy compliance, which forced Porsche through several induction-system transitions. The carbureted 911 T departed first; Bosch mechanical fuel injection (MFI) carried the high-output cars through 1973; and Bosch K-Jetronic (CIS, Continuous Injection System) mechanical fuel injection became Porsche's standard solution for U.S.-market 911s starting with the 1973.5 911 T and extending across the 911, 911 S, 911 SC, and 930 Turbo through the rest of the decade. The 1973 Oil Crisis and the ensuing global recession compressed sports-car sales generally; Porsche's relatively fuel-efficient flat-six (compared with American V8s and Italian V12s) and the company's strategic move into the 924's smaller-displacement segment helped shield it from the worst of the contraction. The Carrera RS 2.7 of 1973 — built originally to homologate the 2.8 RSR for Group 4 — established the template that every subsequent road-going RS, GT3, and GT3 RS would refer to: a stripped, lightweight, MFI-fed flat-six homologation special with a ducktail spoiler, wide rear arches, and a price premium that the market would pay.

Strategically, the most consequential decisions came from CEO Ernst Fuhrmann, who took the helm in March 1972. Fuhrmann believed the 911 was a dead end and pushed development of the front-engined transaxle program (924 launched MY 1976, 928 launched MY 1977) as the company's future. He simultaneously approved the 911 Turbo (930) as a homologation-driven halo for Group 4 racing, a decision that paradoxically extended the 911's life by demonstrating commercial viability at the top of the range. By the close of 1979, the lineup was: 911 SC (introduced MY 1978 as a single mid-range model consolidating the previous 911 / 911 S / Carrera 3-tier hierarchy), 911 Turbo 3.3 (introduced MY 1978 with intercooler), 924 (entry-level transaxle, with 924 Turbo / 931 added MY 1979 in Europe), 928 (V8 grand tourer, in second year of U.S. production). The 914 had been discontinued in 1976; the 912E filled a one-year U.S.-only gap in 1976 between the 914's exit and the 924's U.S. arrival in mid-1976 as a 1977 MY car. Fuhrmann would be dismissed in late 1980, in part because the 928 had not displaced the 911 as projected, but the strategic infrastructure he built — multi-line production, water-cooled engineering, a turbocharged halo — defined what Porsche would be for the next thirty years.

## 911 Long-Hood (F-body), 1970–1973

### Generation Overview

- **Years of production (in scope):** 1970–1973 (model years C-, D-, E-, F-series). The full F-body lifespan ran 1969–1973; the 1969 launch year is documented in the 1960s file.
- **Chassis code:** Type 911 (no internal "F-body" code at Porsche; the term is enthusiast usage referring to the August-1972-to-July-1973 F-series production run, though it has come to label the entire long-hood generation).
- **Body styles:** Coupe, Targa.
- **Engine family:** Air-cooled SOHC flat-six. Bore × stroke 84 mm × 66 mm = 2,195 cc through MY1971 ("2.2L"); 84 mm × 70.4 mm = 2,341 cc from MY1972 ("2.4L," more accurately 2.3L). Trim-specific tunes: Type 911/57 (T carb), 911/51 (T MFI), 911/52 (E MFI), 911/53 (S MFI) for 2.4L; earlier 2.2L tunes 911/03 (T), 911/01 (E), 911/02 (S).
- **Transmissions:** Type 901 4- or 5-speed manual through MY1971; new Type 915 5-speed manual from MY1972 (stronger, conventional H-pattern, ~9 kg heavier). Sportomatic 4-speed semi-automatic optional throughout.
- **Drivetrain:** Rear-engined, rear-wheel drive.
- **Sub-generation splits:**
  - **C-series (MY1970)** — first 2.2L; piston-cooling jets not yet standard; oil tank in stock location behind right rear wheel.
  - **D-series (MY1971)** — galvanized bodyshell zinc coating begins; piston-cooling oil-jet "squirters" introduced; sealed chain tensioner.
  - **E-series (MY1972)** — engine displacement to 2,341 cc; new Type 915 gearbox; oil tank moved forward of right rear wheel with external filler door behind right-side B-pillar (one-year-only, scrapped after gas-station-attendant confusion).
  - **F-series (MY1973)** — oil tank returned to behind-the-wheel location; 911 S gains front chin spoiler; black exterior trim (horn grilles, light surrounds, badging) replaces chrome/gold; rear wiper standard; January 1973 — US-spec 911 T switches from MFI to Bosch K-Jetronic CIS (the "1973.5" cars), making the T the first 911 with the CIS injection system that would carry the line through the rest of the decade.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The C-series (MY1970) and D-series (MY1971) are commercially nearly identical cars and are routinely lumped together as "the 2.2-liter long-hoods" by market participants and auction houses. The most consequential D-series change is the introduction of factory-applied zinc galvanizing on key sheet-metal areas — a multi-year corrosion-protection rollout that would not reach full-shell coverage until MY1976. Crankcase oil-jet "squirters" for piston cooling are also a D-series first; some 911 enthusiasts cite this as the meaningful technical break between C- and D-series.

The 1972 E-series brought the largest mid-decade engineering change: the 2,341 cc engine, the new Type 915 gearbox (replacing the original Type 901 unit, which had been at its torque limit), and the one-year-only forward-mounted oil tank with the external filler door. Cars with the door — recognizable by the body opening behind the right-side B-pillar — are sought by collectors, although the oil-tank relocation was scrapped after one model year (allegedly because gas-station attendants were filling the oil tank with gasoline). The 1973 F-series moved the tank back. The F-series MY1973 also brought the 911 S front chin spoiler (under the bumper), black trim across the lineup replacing chrome/gold, and the January-1973 mid-year switch from MFI to Bosch K-Jetronic CIS on US-spec 911 T cars — the so-called "1973.5" cars. Some collectors strongly prefer the MFI 1973-build T over the K-Jet 1973.5 on grounds of throttle response; mechanical differences are real but engine condition matters more than period at this point.

The Carrera RS 2.7 is the canonical halo of the air-cooled era and the founding spec for every road-going RS that followed. It was conceived in 1972 as a homologation special for the 2.8 RSR race car, with Porsche's marketing department initially convinced that 500 stripped, expensive 911s would not sell in the European market. They sold out in less than two months at the Paris Motor Show, prompting two further production runs and an eventual program total widely cited at 1,580 cars (chassis numbers run 9113600011 to 9113601590). The full production breaks down — by source consensus — as 17 RSH "Homologation" cars (an ultra-stripped sub-trim with no M-package and the lowest weight, ~960 kg, all LHD), 200 M471 "Sport / Lightweight" cars (Recaro shells, fiberglass bumpers, thin steel and glass, ~975 kg), 1,308 M472 "Touring" cars (911 S–level interior with rear seats, carpet, sound-deadening, ~1,075 kg), and 55 M491 RSRs (race-only, with the 2.8L 300-hp engine; sometimes cited as 57). Touring volume is sometimes given as 1,317 (e.g. counts from Starkey's chassis database), and the full program total is sometimes given as 1,590; we record the range. The Touring outsold the Lightweight by roughly 6.5:1 because Porsche's marketing team — burned by the slow-selling 911 R of 1967 — pushed buyers toward a comfort-equipped variant; in 2026 collector valuations have reversed, with M471 Lightweights commanding a substantial premium over M472 Tourings.

US-market participation in the RS 2.7 was effectively zero at the time of new production: Porsche did not federalize the MFI Type 911/83 engine for U.S. emissions, and the cars were never imported through Porsche-Audi distribution. A small number entered the US grey market in period (typically through racing teams) and the population slowly grew through the 25-year-rule import window after 1998. For valuation purposes, U.S.-registered RS 2.7s should be presumed grey-market unless documented otherwise.

### Sub-Generation Splits

For this generation the standard split for valuation is C/D (2.2L) versus E/F (2.4L), with the F-series MY1973 typically the most-desired mainstream long-hood because of the 2.4L torque, the strong Type 915 gearbox, the standard front oil tank, and the late-period long-hood styling without the impact-bumper compromise. Among RS 2.7s, the conventional split is "first series" (chassis ~11–500), "second series" (~501–1067), and "third series" (~1068–1590): first-series cars retain the most aggressive lightweighting throughout, while later-series cars increasingly used heavier production-grade panels, glass, and trim once Group 3 homologation had been achieved and the lightweight measures were no longer required.

## 911 G-body, 1974–1977 (G/H/I/J Series)

### Generation Overview

- **Years of production (in scope):** 1974–1977 (model years G-, H-, I-, J-series). The full G-body lifespan ran 1974–1989 and Porsche built ~198,500 G-bodies in that 16-year window; the 911 SC (1978–1983) and Carrera 3.2 (1984–1989) live in their own sections. Note: enthusiasts loosely use "G-body" for the entire 1974–1989 second-generation 911, although Porsche internally used "G-series" only for the MY1974 production run.
- **Chassis code:** Type 911 (continued).
- **Body styles:** Coupe, Targa.
- **Engine family:** Air-cooled SOHC flat-six. Bore × stroke 90 mm × 70.4 mm = 2,687 cc ("2.7L") for the G/H/I/J base, S, and Carrera (MFI Euro and K-Jet US). For MY1976–1977 the new top variant — Carrera 3.0 — used a 95 mm × 70.4 mm × 2,994 cc ("3.0L") naturally aspirated derivative of the 930 Turbo engine. Trim-specific tunes: 911/81 (911 Lux RoW K-Jet), 911/82 (911 S 49-state K-Jet), 911/84 (911 S California, with thermal reactor), 911/83 (Carrera 2.7 MFI Euro/RoW), 930/02 (Carrera 3.0 K-Jet).
- **Transmissions:** Type 915 5-speed manual; 4-speed available; Sportomatic 3-speed semi-automatic optional throughout.
- **Drivetrain:** Rear-engined, rear-wheel drive.
- **Sub-generation splits:**
  - **G-series (MY1974)** — first impact-bumper 911. Three-tier lineup: 911 (base, 150 hp), 911 S (175 hp), 911 Carrera (Euro 210 hp MFI / US 175 hp K-Jet). All 2.7L. Wide-flared Carrera body adopts RS-style rear arches. Ducktail spoiler optional on Carrera (and in Germany only for MY1974, due to road-rule constraints; whale-tail introduced as the rest-of-Europe option).
  - **H-series (MY1975)** — 911 S now 165 hp (US/49-state) due to additional emissions equipment (air injection pump, thermal reactor on California cars); compression dropped. 911 Carrera continues; 911 S Silver Anniversary edition appears for MY1975. Body shells now substantially galvanized.
  - **I-series (MY1976)** — *the* major year. Lineup consolidates from three to two: 911 Lux (Euro) / 911 S (US-market name, both 165 hp K-Jet 2.7L) and Carrera 3.0 (Euro only, 200 hp 3.0L K-Jet, replacing the 2.7 MFI Carrera). **Full hot-dip galvanized bodyshell** introduced — Porsche begins offering a 6-year body-corrosion warranty. New cast-aluminum front suspension cross-member; redesigned interior trim; body-color driver's mirror standard. The 1976 Carrera 2.7 MFI "Sondermodell" — 113 coupes for the German market and 30 narrow-body Targas for the Belgian Gendarmerie — runs out the remaining stock of 911/83 RS engines and gearboxes.
  - **J-series (MY1977)** — incremental updates only. Boost gauge in tach for the first time on the Turbo (separate Turbo section). Carrera 3.0 final year. Replaced from MY1978 by the consolidated 911 SC (separate section).

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The MY1974 transition is the single most consequential year-on-year break in 911 history short of the 964 launch in 1989. Federally mandated 5-mph impact bumpers reshaped both ends of the car; the new front bumper integrated directional indicators and (on the Carrera) a chin spoiler, while the rear gained large rubber-faced overriders. Despite enthusiast pessimism at launch, the impact-bumper integration was elegant enough to survive structurally unchanged through 1989. Less visible but more important: the 1974 redesign also moved Porsche to the K-Jetronic CIS injection system across the US lineup (Carrera, S, base 911) — the system that, with refinements, would carry every air-cooled 911 through to the 964 era.

The "Carrera" name in 1974–1977 is genuinely confusing and is the single biggest source of valuation errors in this generation. There were three different cars wearing the badge: (1) a US-market Carrera 2.7 (1974–1975) that was mechanically a wide-arch 911 S with K-Jet, (2) a Euro-market Carrera 2.7 MFI (1974–1976) that was mechanically a 1973 RS Touring with G-body bodywork (same Type 911/83 engine and 210 PS), and (3) a Euro-market Carrera 3.0 (1976–1977) with the new naturally aspirated 3.0L derivative of the 930 Turbo engine. The Carrera 2.7 MFI is the most desired of these — same engine as the 1973 RS but in galvanized G-body shell — and was never sold in the US at the time of new production. The MY1976 "Sondermodell" coupes (113 cars sold only in Germany) are a particular collector target as the last MFI 911 street cars; the 30 Belgian-Gendarmerie Targas are scarcer still. The Carrera 3.0 (1976–1977) is materially different: a heavier, more luxurious, lower-strung-engine car (200 PS) that prefigured the 911 SC formula, and sometimes cited as the better all-around classic-era 911 to drive even though collectors typically prefer the MFI cars.

The MY1976 introduction of full hot-dip galvanizing was a watershed for long-term 911 ownership: pre-1976 G-bodies are notably more rust-prone than 1976+ cars, and Porsche's contemporaneous 6-year corrosion warranty (extended to 7 years in 1981 and 10 years in 1985) reflected real engineering confidence, not marketing alone. Body shells from this period regularly outlive their first three or four engines if maintained.

US-market 911 S cars from MY1975 (the H-series) suffered from the well-documented "pulled head studs" problem caused by the California-spec thermal reactor mounted directly below the left cylinder bank, generating chronic localized heat. Porsche addressed this with new head studs and a magnesium crankcase change for MY1976. For valuation, original 1975 California-delivery 911 S cars merit close inspection of the upper-block and head-stud condition; many have had the case replaced.

The 1975 911 S Silver Anniversary edition (MY1975 only, 1,063 cars) was Porsche's first commemorative limited edition — built to mark 25 years of Porsche sports car production — distinguished by Diamond Silver Metallic paint, special black-leatherette-and-tweed upholstery, and a numbered console plaque. Sources cite 504 (some say 510) of the 1,063 going to the United States. Available as both Coupe and Targa, mechanically identical to a regular 911 S 2.7.

### Sub-Generation Splits

For this section the cleanest split is pre-galvanized (MY1974–1975) versus post-galvanized (MY1976–1977). The 1976+ cars have substantially better long-term chassis durability, a meaningfully refreshed interior, and (in the Carrera 3.0) the engine that would underpin every 911 SC and Carrera 3.2 to follow. For collectors willing to accept the rust risk, MY1974 Carrera 2.7 MFI Euro cars with original ducktail are the most desired G-body configuration short of the 1973 RS itself.

### Generation Overview

- **Years of production (in scope):** 1978–1979 (model years K- and L-series). ### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 911 SC was originally conceived as a runout consolidation model — Porsche's then-CEO Ernst Fuhrmann had decided the 911 would be wound down in favor of the 928 by ~1981, and the SC was meant to simplify production for the final years. The simplification was real (one normally aspirated 911 instead of three), but the wind-down never happened: Peter Schutz, who replaced Fuhrmann in 1981, immediately reversed the strategy on the strength of strong SC demand. The MY1978–1979 SCs represent the first-half of the SC's six-year run; in valuation terms these early cars are typically less desired than the MY1981+ cars because they have lower power (180 hp versus 188 hp from MY1980 RoW or 204 hp from MY1981 across markets) and use the older head-stud / case-stud configuration that is sometimes cited as marginally less robust than the 1980+ refinement.

The chrome-trim MY1978 SC is visually unique in the SC family and is sometimes marketed as a "first-year" collector subset; collector market premiums for trim chrome are modest. Targa share of SC production in the 1970s portion was notably high (substantially higher than coupe in some MY1978 markets), reflecting the Targa's continued popularity after a decade of availability.

The SC's launch coincided with the 911 Turbo 3.3's introduction in MY1978; both shared the 3.0L block as their starting point, with the Turbo bored to 3,299 cc and force-induced. Together they established Porsche's "two cars, one platform" strategy that ran until the SC's replacement by the Carrera 3.2 in 1984.

### Sub-Generation Splits

Not material within the 1978–1979 in-scope window. The MY1978 → MY1979 changes are cosmetic (trim color) and minor mechanical (gear ratios). The major SC sub-generation split occurs at MY1980 (1980s file): power increase, vin-format change, switch from K-series to A-program internal designation.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The Carrera RS 3.0 is materially different from the 1973 Carrera RS 2.7 despite the shared "Carrera RS" naming and similar specification philosophy. ### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 1975 launch year of the 911 Turbo is the most commercially consequential model-year debut in 1970s Porsche history. Conceived as a 400-unit homologation special for FIA Group 4 (the 911 Turbo's competition derivative becoming the 1976 934), the production car wildly exceeded its quota — the 1,000th 930 was completed on May 5, 1976 — and became Porsche's flagship. The 1975 model year was Europe-only; US-market 911 Turbos arrived for MY1976 with the revised Type 930/51 emissions-controlled engine, badged "Turbo Carrera" through ~1976 and simply "Turbo" thereafter. The 3.0L cars (MY1975–1977) suffered a reputation for sudden lift-off oversteer when boost arrived mid-corner — the "Widowmaker" nickname dates from this period — driven by the combination of short wheelbase, rear weight bias, narrow tires by modern standards, and a turbocharger with significant lag.

The MY1978 transition to the 3.3L intercooled engine is the major mid-decade change. The intercooler — Porsche's first use in a production car — allowed an effective compression increase, smaller turbocharger (less lag), 40 hp more power (300 PS vs 260 PS RoW; or 265 SAE US versus 245), and importantly a bigger jump in torque from ~253 lb-ft to ~304 lb-ft. The visual change was the raised "tea-tray" rear wing, which now incorporated the intercooler ducting into its leading edge and stood vertically at the sides; the underlying wide-body shell was unchanged. The MY1978 launch also brought 917-derived four-piston aluminum brake calipers — a substantive upgrade over the 2.7 Carrera-derived units used on the 3.0L cars. Weight rose from 1,195 kg to ~1,300 kg, partly offsetting the power increase; collector preference is roughly evenly split between the lighter, edgier 3.0L and the more capable, more livable 3.3L.

US-spec 3.3L Turbos (MY1978–1979) used a slightly less aggressive turbo and additional emissions equipment, producing ~265 SAE versus the RoW 296 SAE figure. After MY1979, Porsche withdrew the 930 from the US and Japanese markets due to inability to meet 1980 emissions and CAFE rules; the 930 returned to the US for MY1986 (covered in 1980s file). MY1978–1979 US 3.3L Turbos are therefore the only federally legal new-production 930s sold in the US for the entire 1980–1985 window — a fact that materially affects their valuation.

### Sub-Generation Splits

The major split is 3.0L (1975–1977, no intercooler, whale-tail) versus 3.3L (1978–1989, intercooled, tea-tray). Within the 3.0L window: MY1975 cars are RoW LHD only and the smallest-volume year; MY1976 brings US market entry; MY1977 brings the boost gauge in the tachometer pod (a signature Turbo detail thereafter), 16" Fuchs as standard, and centre console as standard. Within the 3.3L 1978–1979 window: MY1979 cars relocate the wastegate from external to within the rear muffler, gain Pasha checkered upholstery as a new option, and (in North America) standardize air-conditioning.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 912E exists because of a calendar mismatch: the 914 ended US production for MY1976, but the 924 was not yet ready for US emissions/safety certification (it would arrive in July 1976 as a 1977 MY car). Porsche's solution was to drop the 914's federalized 2.0L Type-4 flat-four into the 911 G-body — for one year only, US only — at a price ($10,845) that sat $3,000 below the 911 S and ~$3,600 above the 914 2.0L. The 912E should not be confused with the original 912 (1965–1969, 1960s file), which used a four-cylinder derived from the 356; the 912E's engine is derived from the 914 2.0L (which is in turn derived from the VW 411 EA831 platform), and the Es are the only 912s ever with galvanized chassis.

The 912E is the slowest air-cooled 911-bodied Porsche of the decade — period road tests measured ~11.3 seconds 0-60 mph and ~115 mph top speed — but its 23 mpg observed economy was excellent for the era and the chassis dynamics are essentially those of a contemporary 911 with less weight over the rear wheels (the four is lighter than the six). Approximately 500 of the 2,099 cars are reported to have been ordered with the electric sunroof; that subset commands a moderate premium. About half the original production is reported still extant.

## 914, 1970–1976

### Generation Overview

- **Years of production (in scope):** 1970–1976 (full lifespan in scope; pre-1970 production exists for MY1970 cars built in 1969 but is included in the 1970s file by model-year convention). Total 914 program production: 118,978 cars across all variants 1969–1976.
- **Chassis code:** Type 914 (914-4) and Type 914/6 (six-cylinder).
- **Body style:** Two-seat targa (Targa-roof) only — removable composite roof panel that stowed in the rear trunk.
- **Engine families:** 
  - **914-4:** VW-Porsche Type 4 air-cooled SOHC flat-four (originally derived from VW 411/412 and Audi 100 EA831). Three displacements: 1.7L (1970–1973), 1.8L (1974–1976), 2.0L (1973–1976). Bosch D-Jetronic injection on 1.7L US cars; carbureted 1.7L for some Euro markets; Bosch L-Jetronic on 1.8L US cars (a US-specific addition for emissions); Bosch D-Jetronic and L-Jetronic variations across the 2.0L production.

- **Drivetrain:** Mid-engine, rear-wheel drive — Porsche's first mid-engine production road car (excluding the limited 904 GTS of 1964).

  - **1970–1972:** "tail-shift" cars; 914-6 in production; 1.7L only on the four-cylinder.

  - **1974:** 1.7L replaced by 1.8L (US cars get L-Jetronic); rear bumper guards added; impact-bumper-era styling adjustments.
  - **1974–LE only:** 1,000-car Limited Edition "Can-Am" run (Bumblebee + Creamsicle).

### Variant Differentiation Notes

For valuation, the 914-6 is materially scarcer and pricier than any 914-4 — fewer than 3,400 produced versus over 115,000 four-cylinders, and the Porsche flat-six is a more valuable engine in its own right. 914-6 cars typically command 3-5× the price of equivalent-condition 914 2.0L four-cylinders in 2026. The 914-6 GT (with the race-prepped 210 hp engine, ~40 cars) is a distinct collector tier above the standard 914-6.

Within the four-cylinder variants, the 2.0L cars (1973–1976) have the most power and are the most desired drivers; the 1.8L US cars are the "standard" 914 of the late period; the 1.7L cars are the most numerous and the most affordable. The Bumblebee and Creamsicle Limited Editions of 1974 are distinct collector items: 1,000 cars total, all 2.0L, all US-market, all built in a 7-week window in February-April 1974, and approximately 25% of the original population is reported to remain extant. The Bumblebee colorway (black with Sunflower Yellow) survives in larger numbers than the Creamsicle (Light Ivory with Phoenix Red), possibly because the more visually distinctive Bumblebee was less likely to be repainted to a standard 914 color over time.

The 914 program ended in 1976 because the joint venture with Volkswagen had become commercially difficult: VW had refused to renew the 914 body-supply contract on terms Porsche could meet, and Porsche's own development priorities had shifted to the front-engined 924 (which used a related VW-Porsche joint development pathway but was sold as a pure Porsche). The 1976 close of 914 production created the one-year gap that the 912E filled in the US market.

### Sub-Generation Splits

Pre-1973 (tail-shift transmission, chrome/painted bumpers, narrow rear engine fender bulge) versus 1973+ (side-shift, wider rear fender for 2.0L, rear bumper guards added). Then again 1974/early-1975 (chrome or painted bumpers) versus 1975-1976 (heavy rubber-covered impact bumpers — not popular with most enthusiasts as they significantly compromise the styling). The collector-grade 914-4 cars are typically the 1973–1974 2.0L pre-impact-bumper coupes and the 1974 Limited Edition cars; 1975-1976 cars trade at modest discounts despite being mechanically improved.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 924 was the most commercially significant new-product launch in 1970s Porsche history by unit volume. Originally designed as VW project EA425 (a successor to the 914 to be sold under VW badging), it was returned to Porsche when VW canceled its sports-car plans in 1974; Porsche bought back the design, contracted Audi-NSU to build it at Neckarsulm, and used VW-Audi running gear (EA831 engine block, Audi gearbox internals) under a Porsche-designed chassis and Porsche-designed cylinder head. The result was the lowest-priced Porsche of the late 1970s, the first Porsche with a transaxle layout, the first water-cooled Porsche road car, and the first Porsche with a conventional fully automatic transmission as an option.

The launch was rough commercially: 1976 sales were tepid (~5,140 cars in a partial year, all RoW), partly because Porsche enthusiasts were skeptical of the VW-derived running gear and partly because the US-spec 95 hp engine was widely judged underpowered for a Porsche. The 1977 introduction in the US, the mid-1977 US power bump from 95 to 110 hp, and the addition of right-hand drive transformed the commercial picture: ~25,590 cars in MY1977, peaking at over 21,500 in MY1978. The 924 Turbo (931) launch for MY1979 in Europe was Porsche's response to the persistent "underpowered" critique; output rose to 168 hp DIN with a KKK turbo, intercooler-less in this first generation. The North American 924 Turbo (the federalized 931) arrived for MY1980 — that production year falls in the 1980s file — but the cars were assembled from June 1979 onwards.

For valuation, MY1976 RoW cars (LHD only, 4-speed manual only, first-year cars) have a small early-adopter premium; MY1977.5+ US cars with the 110 hp engine drive better than the earlier 95 hp variant; MY1978+ cars have the full chassis galvanizing and significantly better long-term durability. The 924 Martini, 924 Limited Edition, and 924 Sebring '79 special editions of the 1970s are all desirable to specialist collectors but production volumes are not consistently published in the primary marque references reviewed; we record this as an editorial-review item.

### Sub-Generation Splits

Pre-galvanization (MY1976) versus post-galvanization (MY1977 onwards). 4-speed only (MY1976) versus 5-speed available (MY1977+). 95 hp US (MY1976-1977 early) versus 110 hp US (MY1977.5+). And the introduction of the Turbo for MY1979 RoW, which represents a different car commercially even though the body is shared.

- **Body style:** 2+2 hatchback coupe (no Targa, no Cabriolet).

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 928 was the most strategically important new-model launch in 1970s Porsche history regardless of the 924's higher unit volume. CEO Ernst Fuhrmann had concluded by 1971 that the 911's rear-engined air-cooled architecture was a commercial dead end — that emissions, safety, and consumer-luxury expectations were incompatible with the 911's engineering DNA — and had pushed the 928 program through development as the eventual flagship replacement. The decision to use a clean-sheet front-engined V8 with transaxle layout, water cooling, aluminum body panels (doors, hood, front fenders for weight saving), and the Weissach passive-rear-steer geometry was a complete repudiation of the 911 formula.

The launch reception was technically rapturous — the 928 won 1978 European Car of the Year (the only sports car ever to do so), beating the BMW 7 Series and Ford Granada — and commercially soft. MY1978 production of 3,830 cars was below Porsche's projections; MY1979 rose to 5,440, the second-highest 928 year ever (only MY1984 with the S model US launch ever exceeded it). The car cost more than a 911 Turbo, was heavier, and lacked the 911's enthusiast credibility despite arguably being the better long-distance grand tourer. Sales never displaced the 911 as Fuhrmann had projected, and Fuhrmann was dismissed in 1980. His successor Peter Schutz immediately committed to the 911's continued development, locking in a two-flagship strategy that ran for the next decade.

For valuation, the 1978–1979 base 928s are typically the cheapest 928s in the market in 2026 — earlier engines than the more powerful S/S2/S4 cars, fewer luxury features, and lower demand among modern enthusiasts who prefer the more capable later cars. In #2 (excellent) condition, MY1978 cars trade roughly $32,000-40,000 in 2026 markets; manuals carry a meaningful premium over automatics (the manual-trans share was ~60% in 1978-1979 and would later flip to majority automatic, making 1970s-era manual 928s a relative scarcity).

The 928 was Porsche's first production V8 and would remain the only front-engined V8 Porsche road car until the front-engined Panamera launched in 2009. The MY1978 Pasha-pattern interior — abstract checked upholstery in the central seating areas — has become a 1970s design signature. The MY1978-1979 cars are easily distinguished from later 928s by the absence of any rear spoiler (the smooth, uninterrupted hatch line) and the 5-hole telephone-dial wheels.

### Sub-Generation Splits

Within the 1970s window, the MY1978 → MY1979 transition is minor (battery-box relocation; minor interior trim updates; RoW power rise to 230 hp). The major mid-life break is at MY1980, when the 928 S launches alongside the base 4.5L car for RoW (3.0L would be added later as well; the S used a 4.7L unit at 300 PS); that change is in the 1980s file. The 1970s portion of 928 production is therefore relatively cohesive: original 4.5L K-Jet cars without rear spoiler, with telephone-dial wheels, in the original Anatole Lapine / Wolfgang Möbius design language untouched by the spoiler additions of the S model.

## Sonderwunsch and Other Specials, 1970s

### Generation Overview

The Sonderwunsch ("special wishes") program was formalized as a department at Zuffenhausen in 1978 under Rolf Sprenger, but informal one-off and bespoke work for selected customers had been a Porsche service since the late 1950s, intensifying through the 1960s and early 1970s. The two best-known 1970s Sonderwunsch products predate the formal department: a road-converted Porsche 917 K (Kurzheck) commissioned by Italian Count Gregorio Rossi di Montelera in 1975, and a second similar 917 K "Street" in 1977. Both used the same 4.5/4.9L flat-twelve race engine in detuned form, with mirrors, lights, mufflers, and a leather-clad cabin grafted onto the prototype-class race chassis. These are race cars converted for road use rather than road cars per se and are catalogued here as cross-references.

The 1970s-era series-production special editions — model-year-specific cosmetic packages built in batches — fit alongside but distinct from the Sonderwunsch one-offs:

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The Silver Anniversary 911 S of 1975 — Porsche's first commemorative-edition model — established the template for Porsche's commemorative-edition strategy that would scale dramatically through the 1980s and 1990s (50th Anniversary 1982, 25 Years of 911 in 1989, RUF/Sonderwunsch slant-noses, etc.). Of the 1,063 Silver Anniversary cars, sources cite 504 to 510 going to the United States, with the remainder split among RoW markets and a small allocation to RHD markets. The cars were available as both Coupe and Targa with the 175-PS 2.7L K-Jet engine and 5-speed manual; their commemorative plaque is numbered (e.g. "Silver Anniversary Modell Nr. 122 / 1063"), and authentic numbered cars trade at modest premiums over comparable standard 911 S 2.7 cars.

The 1976 Carrera 2.7 MFI Sondermodell coupes (113 cars, German market only, built June-July 1976) and the Belgian Gendarmerie Targas (20 narrow-body cars, also 1976) are the rarest series-built 1970s 911s after the Carrera RS 2.7 itself. Both used the original 911/83 RS engine that had been carried forward through the Carrera 2.7 MFI line; the Sondermodell coupes were essentially Porsche running out the remaining stock of 210-PS engines and Type 915 gearboxes after the Carrera 3.0 had taken over as the top-trim car. The Belgian Gendarmerie Targas are particularly interesting from a documentation-and-provenance perspective — they entered service as state-owned police vehicles and have unusually clear ownership histories.

For 1970s 924 special editions, primary marque sources are inconsistent and detailed production-volume documentation is not consistently available; the Martini Edition is best documented (build window December 1976 – March 1977, Grand Prix White only, Martini stripes), but even there exact production numbers vary across published sources. We record this as an editorial-review item.

The two 917 Kurzheck Street commissions of 1975 and 1977 are the most commercially significant Sonderwunsch products of the decade in absolute value terms — both are catalogued individually in racing-car registries — and they prefigured the formal launch of the Sonderwunsch Abteilung in 1978. Alongside the 917 Streets, the Sonderwunsch department after 1978 took on bespoke 911 Turbo modifications (slant-nose / Flachbau conversions appeared experimentally in 1978-1979 and were formalized as Sonderwunsch options for MY1981 production — that formalization is an early-1980s file event). The first formal slant-nose 930 Turbo Sonderwunsch was delivered in July 1981; first-generation 930 slant-noses (1981-1983) were the rarest at 58 cars per published sources. None of these formal slant-nose cars fall within the 1970s file scope, but the Sonderwunsch department's foundation in 1978 does.

## Cross-Generation Notes

### The 1973 → 1974 transition: long-hood to G-body

The single most consequential year-on-year break in 911 history within the decade came at MY1974, when the long-hood ("F-body") 911 was replaced by the impact-bumper G-body. The change was driven by the U.S. NHTSA's 5-mph low-speed-impact standard (which would not have been met by the long-hood's chrome bumpers), and Porsche's design solution — integrating large body-color bumpers with internal energy-absorbing structures into the existing 911 silhouette — was admired enough to survive without major change for 16 model years. Beyond the bumpers, the MY1974 redesign included new wider rear arches on the Carrera (using the Carrera RS 2.7's wide-arch tooling), a redesigned interior with new high-back seats and redesigned dashboard, and the introduction of Bosch K-Jetronic CIS injection across all U.S.-market 911s. The U.S. K-Jet adoption (already partial in MY1973 with the 911 T) was the engineering response to U.S. emissions standards and would carry through every air-cooled 911 to 1989. Ride height was also raised marginally for MY1974 to meet headlight-height regulations.

### The galvanized chassis rollout

Porsche's transition to a fully galvanized 911 bodyshell was a multi-year process, not a single-year event. Zinc coating of selected underbody areas began with the D-series MY1971; major panel galvanizing expanded through the mid-1970s; and the full hot-dip-galvanized bodyshell was introduced for MY1976 (I-series). Porsche extended a 6-year body-corrosion warranty starting with MY1976 cars, then a 7-year warranty for MY1981, then a 10-year warranty by MY1985. Pre-1976 G-bodies (i.e., MY1974 and MY1975) are notably more rust-prone than 1976+ cars in any climate with road salt or coastal humidity; this is one of the cleanest valuation breaks in the air-cooled 911 collector market.

### Carrera RS 2.7 → RS 3.0 → SC/RS family lineage

The 1973 Carrera RS 2.7 established the template for every subsequent road-going Porsche RS: stripped, lightweight, MFI-fed (or later electronically injected), wide-arch, ducktail or bigger rear spoiler, short production run for homologation purposes. The 1974 Carrera RS 3.0 carried the formula forward into the G-body era with the new 3.0L aluminum-case engine; only 55-56 road cars were built, and the program ended after one model year because Porsche shifted its racing focus to the 1974 Carrera RSR Turbo and the subsequent 911 Turbo / 934 / 935 family. There would be no further road-going Porsche RS until the 964 Carrera RS of 1991 — a 17-year hiatus that makes the 1974 RS 3.0 a unique link in the Carrera RS lineage. The 1984 911 SC/RS (20 cars, Group B rally homologation) was the only intervening RS-style limited build, but it falls outside the 1970s window. The 1973 RS 2.7's commercial success (1,580 cars sold despite a price double a standard 911 S) established the proof-of-concept that Porsche's halo cars could pay for themselves — a lesson that would underpin the 911 Turbo program from 1975 onwards.

### The 911 Turbo as a market category creator

The 1975 Porsche 911 Turbo was the first turbocharged production car from a major sports-car manufacturer (BMW's 2002 Turbo of 1973-1974 was a small-volume launch from a saloon-car manufacturer and was withdrawn during the oil crisis). The 911 Turbo's commercial success — initial 400-unit homologation target wildly exceeded, with 1,000 cars built by mid-1976 — established the template for high-performance turbocharged road cars across the rest of the 1970s and 1980s: Maserati Biturbo (1981), Ferrari 288 GTO (1984), Lancia Delta Integrale (1986), Audi Quattro turbo (1980), and dozens of others. The Turbo's cultural reach exceeded its sales volume; it appeared in films, set new performance benchmarks, and trained a generation of Porsche customers to expect 300-PS performance from a 911. Its return-on-engineering was perhaps the highest of any car program of the decade for Porsche.

### Bosch K-Jetronic (CIS) adoption

Bosch's Kontinuierliche Einspritzung (CIS), marketed as K-Jetronic, is mechanical fuel injection that meters fuel continuously rather than pulse-by-pulse — a fundamentally different approach from the Bosch MFI used on high-output 911s through 1973 and from the Bosch L-Jetronic electronic system used on US 914 1.8L cars and the 912E. Porsche adopted K-Jet first for the US 911 T in January 1973 (the "1973.5" cars) as an emissions response, then for all MY1974 911s sold in any market (with the exception of the Euro Carrera 2.7 MFI), then for the 911 SC, 911 Turbo, Carrera 3.0, and Carrera 3.2 — every air-cooled 911 from MY1974 through 1989. K-Jetronic adoption is the single most important emissions-compliance decision in 1970s Porsche engineering, and is the reason the 911 remained federally legal in the U.S. throughout the decade (as compared to many European competitors that withdrew from the U.S. market for emissions reasons).

### The 914 / VW partnership and the 912E gap year

The 914 program was a Porsche-VW joint venture initiated in the late 1960s: VW needed a sports-car halo to replace its aging Karmann-Ghia, and Porsche needed an entry-level model below the 911. The agreement called for VW to assemble bodies (at Karmann coachbuilders, Osnabrück) and Porsche to handle marketing in select markets; the 914-4 was sold as a "VW-Porsche" in Europe through VW dealerships and as a "Porsche" in North America through Porsche-Audi dealerships. The 914-6 (Porsche flat-six) was sold under the Porsche name everywhere. By 1972, VW's executive team had concluded the joint venture was uneconomic for them, and contract negotiations for body supply broke down. The 914-6 was discontinued at the end of MY1972 (the engine was too expensive relative to the body-and-platform costs, especially as a 2.4L 911 T was scarcely more expensive); the 914-4 continued through MY1976 but at declining volumes. With the 924 not yet certified for the US market, Porsche slotted the 912E into the I-series 911 G-body for MY1976 only (US only) as a one-year stop-gap. The 924, when it arrived in July 1976 as a 1977 MY car, replaced both the 914 and the 912E in the US market.

### The transaxle program: 924 + 928

Porsche's strategic bet of the late 1970s was that water-cooled, front-engined, transaxle-layout cars represented the future. The 924 (entry-level 4-cylinder) and 928 (V8 grand tourer) shared a transaxle architecture (engine front, gearbox rear, connected by torque tube) but were designed independently and shared almost no parts in their initial form. The 924 was contracted to Audi-NSU for assembly at Neckarsulm; the 928 was assembled at Zuffenhausen alongside the 911. By the end of 1979, the transaxle program had grown into the lineup's volume base (the 924 was the highest-volume Porsche model 1976-1979) and arguably its profit base (the 924 was high-margin given its Audi-derived running gear). The 944 (1982) and 968 (1992) would extend the transaxle program until 1995, when the last 968 and last 928 GTS rolled off the line — closing the architectural era that had launched in 1976.

### MY1979: the inflection year

MY1979 was the single busiest mid-decade year in Porsche product strategy. The 911 SC was in mid-life (its second model year); the 911 Turbo 3.3 was in its second year with the new intercooled engine; the 924 Turbo (931) launched in Europe (with US launch built into MY1979 production for MY1980 sale); the 928 was in its second model year of US sales and at its highest-volume launch year. The long-hood era was completely behind Porsche; the impact-bumper era was firmly established; the transaxle program was profitable; and the 911 Turbo had created a market category that the rest of the industry was racing to enter. Within 12 months, Ernst Fuhrmann would be dismissed as CEO (1980); within 24 months, Peter Schutz would commit to the 911's continued development; within five years, the 928 S, 944, and 911 Carrera 3.2 would together define Porsche's 1980s lineup. The 1970s closed with Porsche substantially larger, more diverse, and more profitable than it had begun the decade.

### General note on confidence

For variants where worldwide production exceeds ~5,000 units, our confidence in the rounded-to-nearest-10 figure is high; the underlying production records (tracked by chassis VIN range) are well documented. For variants under ~1,000 units (Carrera RS 2.7 sub-variants, Carrera RS 3.0, 1976 Carrera 2.7 MFI Sondermodell, 914 LE colorways, 1975 Silver Anniversary Edition, 1976 911 Turbo 3.0 first US batch, 1975 911 Turbo 3.0 H-series), individual chassis are catalogued in registries and our confidence is correspondingly higher than for mass-production variants where Porsche's internal records are the only authority. For 911 base, S, and Lux variants in MY1976-1977, the apparent dual-naming of "Lux" (RoW) and "S" (US) creates the highest single source of confusion in the dataset; our totals are derived from summing the separate Stuttcars rows but the line-item interpretation should be verified against a primary Porsche document.
