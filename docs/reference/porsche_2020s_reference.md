# Porsche Road Cars: 2020s Reference

*Document scope: MY2020 through Q1 2026 production. Snapshot as of April 30, 2026. The decade is in active production; this file is intended to be revised as model lines complete and as currently-in-production figures become available.*

---

## Era Context

The 2020s opened for Porsche on two simultaneous platform events. The 992-generation 911 had been revealed in November 2018 at the Los Angeles Auto Show and entered worldwide deliveries through MY2020. Within weeks of the same auto-show season, Porsche revealed the J1-platform Taycan — the company's first all-electric production car — and began deliveries to customers in late 2019/MY2020. The decade's character was set in those months: continuation of the 911 as the brand's gravitational center, alongside the most consequential platform expansion since the Cayenne entered production in 2002. By April 2026, that initial pairing has been joined by the Macan EV (PPE platform, MY2024), the all-new 972-generation Panamera (MY2024), the 9Y0 PA2 facelift Cayenne (MY2024), and the 992.2 facelift 911 (MY2025) — the latter introducing the first production hybrid 911, the Carrera GTS T-Hybrid.

Three trends define the decade so far. First, electrification has progressed unevenly: the Taycan and Macan EV represent fully realized BEV product lines on dedicated platforms, while the 992.2 T-Hybrid system applied to the Carrera GTS (May 2024) and the 992.2 Turbo S T-Hybrid (Munich 2025) demonstrates a non-plug-in performance-hybrid architecture distinct from the prior plug-in E-Hybrid systems used in Cayenne and Panamera. The 992.2 Turbo S T-Hybrid has been described by Porsche as the most powerful production 911 ever built, at 711 PS (701 hp) from a 3.6-litre 9A3 flat-six paired with two electric exhaust-gas turbochargers and an electric motor in the PDK. Second, the limited-edition cadence on the 992 has been unusually dense even by Porsche standards: the Targa 4S Heritage Design Edition (992 units, MY2021), the Sport Classic (1,250 units, MY2023), the Dakar (2,500 units, MY2023), the S/T (1,963 units, MY2024), the Spirit 70 (1,500 units, MY2026), plus regional editions across the GTS and Turbo trims. Three of those are Heritage Design models — a deliberate "lifestyle" product line announced in 2020 and intended to span four entries by decade's end. Third, the brand's electrification trajectory has been revised mid-decade. ICE production of the 718 Boxster and Cayman ended in October 2025, with the EV successor delayed to 2026/2027, and Porsche has reversed course to confirm that future 718 generations will offer ICE alongside electric variants. The first-generation Macan ICE is scheduled to end production in summer 2026 with a multi-year gap before its hybrid/ICE successor arrives in 2028.

The decade's macro events bracket the product changes. The COVID-19 pandemic disrupted deliveries through 2020 and produced a semiconductor shortage that constrained allocation through 2021–2022, particularly in North America. Porsche AG completed its Frankfurt Stock Exchange listing in September 2022 — the largest German IPO in over two decades — establishing financial separation from the Volkswagen Group while VW retained majority ownership. Market reception of high-end EVs softened in 2024–2025, prompting Porsche's product-plan revisions noted above. As of April 2026 the decade is approximately 60% elapsed with substantial production ahead, particularly for current-generation Cayenne, Panamera 972, Taycan facelift, Macan EV, and 992.2 911 variants. The Mission X concept (2023) remains a concept as of this snapshot; a production halo car above the 911 has been the subject of public statements by Porsche leadership throughout 2025 and into Q1 2026 but has not been confirmed for production. The 911 GT2 RS for the 992 generation has been the subject of consistent spy-shot reports and order-book leaks in 2025–2026 but has not been officially launched as of this writing.

## Per-Model/Generation Sections

### 911 (992.1, MY2020–MY2024 portion)

#### Generation Overview

The 992.1 entered production for MY2020 worldwide and MY2020/MY2021 in North America, replacing the 991.2. Compared with its predecessor it is wider by 45 mm, uses extensive aluminium bodywork, and standardises wide rear arches across all variants — a styling cue that previously only appeared on the wide-body trims. The platform was designed from the outset to accommodate hybridisation, with the new eight-speed PDK leaving room in the bell housing for an electric motor; that capability went unused on 992.1 production trims and was first activated in the 992.2 (see below).

The 992.1 carrier engines through MY2024 were the 9A2 Evo 3.0-litre twin-turbocharged flat-six (Carrera, Carrera S/4S, Carrera GTS / Targa 4 GTS), the 9A2 Evo 3.7-litre twin-turbocharged flat-six (Turbo and Turbo S, with the Turbo nominally 3.745 L), and the GT-department 4.0-litre naturally-aspirated flat-six derived from the 991.2 GT3 RS lineage (GT3, GT3 Touring, GT3 RS, S/T). The 992.1 introduced two transmission options to the standard line — eight-speed PDK on Carrera through Turbo trims, with a seven-speed manual reserved for the base Carrera, Carrera T, GT3 / GT3 Touring (with a six-speed in the GT3), Sport Classic (seven-speed manual), and S/T (six-speed). The 992.1 GT3 was notable for restoring the manual transmission as a no-cost factory option from launch — resolving an enthusiast complaint that originated with the 991.1 GT3's PDK-only debut.

Body styles produced through the 992.1 portion of the decade: Coupe, Cabriolet, Targa (4 and 4S only). Drivetrain: rear-wheel drive on Carrera, Carrera S, Carrera T, GTS, GT3, GT3 Touring, GT3 RS, Sport Classic, S/T; all-wheel drive on Carrera 4, Carrera 4S, Carrera 4 GTS, Targa 4 / 4S / 4 GTS, Dakar, Turbo, Turbo S.

Mid-cycle additions through the 992.1 era: the GTS family was added for MY2022 with revised aerodynamics including an Aerokit option that replaces the deployable rear spoiler with a fixed wing; Carrera T returned as a regular catalogue model in MY2023 between Carrera and Carrera S; Sport Classic, Dakar, GT3 RS launched in 2022–2023; S/T launched MY2024 as the 60th-anniversary model. Numerous regional editions (America, Belgian Legend, Ben Pon Jr, 50 Years Porsche Of Taiwan, Le Mans Centenaire, 50 Years Porsche Design) were produced in unit-counted runs.

#### Variant Differentiation Notes — 992.1

The 992.1's value structure runs from Carrera (3.0 L, RWD, manual or PDK, no Sport Chrono std) through Carrera S to Carrera 4S, Targa 4 / 4S, then to GTS-tier (3.0 L turbocharged with intercooler relocation borrowed from Turbo, PASM Sport std, GTS-spec brakes), then Carrera T (lightweight, RWD, manual std, less insulation, no rear seats), Turbo / Turbo S (3.745 L, AWD, PDK only), and the GT-department variants. Sport Chrono Plus take rate on 992.1 Carreras was very high (above 80% in Porsche-disclosed market reporting), driven by the chrono cluster's standardization with the rotary mode dial on the steering wheel and the Launch Control function that improves 0–60 mph by 0.2 seconds. PCCB carbon-ceramic brakes remained an option except where standard (Turbo S, GT3 RS, Sport Classic, S/T, Dakar where appropriate). Burmester High-End Surround Sound was the dominant audio upgrade with a take-rate Porsche has not officially disclosed but is widely reported as above 50% on US Carrera S and above 70% on Turbo / Turbo S.

Manual versus PDK pricing implications inside the 992.1 are unusual: on the Carrera, Carrera S, and Carrera GTS the seven-speed manual was a no-cost option (US market), while on the GT3 the six-speed manual carried no premium over the seven-speed PDK. The Sport Classic and S/T were manual-only by design; the GT3 RS and Dakar are PDK-only by design. The Carrera T is manual-standard with PDK as an option.

Allocation-only options: the 992.1 GT3 RS Weissach Package adds magnesium wheels, additional carbon-fibre body panels including the roof, hood, and front fenders, titanium roll cage, anti-roll bars and shear panels, and is available only with a roll cage in jurisdictions where this is legal. The 992.1 GT3 also offers the Weissach Package in a pared-down form. The Sport Classic Heritage Design Package is automatically included; the S/T Heritage Design Package is a separate option that adds a different paint scheme (white with stripes, gold accents) and additional interior corduroy.

The Carrera GTS Aerokit is an option that replaces the deployable rear spoiler with a fixed wing and adds a more aggressive front fascia. Take rate on Aerokit is reportedly low — under 15% per dealer disclosure to specialty press — because most buyers prefer the visual continuity with the Carrera S body.

#### Sub-Generation Splits — 992.1 vs 992.2

The 992.2 facelift, revealed May 28, 2024, is mechanically a generation event for the GTS, Turbo S, and forthcoming Turbo trims because of the engine and hybrid-system changes (see 992.2 section below). For the Carrera, Carrera S, Carrera 4S, Targa 4S, Carrera T, GT3, and GT3 Touring — the 992.2 update is more incremental: same fundamental 3.0 L engine architecture (with new turbochargers and intercooler relocation in the Carrera S for a +30 hp bump), revised front fascia consolidating fog lamp and indicator functions into Matrix LED headlamps, fully digital 12.65-inch instrument cluster replacing the 992.1's analogue centre tachometer, and a relocated start button on the left of the steering wheel. Ergonomics changes affect feel more than mechanicals. Treat the GTS / Turbo S as a clear sub-generation split (different engine families); treat the Carrera / Carrera S / GT3 as an evolutionary update within the same engine family but with significant cosmetic and electronics changes.

### 911 (992.2, MY2025–present)

#### Generation Overview

The 992.2 was revealed by Porsche on May 28, 2024 and entered production in June 2024 at Zuffenhausen. The launch trims were the Carrera (still 3.0 L 9A2 Evo at 388 hp, RWD-only at launch, PDK-only at launch) and the Carrera GTS T-Hybrid — the latter being the first hybrid-powertrain 911 ever produced at scale. The 992.2 GTS uses an all-new 3.6-litre 9A3 flat-six paired with a single electric exhaust-gas turbocharger (eTurbo) and an electric motor integrated into the new eight-speed PDK transmission housing, drawing energy from a 1.9 kWh, 400-volt lithium-ion battery mounted under the front hood. The combined system output is 541 hp / 449 lb-ft for the rear-wheel-drive variant, with all-wheel-drive Carrera 4 GTS and Targa 4 GTS variants following.

The 992.2 introduced key cabin and electronics changes across all trims: a fully digital, curved 12.65-inch instrument cluster replaces the 992.1's analogue centre tachometer, a new push-button start replaces the left-hand twist key, and revised infotainment with Apple CarPlay+ integration is available. The fascia was also revised, with all front-light functions consolidated into the headlamps (no separate indicator/DRL housing in the bumper) and the front bumper given larger functional intakes feeding the relocated intercoolers. The rear features a thicker full-width light bar with integrated Porsche script.

The 992.2 lineup expansion proceeded through 2024–2026:
- May 2024: Carrera, Carrera GTS T-Hybrid (Coupe and Cabriolet, RWD and AWD GTS variants)
- October 2024: GT3 992.2 and GT3 Touring 992.2 (4.0 L NA carry-over engine, manual + PDK, revised aero, lower final-drive ratio)
- October 2024: Carrera T 992.2 (manual-only, lightweight)
- January 2025: Carrera S (PDK-only, 473 hp from new turbos and intercoolers)
- April 2025: Spirit 70 (1,500-unit Heritage Design model, GTS Cabriolet T-Hybrid base)
- July 2025: Carrera 4S, Targa 4S
- September 2025: Turbo S T-Hybrid (711 PS / 701 hp, two eTurbos, most powerful production 911 ever)
- 2025: Carrera T Club Coupe (PCA edition)

The 992.2 GT3 retains the 4.0 L flat-six derived from the GT-department lineage, rated at 510 PS / 503 hp — unchanged from the 992.1 GT3 in headline numbers. Acceleration figures improve marginally due to lower final-drive gearing; top speed drops slightly to 311 km/h (193 mph). Both the wing and Touring body remain available, both with manual or PDK at no cost.

The 992.2 Carrera dropped manual transmission availability at launch; the Carrera T (manual-only) and GT3 / GT3 Touring (manual + PDK) are the only 992.2 trims through Q1 2026 that offer a clutch pedal. The Carrera S also went PDK-only for the 992.2, dropping the seven-speed manual that was a no-cost option in the 992.1.

The 992.2 platform-level significance is the introduction of the 9A3 engine family. The 9A3 is described by Porsche as a clean-sheet flat-six replacing the long-running 9A2 (in turn the successor to the 9A1 that debuted in 2009). The 9A3 is designed around the T-Hybrid system and uses a 400-volt electrical architecture, two-piece exhaust headers, redesigned heads, repositioned intercoolers, and new eTurbo turbocharger units that incorporate small electric motors on the turbine shafts to eliminate lag.

#### Variant Differentiation Notes — 992.2

The T-Hybrid system is the central engineering event across the 992.2 GTS and Turbo S trims. It is non-plug-in: the 1.9 kWh battery is recharged through brake regeneration and through the eTurbo working as a generator, and is not designed to be charged externally. This distinguishes T-Hybrid clearly from the prior plug-in E-Hybrid systems on Cayenne and Panamera, which are plug-in PHEVs with much larger batteries (25.9 kWh on the latest Cayenne and Panamera E-Hybrid trims) and meaningful electric-only ranges. The T-Hybrid system is performance-derived: Porsche has explicitly framed it as motorsport-derived technology (the company has cited learnings from the 919 Hybrid LMP1 and 963 LMDh programmes), with the goal of eliminating turbo lag and adding boost without significantly increasing weight or complexity. Total weight gain on the GTS T-Hybrid versus the 992.1 GTS is approximately 50 kg (110 lb); on the Turbo S T-Hybrid the gain is approximately 85 kg (187 lb).

The Turbo S T-Hybrid uses two eTurbos versus one on the GTS — a deliberately structured hierarchy. The Turbo S also gets electro-hydraulically controlled Porsche Dynamic Chassis Control (ehPDCC), faster than the prior PDCC system because it is integrated with the 400-volt battery; an optional front-axle lift system actuates faster for the same reason. Standard ceramic-composite brakes on the Turbo S are 420 mm front / 410 mm rear — Porsche has stated this is the largest PCCB system ever fitted to a two-door Porsche. Tires increase to 255/35ZR20 front and 325/30ZR21 rear, with the rear gaining 10 mm in width versus the 992.1 Turbo S.

The Spirit 70 is the third Heritage Design 911 and the first on the hybrid platform. Production is 1,500 units worldwide. The launch colour is Olive Neo, an exclusive metallic green; lower body sections, front bumper lip, and the five-spoke "Fuchs" design wheels are finished in a grey-gold called Bronzite. The interior uses the Pasha fabric pattern — a graphic pattern of intentionally arranged rectangles inspired by 1970s Porsche interiors — in black/Olive Neo. The Spirit 70 is a Cabriolet only and uses the rear-wheel-drive Carrera GTS Cabriolet T-Hybrid as its mechanical base; performance figures match the GTS Cabriolet (541 hp). MSRP at US launch was $240,000 plus $2,250 delivery, processing, and handling. Deliveries to US Porsche Centers were scheduled to begin late summer 2025.

The Turbo S T-Hybrid pricing is materially higher than the 992.1 Turbo S it replaces. Porsche disclosed coupe MSRP of $272,650 with destination — approximately a $31,000–$34,000 increase versus the outgoing 992.1 Turbo S at $238,700 (2025 MSRP). Porsche's press release notes that the MSRP excludes potential tariffs.

Manual-transmission availability has narrowed in the 992.2 generation. Through Q1 2026, the only manual-transmission 992.2 variants are the Carrera T (manual-only, RWD) and the GT3 / GT3 Touring (manual or PDK at no cost). The 992.1's manual-transmission Carrera S, Carrera 4S, Carrera GTS family, and base Carrera have all been replaced by PDK-only 992.2 successors. This is a significant valuation event for buyers seeking a manual non-GT 911: the 992.1 Carrera T with manual is the most recent manual-transmission non-GT 911 with a near-current chassis.

#### Sub-Generation Splits — Within 992.2

Treat the 992.2 Carrera and Carrera S (3.0 L non-hybrid, evolutionary update of 992.1 powertrain) as one engineering subset, and the 992.2 GTS / Turbo S T-Hybrid (3.6 L 9A3 hybrid, all-new engine family) as a separate subset. The GT3 / GT3 Touring 992.2 are an evolutionary update of the 992.1 GT3, with the same 4.0 L NA engine but lower final-drive ratio and revised aerodynamics. The Carrera T 992.2 is the entry-level manual continuation with the 3.0 L 9A2 Evo. The Spirit 70 is mechanically a GTS Cabriolet T-Hybrid with bespoke trim. The forthcoming non-S Turbo (announced as planned but not yet launched as of April 2026) is, per industry reporting from Jalopnik and Carbuzz, expected to be non-hybrid, which would mean the Turbo S T-Hybrid sits above a non-hybrid Turbo in the lineup.

### 718 Boxster + Cayman (982, 2020s portion)

#### Generation Overview

The 982-generation 718 had been on sale since 2016 and continued production through October 2025, when Porsche ended ICE production of both the Boxster (roadster) and Cayman (coupe). The 2020s portion of the 982's life is significant for two reasons: the return of the naturally-aspirated 4.0-litre flat-six to mid-engine Porsches in 2019/MY2020 on the GTS 4.0, Boxster Spyder, and Cayman GT4 — partially reversing the 2010s transition to flat-four turbocharged engines on base/S/GTS — and the eventual end of ICE production in 2025, which marks the close of mid-engine internal-combustion Porsches.

The 4.0-litre flat-six in the GTS 4.0, Boxster Spyder (MY2020), and Cayman GT4 (MY2020) is derived from the 9A2 Evo 3.0-litre twin-turbocharged flat-six used in the 992.1 Carrera, with the turbochargers removed and the displacement increased through bore and stroke changes. It produces 414 hp on the GTS 4.0 (Boxster and Cayman) and 414 hp on the GT4 / Spyder, with an 8,000 rpm redline. Cylinder deactivation is fitted as a fuel-economy measure — a first for Porsche.

In MY2022, Porsche introduced the 718 Cayman GT4 RS and 718 Boxster Spyder RS (the latter for MY2024, separated by two years from the GT4 RS). These two variants use a different 4.0-litre flat-six — one derived from the 992 GT3, with 493 hp / 331 lb-ft, a 9,000 rpm redline, and individual throttle bodies. This is the same engine family used in the 911 GT3 R race car. Both the GT4 RS and Spyder RS are PDK-only (seven-speed) and rear-wheel drive only. The GT4 RS is the more track-focused of the two, with a fixed swan-neck rear wing, while the Spyder RS has a ducktail spoiler and a manually-operated lightweight soft-top. Within the 718 lineup, this represents a within-generation engine event: the Cayman GT4 / Boxster Spyder use the 991.2 GT3-base-engine-derived 4.0 L (NA, 414 hp, 8,000 rpm redline), while the GT4 RS / Spyder RS use the 992 GT3-derived 4.0 L (NA, 493 hp, 9,000 rpm redline) — the same engine family as the 911 S/T but in a mid-mounted layout.

Per Porsche's press release on the GT4 RS, the engine in the GT4 RS is positioned as derived from the 992 GT3 R race-car spec rather than the road-car GT3, which technically makes the GT4 RS the closest mid-engine sibling to a current GT3 Cup race car.

ICE production of the 718 Boxster and Cayman ended in October 2025 according to Porsche Cars North America. Per the announcement, dealer inventory will continue selling into early 2026 in some markets, with stock levels declining through 2026. EU markets had already lost ICE 718 availability in mid-2024 due to new cybersecurity regulations that were not retrofitted into the 982. The all-electric replacement is delayed: originally planned for 2025/2026, the launch is now expected 2026 or 2027. In a December 2025 reversal, Porsche has announced that future fifth-generation 718 Boxster and Cayman models will offer ICE alongside electric variants, with the ICE versions requiring re-engineering of the chassis to accommodate combustion engines.

The 718 Style Edition (MY2023) was a regional appearance package on the base Boxster and Cayman with specific exterior accents and interior trim. The GT4 RS 25 Years Boxster Edition was a small special run celebrating the 25th anniversary of the original Boxster (1996 launch), produced in limited quantities late in the 982's life.

ICE 982 production ending October 2025 closes the generation. The fifth-generation 718 (anticipated 2026/2027 BEV; ICE follow-on around 2028 per Porsche's December 2025 reversal) will be a separate vehicle line.

### Cayenne (9Y0, 2020–present)

#### Generation Overview

The third-generation Cayenne (9Y0 SUV / 9Y3 Coupe) launched in 2017 for MY2018 in Europe and MY2019 in North America. It is built on the Volkswagen Group MLB Evo platform, shared with the Audi Q7/Q8, Bentley Bentayga, and Lamborghini Urus. The 9Y0 portion of the 2020s decade covers MY2020 through Q1 2026 ICE production, plus the introduction of the all-electric Cayenne Electric (separate vehicle, 2026 launch — outside of this file's scope on the basis of the launch timing falling at the end of Q1 2026 / beginning of Q2 2026 reporting period; cross-referenced briefly below).

The MY2020 model year added the Coupe body style — the 9Y3 — alongside the SUV. The Coupe has a more rakish roofline, lowered 20 mm at the rear, narrower A-pillar, and 18 mm wider track. It is offered with a fixed glass panoramic roof or an optional carbon-fibre roof. From the start, the Coupe was offered in Cayenne Coupe, Cayenne S Coupe, and Cayenne Turbo Coupe trims, with Coupe-body GTS, E-Hybrid, Turbo S E-Hybrid, and Turbo GT variants added in subsequent model years.

The 9Y0 mid-cycle facelift was revealed in April 2023 for MY2024 (referred to as "PA2" or "9Y0.2" depending on source). Changes include: Taycan-style fully digital 12.6-inch instrument cluster replacing the analogue dial cluster; new dashboard layout with 12.3-inch touchscreen and optional 10.9-inch passenger display; Matrix LED headlamps standard with optional HD Matrix LED units (>32,000 pixels per side); revised front and rear bumpers, hood, headlights, and tailgate. The mid-cycle facelift also brought significant powertrain revisions: the base V6 grew from 335 hp to 348 hp; the Cayenne S returned to a twin-turbo 4.0-litre V8 (468 hp) for MY2024, replacing the prior 2.9-litre twin-turbo V6 in S trim; the E-Hybrid received a more powerful 174 hp electric motor and a larger 25.9 kWh battery (gross), extending WLTP electric range to ~90 km; and a new Cayenne S E-Hybrid trim was added for MY2024 (V6 + larger PHEV system, 512 hp total). The Cayenne Turbo trim was discontinued in favour of the Cayenne Turbo E-Hybrid for the PA2, with the latter producing 729 hp combined.

MY2025 added the Cayenne GTS / GTS Coupe to the PA2 facelift family with a 493 hp version of the 4.0-litre V8 — up from the pre-facelift GTS's 453 hp — plus revised damper calibration and the Sport Chrono-tuned chassis settings.

The Cayenne Turbo GT, launched June 2021 for MY2022, is the highest-performance ICE Cayenne ever produced. It uses a 631 hp version of the 4.0-litre V8, is available only in the Coupe body, and is available only with all-wheel drive and PDK-equivalent eight-speed Tiptronic S transmission. It is 17 mm lower than the standard Cayenne Turbo Coupe with stiffer three-chamber air suspension, ceramic-composite brakes (440 mm front / 410 mm rear), and bespoke 22-inch Pirelli P Zero Corsa tires. 0–60 mph in 3.1 seconds (Porsche claim) or 2.8 seconds (Car and Driver test). The Turbo GT continues into the PA2 era as the performance halo of the 9Y0 lineup. Worldwide MSRP was $180,800 at 2022 US launch.

#### Variant Differentiation Notes — Cayenne

The two body styles, SUV and Coupe, share platform and powertrains but are distinct vehicles for buyer purposes. The Coupe trades 30–40 mm of headroom, slightly less rear-seat room, and slightly less cargo capacity for a more rakish silhouette. The optional carbon-fibre roof on the Coupe drops weight and lowers the centre of gravity by approximately 4–6 mm versus the standard glass roof. Coupe pricing typically runs $5,000–$8,000 above the equivalent SUV in US MSRP.

The Turbo GT is Coupe-only by design. The S E-Hybrid (PA2 onward) is offered in both bodies. Sport Chrono Plus is standard on GTS, Turbo, Turbo GT, and Turbo E-Hybrid; optional elsewhere. PCCB is optional on GTS and standard on Turbo GT.

The PA2 facelift's most consequential mechanical change is the return of the 4.0-litre V8 to the S trim. From the 9Y0's 2018 launch through MY2023, the S used a 2.9-litre twin-turbo V6 (the same engine family as the 992.1 Targa 4 GTS, in a different state of tune); the PA2 returns the S to V8 power, restoring an eight-cylinder S Cayenne for the first time since the 957 generation ended in 2010. This is widely viewed by enthusiasts as a meaningful change for valuation.

The Cayenne Turbo E-Hybrid (PA2) replaces the pure-ICE Cayenne Turbo from the pre-facelift era. The latter (541 hp, V8 twin-turbo, no hybrid) is no longer available; if a buyer wants a non-hybrid V8 Cayenne in the PA2 era, they must buy the GTS (493 hp) or the Turbo GT (631 hp, Coupe only). This is a deliberate Porsche move to consolidate the V8 trims around hybrid powertrains.

#### Sub-Generation Splits — Cayenne

Treat the 9Y0 pre-facelift (MY2020–MY2023) and 9Y0 PA2 (MY2024–present) as the relevant split. Pre-facelift uses the analogue centre dial cluster, the 2.9 L V6 in the S, and the 14.1 kWh battery in the E-Hybrid. PA2 uses the digital instrument cluster, the 4.0 L V8 in the S, the 25.9 kWh battery in the E-Hybrid (and the new S E-Hybrid tier), and the 729 hp Turbo E-Hybrid replacing the pure-ICE Turbo. The Turbo GT continues across both sub-generations with no significant mechanical change.

The all-electric Cayenne Electric, launched in late Q1 2026 / early Q2 2026 on a new dedicated platform (separate from the 9Y0 MLB Evo), is technically a separate vehicle line and is not covered in detail here. It uses a different platform, different drivetrains (dual electric motors, multi-motor in higher trims), and a much larger battery. Industry reports note the Cayenne Turbo Electric produces over 1,100 hp, making it the most powerful Cayenne ever produced. As of April 2026, full Cayenne Electric production figures and detailed spec disclosure are still being released by Porsche; this trim is at the boundary of this file's scope and will be expanded in a future revision once the launch is fully complete.

### Macan ICE (95B, 2020–MY2026)

#### Generation Overview

The first-generation Macan launched in 2014 on the Volkswagen Group MLB platform shared with the Audi Q5. It received a major facelift in 2018/MY2019 (the "95B II" or "facelift" generation, often referred to internally as the second iteration of the 95B). For the 2020s portion in scope, MY2020 through MY2026 covers the late life of this single platform generation, which has been on sale for over a decade.

The 2020s ICE Macan portfolio is the simplest in this file: Macan, Macan S, Macan T (added MY2023), Macan GTS, Macan Turbo. The Macan Turbo was discontinued in many markets ahead of the all-electric Macan Turbo Electric launch; in the US it remained available through the late 2010s 95B II refresh but was effectively phased out for the 2020s portion. The MY2022 update brought additional standard equipment, revised front and rear styling, and a power bump on the Macan S (now using the 2.9-litre twin-turbocharged V6 from the Cayenne S of that period, producing 375 hp) and Macan GTS (also 2.9 L twin-turbo V6, producing 434 hp). The base Macan continued with the 2.0-litre turbocharged inline-four (~261 hp) shared with the Audi Q5 and other VW Group MLB-platform vehicles.

The Macan T, launched MY2023, is positioned similarly to the 911 Carrera T and 718 T: lighter weight focus, sportier suspension setup, the base 2.0 L inline-four engine, and trim aimed at driving engagement rather than absolute power. Pricing slots between Macan S and base Macan.

ICE Macan production is scheduled to end in summer 2026 according to Porsche CFO Jochen Breckner's Q3 2025 and Q1 2026 earnings call statements. The official end date will be approximately mid-2026 at the Leipzig assembly plant. Inventory will continue selling into 2027 in some non-EU markets as Porsche stockpiles vehicles ahead of the production end. EU markets stopped selling ICE Macan in mid-2024 due to the same cybersecurity regulations that affected the 718. The replacement combustion-engine Cayenne-segment-below SUV is not expected until 2028 and will use a different name and a different platform (the Premium Platform Combustion / PPC platform, related to the Audi Q5's underpinnings).

This is significant for valuation: between mid-2026 (ICE Macan production end) and approximately 2028 (ICE successor launch), Porsche will have no compact ICE crossover. The Macan EV (separate vehicle line, see next section) continues during that gap.

### Macan EV (PPE platform, MY2024–present)

#### Generation Overview

The Macan Electric is the second all-electric Porsche road car after the Taycan, and the first Porsche to use the Premium Platform Electric (PPE). PPE is co-developed with Audi (the Audi Q6 e-tron uses the same architecture) and is a clean-sheet 800-volt skateboard platform designed for medium-size luxury EVs. The Macan EV is a separate vehicle from the ICE Macan: different platform, different dimensions, different styling, different infotainment, sharing only the model name. It launched in 2024 for MY2024.

The PPE 800-volt architecture allows DC fast charging at up to 270 kW, achieving 10–80% in approximately 21 minutes under ideal conditions. The 100 kWh battery (95 kWh usable) sits in the floor between the axles and consists of 12 modules of Lithium-Nickel-Manganese-Cobalt (NMC) cells.

The Macan EV launched with two trims: Macan 4 (dual-motor AWD, 402 hp with launch control) and Macan Turbo (dual-motor AWD, 630 hp with launch control). MY2025 added the rear-wheel-drive Macan (single rear motor, 355 hp) and the Macan 4S (dual-motor AWD, 509 hp with launch control). MY2026 added the Macan GTS (dual-motor AWD, 563 hp with launch control), revealed October 2025 in Shanghai. The GTS uses the same large rear motor as the Turbo (210 mm rotor, 230 mm active length, 900 amp silicon-carbide pulse inverter) but with different software calibration and a 9.0:1 transmission ratio rated for higher loads. The GTS also gets sport air suspension lowered 10 mm and Porsche Torque Vectoring Plus (PTV+) with electronically-controlled limited-slip rear differential.

US MSRP at launch (MY2024) was $80,450 for the Macan 4 and $106,950 for the Macan Turbo. MY2025 expanded the entry point: base RWD Macan from $77,295 and Macan 4S from $86,895. The Macan GTS starts at approximately $171,100 in international markets per Porsche Australia disclosure; US pricing is comparable.

EPA range varies by trim and tire/wheel selection: Macan (RWD) ~315 miles, Macan 4 ~308 miles, Macan 4S unknown EPA but expected ~280–300 miles, Macan Turbo 288 miles, Macan GTS not yet finalized but likely ~280 miles. Real-world testing (Edmunds EV Range Test on the Macan 4) recorded 352 miles at 29 kWh/100 mi, indicating the EPA figures are conservative.

The Macan EV uses single-speed transmissions front and rear (in dual-motor variants); there is no two-speed gearbox like the Taycan rear axle. Top speed is 161 mph (Turbo) without the need for the Taycan's 2-speed.

Porsche has confirmed the Macan EV will continue in production beyond the ICE Macan's mid-2026 production end, with deliveries continuing throughout the next-generation ICE-Macan replacement gap (2026–2028).

#### Variant Differentiation Notes — Macan EV

All Macan EV trims share the same 100 kWh battery (95 kWh usable). Differentiation is by motor count, motor specification, software calibration, and chassis tuning. The base RWD Macan uses a single 250 kW (335 hp continuous) rear motor with a 480-amp silicon-carbide pulse inverter; total output is 265 kW (355 hp) with overboost. The Macan 4 adds a front motor (160 mm diameter), bringing total system output to 300 kW (402 hp) with overboost and 0-60 mph in approximately 4.8 seconds (per Edmunds testing). The Macan 4S replaces the 4's rear motor with a more powerful unit and uses a 600-amp pulse inverter; total system output is 380 kW (509 hp) overboost. The Macan Turbo gets the largest rear motor (230 mm rotor, 210 mm active length, 900-amp inverter) producing 630 hp with overboost. The Macan GTS uses the same hardware as the Turbo with different calibration, producing 563 hp.

The optional rear-wheel-steering system (up to 5°) is a meaningful chassis upgrade and is available on all Macan EV trims; it is standard on the GTS. Sport air suspension with 10 mm lower ride height is standard on the GTS and is optional on other trims.

The Macan EV does not offer a one-pedal driving mode like many EVs. Porsche has explicitly said this is a deliberate decision: regenerative braking is managed via the brake pedal, with up to 240 kW of regen power available depending on battery state of charge.

The Macan EV's 800-volt architecture and PPE platform underpinnings position it as a sister vehicle to the Audi Q6 e-tron in product-planning terms, although the two cars are styled and tuned differently. Within Porsche's lineup, it sits between the Taycan (sport sedan, J1 platform) and the future Cayenne Electric (separate dedicated EV platform) as the company's volume EV proposition.

#### Sub-Generation Splits — Macan EV

### Panamera (971, 2020–MY2023 portion)

#### Generation Overview

The second-generation Panamera (971) launched in 2017 and is covered through its MY2020–MY2023 portion in this file (the original 2017–2019 launch period is covered in the 2010s file). The 971 is built on the Volkswagen Group MSB platform and is produced at Porsche's Leipzig plant. It is offered in two body styles: standard four-door fastback sedan and the Sport Turismo wagon-style estate. Long-wheelbase Executive variants (with 150 mm extended wheelbase, intended primarily for chauffeured rear-seat use) are offered on the Turbo, Turbo S, and Turbo S E-Hybrid.

The 971.2 facelift was revealed in 2020 for MY2021. Changes include revised front and rear bumpers, slightly tweaked headlights and taillights, a power increase across most trims, and a larger battery for E-Hybrid PHEV variants. Transmission across all 971 variants is the eight-speed PDK (referred to in some markets as Tiptronic S).

### Panamera (972, MY2024–present)

#### Generation Overview

The third-generation Panamera, internally designated 972, launched in November 2023 and entered production for MY2024. It is built on a substantially revised platform shared with the future updates of the same MSB family. Production continues at Porsche's Leipzig plant alongside the Macan EV and the all-new Cayenne Electric.

The 972's headline features are: a redesigned cabin with the Porsche Driver Experience layout (curved digital cluster, optional passenger display, fully digital instruments — same architectural language as the 992.2 and PA2 Cayenne); HD Matrix LED headlights as standard; standard adaptive air suspension (two-valve, dual-chamber) on all trims; an optional Porsche Active Ride chassis system that uses electro-hydraulic dampers individually controlled per wheel to suppress body roll, pitch, and squat; significantly upgraded PHEV systems with a 25.9 kWh battery (gross) supporting up to 88 km / 55 miles WLTP electric range; an 11 kW on-board AC charger reducing charging time to 2.5 hours under ideal conditions.

The 972 is sedan-only. The Sport Turismo wagon body is discontinued. The Executive long-wheelbase variant is offered on a market-specific basis (primarily in China and the Middle East) on the Turbo S E-Hybrid trim.

The 972 lineup expanded through 2024:
- November 2023 reveal: Panamera, Panamera 4, Panamera Turbo E-Hybrid (launch trims)
- February 2024: Panamera 4 E-Hybrid, Panamera 4S E-Hybrid added
- July 2024: Panamera GTS, Panamera Turbo S E-Hybrid added (top trims)

The Panamera 4S (non-hybrid, V6 twin-turbo) was added later in MY2025 in select markets, slotting between Panamera 4 and the GTS.

The most powerful 972 trim, the Panamera Turbo S E-Hybrid, produces 771 hp combined system output (591 hp from the 4.0 L V8 twin-turbo + 187 hp from the electric motor integrated into the PDK transmission). This is significantly more than the 971.2 Turbo S E-Hybrid's 690 hp combined. The Turbo S E-Hybrid set a Nürburgring Nordschleife class record in July 2024 of 7:24.17 minutes for executive-class luxury sedans, breaking the previous record (held by the 971 Panamera Turbo S) by 3.62 seconds.

### Taycan (J1 platform, MY2020–present)

#### Generation Overview

The Taycan is Porsche's first all-electric production car, launched in late 2019 for MY2020. It is built on the J1 platform, which is shared with the Audi e-tron GT but is otherwise specific to Porsche. The Taycan is produced at the dedicated Zuffenhausen carbon-neutral plant. It is the first 800-volt production EV from any manufacturer, supporting DC fast charging at up to 270 kW (pre-facelift) or 320 kW (facelift).

The launch lineup (MY2020) included Taycan 4S, Taycan Turbo, and Taycan Turbo S — three AWD dual-motor trims using the Performance Battery (79 kWh gross) or the optional Performance Battery Plus (93 kWh gross). The Taycan rear-wheel-drive base model was added later (MY2021 in some markets) to reduce the entry price.

The Cross Turismo body launched in MY2021 — a wagon-style variant with raised ride height, body cladding, and optional all-terrain tires positioning it as a soft-roader sport wagon. Cross Turismo trims: Taycan 4 Cross Turismo (added MY2022), 4S Cross Turismo, Turbo Cross Turismo, and Turbo S Cross Turismo. The Sport Turismo (lower ride height, no body cladding) was added in MY2022 in select markets, followed by the GTS Sport Turismo. The Taycan GTS / GTS Sport Turismo (MY2022) sat between the 4S and the Turbo with 590 hp.

The Taycan facelift was revealed February 2024 for MY2025 with substantial mechanical upgrades:
- Larger batteries: Performance Battery 79→89 kWh gross (82.3 kWh usable), Performance Battery Plus 93→105 kWh gross (97 kWh usable)
- Improved cell chemistry and energy density
- New, lighter rear traction motor (the same unit used in the Macan EV)
- Higher charging power: 270→320 kW DC peak; 150 kW DC/DC converter (for 400V CCS chargers) standard
- Maximum regen braking power: 290→400 kW
- Across-the-range power increases: Taycan base from 321/376 hp to 402/429 hp (with PB+); Turbo S from 750 hp to 939 hp peak (Sport Plus + Launch Control overboost), making the Turbo S itself significantly more powerful than the pre-facelift Turbo GT could deliver in non-overboost mode
- Standard active air suspension across the range
- Revised front fascia with new Matrix LED headlights, slightly reprofiled bumper, illuminating Porsche script under the rear light bar
- Apple CarPlay+ integration
- Refreshed interior with new upholstery options matching the 972 Panamera

The Taycan Turbo GT and Taycan Turbo GT with Weissach Package were introduced in March 2024 alongside the facelift. The Turbo GT uses the same dual-motor AWD layout as the Turbo S but with an upgraded rear motor (900 amp pulse inverter versus 600 amp on the Turbo / Turbo S) producing 760 kW (1019 hp) with overboost / launch control, and 580 kW (780 hp) continuous. The Taycan Turbo GT also features the new Attack Mode — a 120 kW (160 hp) overboost button accessed via the steering wheel, similar to the boost feature on Porsche's 99X Formula E race car. Top speed: Turbo GT 290 km/h (180 mph), Turbo GT with Weissach 305 km/h (190 mph). 0-60 mph: 2.3 s (Turbo GT) / 2.2 s (Weissach Package). The Weissach Package eliminates the rear seats (replaced by a carbon-fibre helmet rack), uses thinner glass, removes one charge port door's electric mechanism, and adds a fixed carbon-weave rear wing in place of the standard adaptive spoiler. Weight savings: ~75 kg compared to the Turbo S. Both Turbo GT variants set Nürburgring Nordschleife class records in 2024 (7:07.55 by Lars Kern for the Turbo GT with Weissach Package — fastest four-door car of any powertrain at the Nordschleife at the time).

The Taycan facelift continues alongside the Turbo GT through MY2026.

## Cross-Generation Notes

### The hybrid program expansion across the 2020s

Porsche's hybrid powertrain strategy evolved markedly through the 2020s and now spans three distinct architectures. The plug-in PHEV E-Hybrid system, introduced in the 2010s on Cayenne (MY2014/2015) and Panamera, was substantially upgraded in the 2020s. The Cayenne PA2 facelift (MY2024) brought a 25.9 kWh gross battery (up from 14.1 kWh pre-facelift), a 174 hp electric motor, and WLTP-cycle electric range of approximately 90 km. The Panamera 972 (MY2024) brought the same 25.9 kWh battery family with comparable range. The most powerful PHEV Porsches in production as of April 2026 are the Cayenne Turbo E-Hybrid (729 hp) and the Panamera Turbo S E-Hybrid (771 hp); both use a 4.0-litre V8 twin-turbo combined with the integrated PDK electric motor.

The non-plug-in performance-hybrid T-Hybrid system was introduced in May 2024 on the 992.2 Carrera GTS and represents a different engineering approach. T-Hybrid is non-plug-in: the 1.9 kWh battery is recharged via brake regeneration and via the eTurbo (which can act as a generator when driving the turbine), and the system is sized for performance enhancement rather than electric-only range. The 992.2 GTS T-Hybrid produces 541 hp from a 3.6-litre 9A3 flat-six paired with a single eTurbo and an electric motor in the PDK. The 992.2 Turbo S T-Hybrid (Munich Auto Show September 2025) extends the system with a second eTurbo, producing 711 PS (701 hp) — the most powerful production 911 ever. The Spirit 70 (April 2025 reveal) is the first Heritage Design 911 on T-Hybrid hardware and uses the GTS Cabriolet's 541 hp T-Hybrid drivetrain.

The two architectures coexist and serve different purposes: E-Hybrid for SUV and luxury sedan applications where buyers want significant electric-only range and tax-incentive eligibility; T-Hybrid for sports car applications where buyers want maximum performance and minimal weight gain. Both architectures share the same VW Group corporate engineering origin but are tuned and packaged for entirely different applications.

A third dimension is the all-electric line. The Taycan (J1) and Macan EV (PPE) are Porsche's two BEV platforms as of April 2026, with the Cayenne Electric (separate dedicated EV platform) launching at the end of Q1 2026 / early Q2 2026 with industry reporting of >1,100 hp on the Cayenne Turbo Electric.

### The all-electric platform expansion: Taycan (J1, 2020) → Macan EV (PPE, 2024)

Porsche's BEV strategy through the 2020s has been incremental rather than wholesale. The Taycan launched on the J1 platform in late 2019/MY2020 — Porsche's first dedicated EV platform, shared with the Audi e-tron GT but otherwise specific to Porsche. The J1 features 800-volt architecture, a two-speed rear gearbox (used in dual-motor Taycans for higher top speed and improved highway efficiency), and was designed for high-performance sport sedan applications. The Macan EV (MY2024) launched on the all-new PPE (Premium Platform Electric) platform, co-developed with Audi (Q6 e-tron). PPE also uses 800-volt architecture but is a true skateboard platform optimized for SUV and crossover applications, with single-speed gearboxes front and rear. The two platforms do not share fundamental architecture, despite both being all-electric Porsches.

The mid-decade Porsche electrification roadmap was revised significantly. The originally planned all-electric 718 successor (originally 2025/2026 launch) has been delayed to 2026/2027, and Porsche has now confirmed (December 2025 announcement) that future fifth-generation 718 Boxster and Cayman models will offer ICE alongside electric variants. Industry reporting suggests this reflects softer-than-expected EV demand in 2024-2025, particularly in North America following the end of the federal $7,500 EV tax credit.

### The 9A3 engine family

The 9A3 flat-six is the most consequential engine event of the 2020s for the 911. It replaces the 9A2 (introduced 2015 on the 991.2 Carrera as a downsized turbocharged successor to the 9A1), which in turn replaced the 9A1 (introduced 2009 on the 997.2 Carrera as a successor to the M96/M97 family that had powered all water-cooled 911s since 1998). The 9A3 is built around T-Hybrid operation: it has redesigned cylinder heads, repositioned intercoolers in the upper engine bay (similar to Turbo and Turbo S layout), new pistons and connecting rods optimized for the eTurbo's response characteristics, and a new exhaust manifold design. Initial displacement is 3.6 litres (3,591 cc) versus the 9A2 Evo's 3.0 litres in the standard Carrera.

The 9A3 currently powers two 911 trims as of April 2026: the Carrera GTS T-Hybrid (single eTurbo, 541 hp) and the Turbo S T-Hybrid (twin eTurbo, 701 hp). Both are paired exclusively with an eight-speed PDK with integrated electric motor; neither offers a manual transmission option. Porsche has positioned the 9A3 as a multi-decade engine family analogous to the 9A1 / 9A2 chronology, suggesting future GTS, Turbo, and possibly GT variants will use this engine family, while the GT-department 4.0-litre NA flat-six (used in the 992.1 GT3, 992.2 GT3, 911 S/T, 718 GT4 RS, and 718 Spyder RS) continues as a separate development line.

### Heritage Design model strategy

Porsche announced the Heritage Design strategy in 2020 alongside the launch of the Targa 4S Heritage Design Edition (992 units, MY2021). The strategy was disclosed as a four-model series spanning the decades from the 1950s through the 1980s, with each model based on a Carrera GTS chassis (within its generation) but using period-specific styling cues, fabrics, badging, and paint colors. Three of the four planned Heritage Design models have been released as of April 2026:

1. **Targa 4S Heritage Design Edition** (MY2021, 992 units): 1950s/early 1960s theme. Cherry Metallic paint (default), corduroy seats and door inserts, period-correct 1963 Porsche crest, gold lettering, white motorsports stripes on front fenders.

2. **Sport Classic** (MY2023, 1,250 units): 1960s/early 1970s theme. Sport Grey Metallic paint (default), based on 992.1 Turbo wide-body chassis with manual + RWD configuration, ducktail spoiler, double-bubble carbon roof, Pepita houndstooth fabric, paldao wood trim, gold "911" badging.

3. **Spirit 70** (MY2026, 1,500 units): 1970s/early 1980s theme. Olive Neo paint (default), based on 992.2 Carrera GTS Cabriolet T-Hybrid, Bronzite (grey-gold) accents on lower body and wheels, Pasha pattern interior fabric, three-stripe hood graphics, "70" side decals.

A fourth Heritage Design model — themed around the 1980s — has been alluded to in Porsche corporate communications but not yet announced as of April 2026. The Heritage Design strategy is positioned as the "lifestyle" dimension of Porsche's product strategy, distinct from GT-department performance variants.

- 911 Turbo 50 Years: 1,974 units (MY2024)
- 911 Spirit 70 (992.2 Cabriolet T-Hybrid): 1,500 units (MY2026)
- Numerous regional editions (Pfaff, Belgian Legend, Ben Pon Jr, Embraer Duet, Sally Special, 50 Years Porsche of Taiwan, Carrera T Club Coupe) ranging from 1 to ~115 units each

The total numbered-allocation production across the 992 platform's first half-decade exceeds 12,000 units across these editions. This cadence is unprecedented in 911 history at this density and is widely viewed by collectors as a deliberate strategy by Porsche to broaden the GT-car-collector audience and to monetize the Exclusive Manufaktur capacity. Porsche has publicly stated it intends to expand Exclusive Manufaktur output further through the late 2020s.

For valuation purposes, distinguish:
- Heritage Design models (Targa Heritage, Sport Classic, Spirit 70 — designed as future collector items)
- GT-derived limited editions (S/T — GT3 engine but lifestyle positioning)
- Performance limited editions (Dakar — single-purpose, off-road; GT3 RS — track-focused with cap)
- Anniversary editions (Turbo 50 Years, S/T as 60th anniversary)
- Regional editions (US-only, France-only, Taiwan-only — much lower production, often country-specific dealer allocation)

### The 718 NA flat-six return (MY2020) — partial reversal

The 982-generation 718 launched in 2016 with all trims using turbocharged flat-four engines, replacing the previous 981 generation's naturally-aspirated flat-six. This was widely received negatively by the enthusiast community on grounds of sound and character. In MY2020 Porsche introduced the 4.0 L NA flat-six on the GTS 4.0, Boxster Spyder, and Cayman GT4 — engineered by deriving the unit from the 9A2 Evo 3.0 L turbocharged flat-six (used in the 992.1 Carrera) by removing the turbochargers and increasing displacement. The MY2022 Cayman GT4 RS and MY2024 Boxster Spyder RS use a different 4.0 L flat-six, derived from the 992 GT3's engine, with significantly higher output (493 hp versus 414 hp) and 9,000 rpm redline. This restored the NA flat-six character to the top of the 718 lineup but did not return it to the base or S trims, which retained the 2.0 L and 2.5 L flat-four turbocharged engines through the end of ICE production in October 2025.

### ICE 718 production end (October 2025)

The end of ICE 718 production in October 2025 closes the generation of mid-engine ICE Porsches. The 982 was on sale for nine years (2016-2025) — a long lifecycle for a Porsche sports car. The replacement is delayed; originally planned for 2025-2026 launch as a BEV-only successor on the PPE platform, the launch has been pushed to 2026-2027, and Porsche has reversed course (December 2025) to confirm that future 718 generations will offer ICE alongside electric variants. The result is a multi-year gap in the mid-engine sports car segment for Porsche, during which dealer inventory and certified pre-owned 718s will be the only purchase options.

This is a significant valuation event for the 718. Late-MY2024 and MY2025 GT4 RS, Spyder RS, GTS 4.0, and Cayman / Boxster T inventory has firmed in pricing through Q1 2026. The very last-built 982s (October 2025 production) carry symbolic value as the final mid-engine ICE Porsches.

### The 992 GT3 / GT3 Touring / GT3 RS / S/T lineage

All four trims share the same 4.0-litre naturally-aspirated flat-six derived from the engine family established with the 991.2 GT3 RS / GT3 RS 4.0 / Speedster. The 992.1 GT3 / GT3 Touring (MY2022) produce 503 hp, the 992.1 GT3 RS (MY2023) produces 518 hp, the S/T (MY2024) produces 525 hp using the GT3 RS engine, and the 992.2 GT3 / GT3 Touring (MY2025) produce the same 503 hp as the 992.1. Naturally-aspirated 911 production is now confined to GT-department trims; all other 911 variants use turbocharged engines (with hybrid integration on the GTS and Turbo S T-Hybrid trims).

The GT-department engine family runs in parallel to the 9A2/9A3 turbocharged engine family. Both are flat-sixes but they are mechanically distinct and developed by different Porsche engineering groups. The GT-engine is also used (in a different state of tune) in the 718 GT4 RS and 718 Spyder RS, and in the 911 GT3 R race car and the 911 RSR race car. As 911 GT-department production continues through the 992.2 era and into the (rumored) 992.2 GT3 RS, the NA flat-six remains the definitive performance engine of the GT lineup despite the broader transition to hybrids elsewhere in the 911 catalogue.

### COVID-19 supply chain effects (2021–2022)

The COVID-19 pandemic and the subsequent semiconductor supply chain disruption affected MY2021 and MY2022 Porsche production globally. North American allocations were particularly affected because of the increased shipping time and the prioritization of European and Chinese markets where Porsche is closer to the manufacturing footprint. Used-market prices on 992.1 911s, particularly Turbo S and GT3 trims, rose sharply through 2021-2022 with reported dealer markups of $75,000–$150,000 over MSRP for GT3 allocations. The 992.1 GT3 specifically was widely subject to "market adjustment fees" of similar magnitude. As of late 2024 / 2025, this premium has substantially compressed: 2024 average market value on a 992.1 GT3 dropped approximately $40,000 versus 2023 per Classic.com data (rounded; Classic.com reports an average drop of ~$40,000 in 2024).

The 992.1 GT3's elevated worldwide production figure of ~15,670 units (rounded down from 15,667 per the Rennlist community count) — by far the largest 911 GT3 generation ever produced — has been cited as a contributing factor in the value compression. Despite the high production, allocation gates remained tight at most dealers; the production volume and the difficulty of acquisition were not consistent.

### Porsche AG IPO (September 2022)

Porsche AG completed its initial public offering on the Frankfurt Stock Exchange in September 2022. The listing valued Porsche AG at approximately €75 billion at IPO and was the largest German IPO in over two decades. The Volkswagen Group retained majority ownership and the Porsche family-controlled holding company Porsche SE acquired 25% of the voting share capital. Operationally, this reaffirmed Porsche AG's substantial autonomy from VW Group while preserving the corporate ownership structure. From a product strategy perspective, the IPO formalised Porsche's positioning as a separate publicly-traded luxury automotive entity — a move that has since influenced product pricing strategy (price increases on most lines have outpaced inflation through 2023-2026), capital allocation toward Exclusive Manufaktur expansion, and marketing positioning toward direct comparison with Ferrari and Bentley rather than mainstream luxury brands.

### 992 allocation politics

GT car allocation through the 2020s continued the dealer-relationship-gated pattern established in the 2010s. The 992.1 GT3 was widely subject to market adjustment fees of $50,000–$150,000 over MSRP through 2022-2023 at most US dealers; the GT3 RS, Sport Classic, Dakar, S/T, and Spirit 70 were similarly relationship-gated. The Heritage Design models (Targa Heritage, Sport Classic, Spirit 70) and the 911 S/T have all been documented in market reporting as appreciating significantly above MSRP in their first year of production. The 911 Sport Classic specifically traded through 2024-2025 at $400,000–$500,000+ — well above its $272,300 launch MSRP. The Dakar saw similar appreciation, with Classic.com citing a high-water-mark sale of $550,000 on a 2024 Dakar in January 2026.

The 992 GT3 RS is the highest-volume GT3 RS ever produced (estimated cap at ~5,000 units; this is widely cited but not officially confirmed by Porsche), which may temper appreciation versus the more limited 991.2 and 997.2 GT3 RS predecessors. The 992.1 GT3 and Touring at 15,670 units (rounded) is the largest GT3 production total ever and is associated with the value compression noted above.

### Mission X and the Carrera GT successor question

The Mission X concept was revealed June 2023 at the 75th anniversary of Porsche celebration in Stuttgart. It is positioned as a successor to the 918 Spyder (production ended 2015) and the Carrera GT (production ended 2007), filling the Porsche halo hypercar position that has been vacant for over a decade. As an electric concept with claimed 1,500+ hp, 1:1 power-to-weight, and 900-volt architecture, the Mission X was originally targeted for production by 2026.

As of April 2026, the Mission X remains a concept. Porsche has made several public statements through 2024-2026 about the production timeline:
- February 2024 (Oliver Blume): a production decision would be made by end of 2024
- October 2024 (Michael Mauer, Head of Design): "There will be a hypercar in the future" but the launch was being delayed
- March 2026 (CEO Michael Leiters in interview): Porsche is "currently evaluating" a new two-door model above the 911 — could be supercar or hypercar; specifically open to combustion or hybrid powertrains rather than the original BEV concept
- March 2026 (Porsche spokesperson statement to Motor1): "we are currently evaluating various vehicle concepts in the GT and hypercar segment"

The most consistent industry interpretation as of April 2026 is that a Mission X-derived production hypercar is unlikely before approximately 2028-2029 at earliest, and that Porsche is open to making it ICE or hybrid rather than BEV given softening EV demand. The Porsche halo position is currently unfilled by any production hypercar.

### 992 GT2 RS (status as of April 2026)

The 992 GT2 RS has been the subject of consistent spy-shot reporting through 2024-2026 and a leaked dealer order book in February 2025 suggesting a production launch with €450,000 starting price and June 2026 deliveries. Industry consensus is that the GT2 RS will use a 9A3 T-Hybrid drivetrain (twin eTurbo, similar to the Turbo S T-Hybrid) but in higher state of tune (target ~800-850 hp), rear-wheel-drive only, PDK only, with significantly more aggressive aerodynamics than the Turbo S.

As of April 2026, Porsche has not officially confirmed the GT2 RS for production. The 992.2 GT3 RS facelift is reportedly due for reveal in August 2026. The GT2 RS may follow.

For this file: the 992 GT2 RS is documented as cross-reference only and is not given a variant table entry until Porsche officially launches it.

### Manual transmission persistence

Despite the broader move to PDK and hybrid drivetrains, Porsche has retained manual transmission availability on a deliberate set of trims through the 2020s. As of April 2026:

**992.1 manual-available trims (production complete or in run-out):**
- Carrera, Carrera S, Carrera 4, Carrera 4S (7-speed, MY2020-MY2024, no-cost option)
- Carrera T (7-speed, manual standard, MY2023-MY2024)
- Carrera GTS / 4 GTS / Targa 4 GTS (7-speed, no-cost option, MY2022-MY2024)
- GT3 / GT3 Touring (6-speed, no-cost option, MY2022-MY2024)
- Sport Classic (7-speed manual ONLY, MY2023, 1,250 units)
- 911 S/T (6-speed short-ratio manual ONLY, MY2024, 1,963 units)

**992.2 manual-available trims (in production):**
- Carrera T (7-speed manual ONLY, MY2025-present)
- GT3 / GT3 Touring (6-speed, no-cost option, MY2025-present)
- Carrera T Club Coupe (manual ONLY, MY2026)

**718 (982) manual-available trims (production ended October 2025):**
- 718 Boxster / Cayman / S / GTS 4.0 (6-speed, no-cost option)
- 718 Cayman GT4 (6-speed, no-cost option)
- 718 Boxster Spyder (6-speed, no-cost option)
- 718 Style Edition (6-speed, no-cost option)

**Macan, Cayenne, Panamera, Taycan, Macan EV: no manual offered (PDK or single-speed only).**

The trend through the 992.2 has been narrowing manual availability: the Carrera, Carrera S, Carrera 4S, Carrera GTS family, and Targa 4S have all dropped manual transmissions for the 992.2 generation. This makes the 992.1 manual-equipped Carrera, Carrera S, Carrera GTS family, and the 992.1/992.2 Carrera T and GT3 / GT3 Touring the only manual-transmission non-GT 911s in production through the second half of the 2020s.

The 911 S/T (1,963 units) and Sport Classic (1,250 units) are manual-only by design and represent the most recent manual-only 911 trims.
