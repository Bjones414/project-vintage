# Porsche Road Cars: 1960s Reference

## Era Context

The 1960s reshaped Porsche from a single-model boutique into a multi-line sports-car maker, and most of the decisions that defined the company for the rest of the century were taken between 1963 and 1969. The decade opened with the 356 in its B (T5) form — a refined evolution of the 1948 original, still recognisably a war-era design dressed up for an increasingly demanding international market — and closed with three production model lines (911, 912, 914) plus a small population of road-legal homologation specials (904, 911 R) and the discontinuing 356 line phasing out from 1965. Porsche's annual output crossed 10,000 units in 1964 and the 100,000th car overall — a 912 Targa for the Baden-Württemberg police — was delivered in December 1966 [1][7].

Three transitions sit at the centre of the period. The 356 → 911 generational change began with the Frankfurt Motor Show debut of the "901" in September 1963, with series production starting September 1964; the model was renamed 911 in late 1964 after Peugeot asserted French trademark rights over three-digit names with a middle zero [4][6]. Roughly 80–82 cars were built and delivered as 901s before the rebadging, and these are tracked as a distinct sub-cohort by collectors even though Porsche AG counts them as 911s. The 356 continued in 356 C form alongside the 911 through 1965, with a small batch of 1965-model cabriolets assembled for the Dutch Rijkspolitie in early 1966 closing the line [1]. The 912 then bridged the price gap left by the 356's exit, mating the final 356 1.6-litre pushrod flat-four (Type 616/36, 90 PS) to the 911 body shell and the Type 901 transmission [7]. The 914 launched at Frankfurt in September 1969 as a Porsche-Volkswagen joint venture and Porsche's first mid-engine road car, with both four-cylinder and six-cylinder variants [12][13].

The other two big themes are the body-style invention and the start of fuel injection. The Targa appeared for model year 1967 across both 911 and 912, with a fixed stainless-steel-clad roll bar, a removable roof panel, and a flexible plastic ("soft-window") rear screen with a zip-out feature. Porsche introduced a fixed-glass rear window option in January 1968, and the fixed-glass car became the dominant Targa form thereafter; the original soft-window remained a special-order option through 1971 [9][10]. For MY1969, Bosch mechanical fuel injection (MFI) arrived on the hotter 911 trims (911 S and the new 911 E), while the entry-level 911 T retained Solex/Weber carburation through the rest of the F-body run; this split between MFI and carbureted 911s persisted through the early 1970s. The Targa-body invention, the Carrera designation continuing from the 356 Carrera 2 into the 904 Carrera GTS, and the philosophical lineage from the 911 R lightweight (1967–1968) through the 1973 Carrera RS and every subsequent GT-class lightweight, are all 1960s threads that run continuously into the modern era.

By the end of 1969 Porsche of America Corp. (PoAC, founded 1950 by Max Hoffman in New York) had handed over US distribution to a new joint VW-Porsche marketing operation, the 914 was about to ramp into volume production for MY1970, and the 911 had moved from short-wheelbase (SWB, 1965–1968) to long-wheelbase (LWB, 1969+) form — a 57 mm wheelbase increase that is treated as a fundamental sub-generation split in collector valuation. The 1960s file therefore covers the design and architecture decisions that the 1970s file inherits as established facts.

## Per-Model/Generation Sections

### 356 B (1959–1963)

#### Generation Overview

The 356 B was the third major series of the 356 line. Production ran from late 1959 through July 1963, spanning two distinct body sub-generations: T5 (1959–1961, in scope from 1960 onward) and T6 (1961–1963). Chassis-code prefixes follow Porsche's convention for the era; engine codes are Type 616 family pushrod flat-fours, except the 4-cam Carrera variants (Type 692 / 587), which are a distinct engine family derived from the Fuhrmann racing 4-cam.

Key engineering points:
- Air-cooled, rear-mounted, rear-wheel-drive flat-four
- Body by Reutter (Coupe, Cabriolet, Roadster) and Karmann (Hardtop Coupe / Notchback, plus T6 Coupe sub-volume from 1962)
- Drum brakes all four corners (until 356 C in 1963)
- Type 741 four-speed manual synchromesh gearbox standard; no factory automatic
- Carrera 2 (from 1962) used the Type 587/1 4-cam flat-four, an engineering family unrelated to the pushrod 1.6-litre engines

T5 vs T6 (sub-generation split): The T5 (1959–1961) had the original lower beltline with a smaller wraparound rear window. The T6 (introduced for MY1962) raised the windshield and rear window, added twin grilles to the engine lid, moved the fuel filler from inside the front trunk to an external flap on the right front fender/wing, and made minor interior trim revisions [1][3]. Carrera 2 launch (September 1962) coincided with the T6 introduction; all Carrera 2s use the T6 body.

#### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 356 B's variant set is broad because Porsche, in the Reutter and Karmann coachwork era, ran multiple body suppliers and trim levels in parallel. The single most consequential variant distinction is the Carrera 2 versus the pushrod cars: Carrera 2s (Type 587/1 4-cam, ~130 hp) use a fundamentally different engine family from the 1.6-litre pushrod 356s, and matching-numbers verification on a Carrera 2 carries a price implication on the order of 5–10x over an equivalent body-style pushrod 356 in the modern collector market. The Karmann-bodied Hardtop Coupe (Notchback) carries a small coachwork premium today; the T5 Notchback (1961, Cabriolet body with welded hardtop) and T6 Notchback (1962, structurally distinct body) are visually similar but mechanically and structurally different. Roadsters (1960–1962, replacing the Convertible D as the lower-windscreen open variant) command higher prices than Cabriolets in the same engine spec because of their lower production volume and Speedster-adjacent styling. The S90 trim (Solex 40 PII-4, 9.0:1 pistons) commands a modest premium over the 1600 and 1600 S and is mechanically the most demanding of the pushrod 1.6 variants.

#### Sub-Generation Splits

T5 (1959–1961, in scope from 1960) vs T6 (1961–1963): As noted above, the T6 raised the headlamp position, raised and enlarged the windshield and rear window, added twin engine-lid grilles, and moved the fuel filler outboard. The T5 is now considered the more visually delicate body and often commands a small premium over comparable T6 cars on the basis of styling alone. Mechanically, the T5 and T6 use the same engines and transmissions; the differences are purely body and trim until the 356 C disc-brake update.

### 356 C (1963–1965)

#### Generation Overview

The 356 C was the final 356 series, in production from July 1963 to September 1965 (with a final ten Cabriolets assembled in March 1966 for the Dutch Rijkspolitie as 1965 models) [1]. The major mechanical update was four-wheel disc brakes (ATE) replacing drums, plus revised engine designations (1600 C, 1600 SC, 2000 GS Carrera 2). The 60 hp pushrod engine was discontinued. The 1600 C (Type 616/15, 75 hp DIN) became the new base engine, replacing what had been the Super in 356 B; the Super 90 became the SC (Type 616/16, 95 hp DIN), which is the most powerful pushrod 4-cylinder Porsche ever produced [10]. The Carrera 2 continued in 356 C form through 1964.

Body styles: Coupe and Cabriolet only (the Roadster and Notchback Hardtop ended with 356 B). Coupe production was split between Reutter and Karmann; Cabriolets were Reutter through the 1963 Reutter acquisition by Porsche, after which the seat division became Recaro.

Engineering points:
- Disc brakes all around (ATE)
- Type 616/15 (1600 C, 75 hp), 616/16 (1600 SC, 95 hp), 587/1 (Carrera 2, 130 hp) engine choices
- Type 741 four-speed gearbox carried over
- Pirelli Cinturato radial tyres standard

#### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 356 C variant set is the simplest of the 356 generations because of the engine consolidation. The single most valuable distinction is again Carrera 2 vs pushrod (the C-series Carrera 2 is among the rarest 356 cars, with ~126 units across body styles); within the pushrod cars, the SC commands a clear premium over the C, traceable to its higher horsepower and twin-Solex-carb top-end as well as its rarity in late 1965 production. Cabriolets command a substantial premium over Coupes (in some condition tiers, multiples). Karmann-bodied Coupes carry a small coachwork-source distinction that some collectors track but the market treats roughly equivalently to Reutter Coupes. The 1965 calendar-year cars in particular have become a sought-after subset because they are mechanically the most refined and chronologically the rarest within the 356 C series.

#### Sub-Generation Splits

356 B (T6) → 356 C (1963): The visible exterior changes are minimal — bumper trim, vent placement adjustments, and badging — so distinguishing a late T6 356 B from an early 356 C visually requires close inspection. Mechanically, the disc brake change is the major identification point. Engine designation changes (Super → C, Super 90 → SC) and the discontinuation of the 60 hp Normal engine simplified the trim ladder considerably.

### 911 / 901 launch generation (F-body, 1964–1969)

#### Generation Overview

The 911 was Porsche's first all-new car since the 356, presented at the September 1963 Frankfurt Motor Show as the "901" with a non-functional engine mockup, given a working Type 901 flat-six in February 1964, and entering series production in September 1964 [4][6]. The F-body designation refers to the body shell that ran (with revisions) from 1964 through the 1973 model year; the 1964–1969 portion in this file covers the original 2.0-litre engine generation in three sub-series (O, A, B) and the SWB-to-LWB transition.

Key engineering points:
- 2.0 L (1,991 cc) air-cooled flat-six, dry-sump, SOHC per bank, all-aluminium-alloy
- Type 901/01 base engine (130 PS DIN at launch); successive 901/0X type numbers per trim and emissions specification
- Type 901 five-speed manual transmission standard from launch (a four-speed Type 901 was offered on 911 T from MY1968)
- Sportomatic semi-automatic (4-speed with torque converter and vacuum-actuated clutch) optional from MY1968 across most trims; type numbers gain "07," "08," "13," etc. variations
- Body shell unitary steel; styling by Ferdinand "Butzi" Porsche, body engineering by Erwin Komenda
- Wheelbase 2,211 mm (SWB, 1964–1968) → 2,268 mm (LWB, MY1969 onward)

Sub-generation splits within the 1960s portion:

- O-series (Aug 1964 – Jul 1967): SWB; original Solex 40PI carbs replaced by Weber 40 IDA3C in February 1966; engine type 901/01 → 901/05 with the carb change. 911 S (160 PS, Type 901/02) added MY1967. Targa body added MY1967 (December 1966 production start) with soft-window rear screen.
- A-series (Aug 1967 – Aug 1968, MY1968): SWB; trim ladder expanded to 911 T (110 PS, base, Europe), 911 (130 PS mid, Europe and US), 911 L (130 PS top in Europe; US-market top trim, "Lux"), 911 S (160 PS, Europe — not US-legal due to emissions). Targa fixed-glass option from January 1968. Dual-circuit brakes standard. Sportomatic introduced. Engine case material changed from aluminum to magnesium during the series. Engine cases gained 6V to 12V electrical change for some markets.
- B-series (Aug 1968 – Jul 1969, MY1969): LWB (+57 mm wheelbase). 911 L discontinued; 911 E launched as the new mid-trim with Bosch mechanical fuel injection (140 PS, Type 901/09). 911 S also gains MFI (170 PS, Type 901/10). 911 T retained Solex/Weber carburation (110 PS, Type 901/03 / 901/13 Sportomatic). Wider 5.5J wheels standard, magnesium crankcase standard, oil-filler position revised slightly. The B-series is the only LWB 2.0-litre 911 — for MY1970 the engine grew to 2.2 L and the F-body moved to its C-series.

The 911 R (1967–1968) is treated as a separate variant block below.

#### Variant Differentiation Notes

The single most valuation-relevant distinction in the 1960s F-body lineup is SWB vs LWB (1965–1968 vs 1969+). SWB cars are smaller in wheelbase (2,211 mm) and have a reputation for nervous oversteer at the limit; the LWB cars (2,268 mm) are far more stable and represent the form that became the 911's long-running template. Within SWB, the soft-window Targa is a meaningful sub-cohort (MY1967 + ~half of MY1968) — original soft-window cars are increasingly sought after both because they were rare to begin with (Porsche introduced fixed-glass as an option in January 1968 and most customers chose it) and because many were converted to fixed-glass over the decades.

The 1966 Solex-to-Weber carburetor change in February 1966 is another sub-cohort marker. Original Solex-equipped cars are valued by collectors who care about pure originality, but the conversion to Weber carbs was performed by the factory after February 1966 and by independent service shops afterward, so original Solex-spec cars are uncommon today. The MY1969 transition from carbureted (911 T) to MFI (911 E and 911 S) is the start of the fuel-injection era at Porsche and a meaningful technology marker in itself.

The 1968 911 L is rare and unusual: a single-model-year US-market top trim (Europe also got the L but as one of four trims rather than the top), with a production volume of roughly 1,610 across Coupe and Targa (sources vary on the Targa split). The 1968 911 L is a stand-out collector trim today because of its single-MY status and its short production window. Among 1969 cars, the 911 E in MFI form is a relatively rare mid-trim (~2,820 across Coupe and Targa) and is increasingly recognised as a sweet spot for early LWB 911 buyers.

The 1965 calendar-year 911 (the very earliest cars, including those near the end of the 901-badging period) is a particularly small subset of the SWB run. Of the ~3,390 cars built in 1965, only about half (the cars built between January and July 1965) carried the original-build "MY1965" calendar-year designation that registry tracking now uses to mark the formative-year cars [114]. The very earliest cars — the 1964 calendar-year ~230-unit production block, including the ~80 cars badged "901" before the rename — are a sub-cohort tracked separately by collectors under "earliest 911" or "901" designations.

#### Sub-Generation Splits

The full SWB → LWB transition deserves its own note: the wheelbase increase of 57 mm (2,211 to 2,268 mm) was achieved by relocating the rear wheels rearward without lengthening the body; the wheel arches were flared to accommodate wider 5.5J wheels and 185-section tires (vs 4.5J / 165-section before), and the magnesium crankcase introduced on the B-series (replacing the aluminum case used through the A-series) is a parallel mechanical change. The LWB B-series is therefore distinguishable from a SWB A-series both visually (flared arches, oil filler position changes on later LWB cars) and mechanically (magnesium case, MFI on E/S trims).

The Targa soft-window vs fixed-glass split is a meaningful 1960s-era valuation differentiator. The soft-window Targa (1967 through about half of MY1968 production, plus special-order continuation through 1971) has a flexible plastic rear screen with a zip-out lower edge, and the rear cabin lacks a fixed glass surface; the fixed-glass Targa (introduced January 1968 as Targa "Version II" by Porsche) has a conventional fixed glass rear window and is structurally closer to a conventional Coupe with removable roof. Of the ~720 first-year MY1967 Targas, all are soft-window cars; through MY1968 the soft-window remained standard but fixed-glass was widely optioned, and by MY1969 fixed-glass had become the volume choice. Period US DOT changes around rear-seat homologation also influenced the choice for US-spec cars.

### 911 R (1967–1968)

#### Generation Overview

The 911 R was a factory lightweight homologation-targeted special, conceived under Ferdinand Piëch with the goal of qualifying a 911-based car for the FIA's Group 4 / GT 2.0 class. Porsche did not build the 500 units required for homologation — sales department concluded that few buyers would pay 45,000 DM for what was effectively a stripped-out road-registered race car — and the program ended after four prototypes and 20 numbered customer cars [54][55][57][59].

Engineering points:
- Steel unibody retained; bonnet, doors, front fenders, engine cover, and bumpers all replaced by glass-fibre reinforced polymer (GFRP) panels (manufacturer: Karl Baur of Stuttgart) [53]
- Plexiglas side and rear windows (2 mm); 4 mm thin glass windscreen; aluminum doors on R3 prototype only
- Magnesium crankcase, twin-spark cylinder heads, titanium connecting rods, Weber 46 IDA3C carburetors
- Type 901/22 race engine (derived from 906 / Carrera 6 race car), 210 hp at 8,000 rpm; some cars later received Bosch MFI in Type 901/21 form (220 hp)
- Curb weight ~810 kg (vs ~1,030 kg standard 911 S) — approximately 220 kg / 500 lb lighter
- Type 901 5-speed manual; one R3 prototype built with Sportomatic for the 1967 Marathon de la Route win

The 911 R never received FIA homologation as a GT car and was relegated to the Prototype class in international racing, where it nonetheless took the 1967 Marathon de la Route (84-hour Nürburgring) overall win and the 1969 Tour de France with Gérard Larrousse driving — the last Porsche road-car-derived overall win at a major rally event prior to the dominance of the dedicated 911 racing programs in the 1970s.

#### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 911 R is, in 1960s Porsche terms, the philosophical ancestor of the entire RS / GT3 RS / 911 R modern lineage. It is mechanically and structurally distinct from any other 911 of the period — only the steel unibody and certain suspension and drivetrain components are shared with the standard 911 — and its 24-unit production figure makes it among the rarest Porsche road-related cars ever built. None of the 24 cars were imported to the US through PoAC; eight of the 20 customer cars went to France via Sonauto (per RM Sotheby's chassis-by-chassis cataloging) [59]. Authentication of an 911 R relies on chassis numbers in the 11899001R–11899020R range (or the four prototype chassis numbers) plus original engine and registry-tracked panel set.

### 912 (1965–1969)

#### Generation Overview

The 912 was Porsche's entry-level model from April 1965, replacing the 356 in the price-tier left vacant by the 911's substantially higher price [7]. Mechanically, the 912 is a 911 body with the final 356 1.6-litre pushrod flat-four (Type 616/36, 90 PS DIN, slightly detuned from the 356 SC's 95 hp). The Type 901 transmission (4-speed standard, 5-speed optional from launch, 5-speed standard in some markets from MY1968) is shared with the 911. The 912 uses the same body shell as the 911, with subtle visual differentiators (silver-painted "PORSCHE" and "912" badging vs gold-painted on 911s; different wheel choices; single-pipe rather than dual-pipe exhaust).

Engineering points:
- 1.6 L Type 616/36 air-cooled flat-four pushrod, 90 hp DIN at 5,800 rpm
- Type 901 4-speed manual standard; 5-speed optional from launch
- Brakes initially solid discs all around (matching 356 C's late spec); ventilated front discs and dual-circuit master cylinder from MY1968
- Targa body added MY1967 (December 1966 production start), parallel to 911 Targa
- SWB (1965–1968) → LWB (1969) split parallel to 911

Sub-generation splits:

- 1965–1967 (SWB, original 4-speed standard / 5-speed optional)
- 1968 (SWB, US DOT compliance year — dual-circuit brakes, revised door panels, side reflectors, vacuum-advance distributor, dual-circuit master cylinder, larger 420W alternator)
- 1969 (LWB, parallel to 911 B-series; revised dashboard, electric heated rear window on Coupe, larger brakes front and rear, vent slots in Targa bar)

#### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 912 in its 1965–1969 form is mechanically a 4-cylinder pushrod 911 body — a clean and consistent specification through five years of production. Its under-appreciation by collectors through the 1980s and 1990s — when 1960s 911 prices began climbing rapidly while 912 prices stayed nearly flat — has reversed considerably in the 2010s and 2020s. A clean original 912 today commands meaningful prices, especially the Targa (which is significantly rarer than the Coupe at ~2,500 vs ~30,000) and the 1965 calendar-year cars (the very first 912s). The 1969 LWB 912 is also relatively scarce because of the lower model-year volume (4,710 cars), and represents the final 912 in the original 1.6 L form before the model was replaced by the 914-4 for MY1970.

The 912 has the same SWB/LWB split as the 911 (1965–1968 SWB, 1969 LWB), the same Targa soft-window/fixed-glass split (MY1967 cars all soft-window; mixed for MY1968; primarily fixed-glass for MY1969), and the same Reutter/Karmann coachwork-source split (Coupes built at Stuttgart and at the Karmann plant in Osnabrück from 1966 onward; Targas always built at Stuttgart). Karmann-built 912 Coupes are a small differentiator but not a value-relevant distinction in most market tiers.

The 912 should not be confused with the 1976 single-MY US-market 912E (Type 923), which uses a different chassis (911 G-body) and a fundamentally different engine (the 2.0 L VW-Audi Type 4 flat-four from the 914 2.0). The 912E is in the 1970s file and is mechanically and structurally a different car from the 1965–1969 912.

#### Sub-Generation Splits

Soft-window vs fixed-glass Targa (1967–1968 vs 1968–1969) within the 912 line follows the same pattern as the 911 Targa. The 1968 US DOT-related changes (vacuum-advance Bosch distributor, split-shaft Solex carbs, exhaust air pump, dual-circuit master cylinder) make the MY1968 912 mechanically distinguishable from MY1965–1967 cars. The MY1969 LWB 912 has the +57 mm wheelbase, larger brakes, electric heated rear window on Coupe, and a revised dashboard with three-position fan switch — a sub-generation split parallel to the 911 B-series.

### 914 / 914-6 (September 1969 launch; 1969 portion in scope)

#### Generation Overview

The 914 launched at the 1969 Frankfurt Motor Show on 11 September 1969 as a Porsche-Volkswagen joint venture and Porsche's first mid-engine production road car. Two engine variants were offered at launch: the 914-4 (also called 914 1.7) with the 1.7 L flat-four from the VW 411 E (Bosch D-Jetronic electronic injection, 80 hp), and the 914-6 with the Porsche 2.0 L flat-six (Type 901/36, 110 hp) from the 1969 911 T [78][80].

The 914-6 specifically used the 911 T 2.0 L engine derated for the 914 application; it is a 6-cylinder Porsche road car at a price point only slightly below the 911 T (DM 19,980 vs DM 23,199 at launch in Germany), which contributed to its limited commercial success [79]. The 914 chassis was assembled by Karmann at Osnabrück; 914-6 bodies were shipped to Stuttgart for fitment of the 911-derived flat-six, while 914-4 cars were fully assembled at Karmann.

For the 1960s file, the in-scope 1969-portion production is small. Production of the 914-4 began for MY1970 with cars arriving in dealer hands from January 1970; production of the 914-6 began in 1970 as MY1970. Some sources document a small number of 1969 MY badging in European registrations, but the bulk of 914 production lies in the 1970s file [78].

Engineering points:
- Mid-engine layout (between rear axle and seats); 5-speed Type 901-derived manual; transaxle with reverse-rotation drive due to mid-engine packaging
- Karmann-built body shell shared between 914-4 and 914-6
- Targa-style removable roof panel (stowable in rear trunk)
- Pop-up headlights
- 4-wheel disc brakes (914-4 used VW 411-derived brakes; 914-6 used 911-derived brakes)

### 904 Carrera GTS (1964–1965)

#### Generation Overview

The 904 was Porsche's homologation-targeted GT racing car, designed by Ferdinand "Butzi" Porsche to compete in the FIA Group 3 GT class and to satisfy the 100-unit minimum production requirement. The 904 was officially marketed as the "Carrera GTS" — Porsche could not use the "904" designation publicly because of the same Peugeot trademark dispute that forced the 911's rename, although the 904 internal type number was used widely in the press and in collector cataloging [82].

The 904 is mid-engine (the first Porsche street-registered car with this layout), with a steel ladder/box-section frame and a fiberglass body bonded to the frame for rigidity. Body fabrication was outsourced to Heinkel Flugzeugbau, an aircraft manufacturer; this is the first Porsche road-going car with a fiberglass body [81]. Engine: standard Type 587/3 4-cam flat-four (1,966 cc, 180 hp at 7,000 rpm), the final and most developed form of Ernst Fuhrmann's 4-cam Carrera engine family.

Engineering points:
- Tubular steel ladder/box frame (not a unibody)
- Fiberglass body bonded to chassis (drag coefficient 0.34 cd; weight ~640–655 kg race-prepared)
- Mid-engine layout with rear-wheel drive
- Type 587/3 4-cam flat-four engine (most cars); some factory works cars built with 6-cylinder (901/20-derived) or 8-cylinder (Type 771-derived) engines
- 5-speed manual; ZF rack-and-pinion steering shared with 911 development; 4-wheel ATE disc brakes
- Coil springs (first Porsche to abandon trailing-arm front and swing-axle rear); unequal-length A-arms front

The 904 is documented as a road-legal car but is fundamentally a homologation race car with road registration available in many markets. Approximately 106 four-cylinder customer cars were built, plus 6 factory works cars with 911-derived flat-six engines (chassis prefix 906-001, 002, 005, 006, 011, 012; 906-005 destroyed in development testing) and 3 factory works cars with the 8-cylinder Type 771 race engine [81][83]. Some sources cite up to 109 four-cylinder cars due to "recycled" chassis numbers (cars rebuilt and re-issued with the same or near-same number) [86].

#### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 904 is the only road-legal mid-engine production-related Porsche of the 1960s, and it shares essentially nothing with the 911 architecturally. Its valuation today is driven primarily by chassis-number registry tracking and originality verification — original Type 587/3 4-cam engines are difficult and expensive to maintain (some current owners run replacement Capricorn-built race-spec 4-cams to preserve original numbers-matching engines), and many cars have history with multiple replacement engines and panel sets. The factory 904/6 (six-cylinder) and 904/8 (eight-cylinder) cars are not in the customer-car cohort and should be considered separately for any valuation purpose; they are factory-retained race cars with little or no road-legal use. The 904 chassis was also used as the basis for the 904 Bergspyder (1965, hill-climb spyder built on a 904 frame with a Type 771 8-cylinder engine; chassis prefix 906); the Bergspyder is a race-only car and is excluded from this file.

The list price of $7,425 USD FOB Stuttgart (1964) places the 904 above any other Porsche road car of the period — the 1964 911 was $6,500 — making the 904 a deliberate halo car at launch even before considering its race-derived specification. About 106 cars in total were ordered, with build rate of ~1/day in 1964; orders exceeded the 100-unit homologation requirement but Porsche did not extend production further because the 906 successor was already in design [85].

## Cross-Generation Notes

**The 356 → 911 generation transition (1964–1965).** Porsche ran the 356 C and the 911 in parallel for roughly one model year (the 911 in late 1964/early 1965, the 356 C through September 1965, with 10 cars in March 1966 for the Dutch Rijkspolitie). 1964 was the peak production year of the 356 line at ~14,150 cars, the year the 911 debuted at the US market (February 1965), and the year the 911 launched. The strategic pacing — running the older car alongside the newer at full production scale through one model year — was driven by uncertainty about the 911's price acceptance and by the practical question of how quickly the manufacturing line could ramp the new model. By the 1966 calendar year, the 911 had its first full year of substantial production (~3,420 standard plus ~520 S Coupes), and the 912 had launched in April 1965 to absorb the 356's price tier.

**The 901 → 911 name change (1964).** Porsche presented the new car as the "901" at the September 1963 Frankfurt Motor Show. Production began September 1964; before the rename, Porsche built approximately 80–82 cars badged "901" on the deck lid. After Peugeot asserted exclusive French rights to three-digit names with a middle zero, Porsche rebadged the car "911" — adding a 1 — and continued production. Internally, 901 part-number prefixes persisted for years (the engine, transmission, and many body components retained 901 prefixes well into the 1970s). For collectors, an original-build 901-badged car is a meaningful sub-cohort within the earliest 911 production block, although Porsche AG counts these cars as 911s for production-records purposes and the deck-lid badging change is the primary externally-visible distinction. PCA Tech Tips notes "about 80 cars built and labeled 901 before the change", with roughly another 150 cars built before the close of calendar year 1964 [40].

**The Fuhrmann 4-cam engine.** Developed by Ernst Fuhrmann in the early 1950s for race use, the 4-cam family powered the 356 Carrera (1955+), 356 Carrera 2 (1962–1964), and 904 Carrera GTS (1964–1965), with continuous evolution through Type 547, Type 587, and Type 587/3 designations. The engine is a flat-four with shaft-driven double overhead cams per bank (hence "4-cam," referring to the four camshafts total), roller-bearing bottom end (early), and high build complexity. It was expensive to manufacture and to maintain — period accounts cite ~120 hours to assemble one — and ended its production life with the 904 in 1965. The "Carrera" name itself came from the Carrera Panamericana road race; it was first applied to the 356 A 1500 GS Carrera in 1955, continued through the 356 B 1600 GS Carrera GT and 356 B/C 2000 GS Carrera 2, and then carried into the 904 Carrera GTS. After 1965 the Carrera name went dormant on Porsche road cars until it reappeared on the 1973 911 Carrera RS 2.7 — but the 1973 Carrera and all subsequent Carrera-named cars use the 911 flat-six engine family, not the Fuhrmann 4-cam. The 4-cam is therefore a closed engine family at the end of 1965, with no descendants in modern Porsche road cars.

**The 911 R as the prototype "RS" car.** The 911 R (1967–1968, 24 cars) is the philosophical ancestor of the entire Porsche lightweight homologation lineage: the 1973 Carrera RS 2.7, the 1974 Carrera RS 3.0, the 1992 Carrera RS, the GT3 RS series (from 996 onward), the 2016 modern 911 R (991 series, 991 units), and the 2021 911 GT3 with Touring package. The technical themes — fiberglass panels, plexiglass windows, magnesium components, race-derived twin-plug engines, weight reduction taken to the limit — are all 911 R legacies. The 911 R sales-failure-then-cult-status arc is itself a precedent that Porsche has revisited several times (the 1973 Carrera RS, originally projected to sell ~500 units, ended up as a 1,580-unit volume model when demand surprised the company; the 2016 911 R was deliberately made limited at 991 units and instantly commanded multiples of MSRP in the secondary market).

**The 912 as a 911 alternative.** During 1966, the 912 outsold the 911 by roughly 2-to-1 (9,090 vs ~3,720 [68]). Across the 1965–1969 run, 912 production (~32,000) is broadly comparable to 911 production for the same period (~36,500). The 912 was the budget-Porsche niche of the 1960s — the same role the 914 then took for 1970–1976 and the 924 for 1977–1988. The 912's reputation as the "lesser 911" was a fixture of the 1980s and 1990s collector market, but the 1965–1969 912 has been substantially revalued in the 2010s and 2020s, particularly Targas and 1965 calendar-year cars; today a clean 1968–1969 912 Coupe routinely commands prices that would have seemed unthinkable in 1990.

**The Targa body invention (1967).** Porsche's Targa was a Porsche-invented body style, developed in response to anticipated US National Highway Traffic Safety Administration (NHTSA) regulations that some industry observers believed would restrict fully open convertibles. Those regulations did not ultimately materialise in the form Porsche expected, but the Targa was already in production and became Porsche's only open-roof 911 for 18 years (until the 1983 911 SC Cabriolet). The structural element that defines the Targa is the brushed-stainless-steel-clad roll bar, a fixed B-pillar-equivalent structure with a cosmetic stainless cover, plus a removable roof panel and (originally) a flexible plastic rear screen with a zip-out feature. The name comes from the Targa Florio race in Sicily, where Porsche enjoyed extensive success through 1973. For the 911 specifically, the absence of a true Cabriolet through the entire first 18 years of production (1965–1983) is notable: the Targa was the only "open" 911. The same is true of the 912 across its full 1965–1969 run.

**The 914 launch (September 1969).** The 914 was Porsche's first mid-engine production road car, a layout that Porsche had used in race cars since the 550 (1953) and continued in the 904, 906, 907, 908, and so on. The mid-engine architecture transitioned from race cars to road cars with the 914. Commercially, the 914-4 was the volume model and a substantial sales success (over 115,000 cars across the full 1969–1976 run); the 914-6 was a commercial difficulty (around 3,300–3,360 across 1969–1972) because its price overlapped too closely with the 911 T's. Porsche's mid-engine production-car next chapter would be the 924 (front-engine, but mid-mounted-transaxle), the 944, the 968, and eventually the Boxster (1996, mid-engine). The 914 stands as the first chapter in this lineage.

**Bosch MFI (mechanical fuel injection) adoption (1969).** The MFI system from Bosch was first applied to Porsche road cars in 1969 on the 911 S (Type 901/10) and 911 E (Type 901/09). MFI is a high-pressure mechanical pump-and-metering system, distinguishable from later Bosch electronic injection (D-Jetronic 1968, K-Jetronic 1973, L-Jetronic 1973). The MFI cars run on a high-pressure pump driven from the engine; the metering unit is precisely machined and historically expensive to overhaul. MFI continued on the 911 S and 911 E through the 1973 model year (when K-Jetronic CIS replaced it for some markets). The 911 T retained Solex/Weber carburation through 1969 and into 1970–1971 (covered in the 1970s file).

**The Karmann-bodied 356 Hardtop Coupes (1961–1962).** The Karmann Hardtop Coupe ("Notchback") was a relatively short-run variant of the 356 B in two distinct forms: the T5 Notchback (1961, ~1,000–1,050 cars; structurally a Cabriolet body with a welded-in steel hardtop) and the T6 Notchback (1961–1962, ~700 cars; structurally a different body with a proper Coupe windshield frame). Combined production is cited at ~1,750 cars; the two variants are visually similar but mechanically distinct. The Karmann Hardtop Coupes were Porsche's response to customers who ordered Cabriolets with steel hardtops and never raised the soft top — a market signal that a fixed-roof Coupe-Cabriolet hybrid had a niche. Production was discontinued because demand was modest and because Karmann's main 356 work shifted to the Coupe sub-volume.

**The Speedster → Convertible D → Roadster transition.** The 356 Speedster ended in 1958, before this file's 1960–1969 scope. Its successor was the Convertible D (1959 only; "D" for the Drauz coachbuilder), which was a low-windscreen open variant with windup windows, somewhat heavier than the Speedster. The Convertible D was succeeded for 1960 by the 356 B 1600 Roadster (1960–1962; bodied initially by Drauz, later by d'Ieteren of Belgium), which retained the lower-windscreen lighter character but with continued refinement. The Roadster ended in mid-1962 (effectively after the T6 transition); only a small T6-bodied Roadster run was produced in 1962 before the line was discontinued. None of the Speedster/Convertible D/Roadster variants are full Cabriolets — they are lower-windscreen, lighter open-top variants. The conventional Cabriolet (with full windscreen and windup windows) continued in parallel throughout the 1960s portion of 356 production, ending with the final 1965 / March 1966 cars built for the Dutch Rijkspolitie.

**The 1968 US market and emissions regulation.** US emissions regulations introduced for MY1968 forced Porsche to drop the 911 S from the US market for the model year. The 911 L (Lux) was effectively a 1968-only US-market top-trim car — the European 911 L was one of four trims (T, normal 911, L, S), but the US-spec L was the top trim because the S could not pass US emissions. For MY1969 the 911 S returned to the US market with Bosch MFI; the 911 L was discontinued and replaced by the 911 E in the mid-trim slot. The 1968 model year is therefore unusually variant-dense and unusually market-specific in trim availability, and the single-model-year 911 L (~1,610 across body styles) is a meaningful collector cohort.
