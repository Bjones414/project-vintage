# Porsche Road Cars: 1980s Reference

## Era Context

The 1980s was the decade Porsche almost ditched its identity and didn't. At the start of 1980, the company line under chairman Ernst Fuhrmann was that the rear-engined air-cooled 911 had run its course and that the front-engined water-cooled 928, supported by the smaller 924 and the new 944, would carry the brand forward. The 911 SC was meant to be the model's farewell tour. That plan was reversed in mid-1981 when Peter Schutz, having taken over as chief executive at the start of that year, ordered the 911 program extended indefinitely. The decision shaped the rest of the decade: the Carrera 3.2 launched for 1984, the 911 Turbo (930) returned to the United States in 1986 after a five-year emissions-driven absence, and the 911 was eventually treated as the technological halo platform — culminating in the 959 supercar program that started life as a Group B rally homologation car in 1983 and reached customers as a road car in 1987.

Commercially, the front-engined transaxle line — 924, 944, 928 — was the volume and revenue engine for most of the decade. The 944 alone reached production volumes (more than 26,000 cars in calendar 1985 in the U.S. market) that briefly outsold every other Porsche line combined, and Porsche's accounts during this period were largely funded by the four-cylinder cars rather than the air-cooled flagship. The 928 progressed from the 4.7-litre single-cam S to the 5.0-litre 32-valve S4 in 1986, with the GT (1989) introducing the dogleg-only enthusiast variant. The 944 added a turbocharged 951 in 1985, a 16-valve naturally aspirated S in 1986, the Turbo S "Silver Rose" in 1988, and the 3.0-litre S2 (and the platform's first cabriolet) in 1989. Factory turbocharging spread across nearly every model line during the decade.

Currency dynamics drove much of what U.S. buyers paid and how Porsche behaved. The strong dollar of 1980–1985 made German cars cheap in America: a 1984 Carrera coupe carried a roughly $32,000 list price. The dollar's collapse from 1985 onward (the Plaza Accord was September 1985) sent transaction prices upward sharply through 1986–1989; by 1989, a 911 Turbo with the optional Slantnose treatment exceeded $100,000 before options. The 924 Turbo had already been pulled from the U.S. in 1980 on emissions grounds; the 924S was reintroduced cheap to plug the gap; both were dropped by the end of 1988. Mid-cycle, two engineering events stand out: the 1984 Carrera 3.2's larger engine and Bosch Motronic management replaced the K-Jetronic SC, and the 1987 introduction of the Getrag G50 five-speed manual replaced the long-running Type 915 transmission across the 911 line — the most significant 911 mechanical change of the decade. By the end of the decade, Porsche was already deep into 964 development, the 924 was gone, the 928 was a low-volume specialty item, and the 944 was being prepared for its conversion into the 968. This document covers the 1980-1989 portion of all that activity.

## 911 G-Body (Carrera 3.2 and SC)

### Generation Overview

- **Years of production:** 911 SC 1978-1983 (1980-1983 in scope); Carrera 3.2 1984-1989; SC/RS 1984.
- **Chassis code:** 911 (G-Series body shell, in continuous production since 1974).
- **Engine family:** Air-cooled flat-six, single overhead camshaft per bank, two valves per cylinder, dry-sump lubrication. SC: 3.0-litre Type 930/03 (RoW), 930/04 (US 49-state), 930/06 (California), 930/05 (Japan). Carrera 3.2: 3,164 cc Type 930/20 RoW, Type 930/21 US (lower compression, three-way catalyst).
- **Transmission options:** Type 915 five-speed manual standard 1980-1986; Getrag G50 five-speed manual replaced the 915 from model year 1987 forward, accompanied by a hydraulically operated clutch. No automatic was offered on the SC or Carrera 3.2.
- **Drivetrain:** Rear-engined, rear-wheel drive throughout.
- **Sub-generation splits:** SC pre/post-1981 power bump (180 → 188 → 204 PS RoW); Carrera 3.2 pre/post-G50 1987 transmission change. The MY 1987 G50 split is the most consequential mid-cycle event of the entire G-body.
- **Notable mid-cycle changes:** First factory 911 Cabriolet introduced for MY 1983 (SC). Carrera 3.2 introduced redesigned dashboard with larger HVAC vents for MY 1986. M491 "Turbo-Look" wide-body option offered on Coupe from 1984, extended to Targa and Cabriolet from MY 1986. Sixteen-inch Fuchs wheels (8-inch rear) standardised for MY 1989. Speedster body style added late in MY 1989.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The SC and Carrera 3.2 are visually almost interchangeable to the untrained eye. The two clearest external tells are the integrated front fog lights in the lower bumper of the Carrera (the SC carries them in the bumperettes only on cars so optioned) and, post-1986, the redesigned interior with larger air-conditioning vents. Mechanically, the SC's 3.0-litre runs Bosch K-Jetronic mechanical fuel injection, while the Carrera 3.2's 3.2-litre uses Bosch Motronic engine management — a far more reliable system, and the explanation for why the Carrera is generally regarded as a more daily-usable air-cooled 911 than the SC.

The MY 1987 introduction of the Getrag G50 transmission split the Carrera 3.2 into pre-G50 and post-G50 cars. The pre-G50 cars use the Type 915 gearbox (a derivative of the 1972 911 transmission), known for a notchy shift quality. The G50 came with a hydraulic clutch, smoother throw and BorgWarner synchros, and is universally preferred by buyers and drivers — material to valuation. A 1987 Coupe with G50 commands a meaningful premium over a 1986 Coupe with otherwise identical equipment.

The 911 SC/RS is a distinct car despite the SC name. Built for FIA Group B Evolution homologation, it used the older 3.0-litre block (the new 3.2-litre would have placed it in a heavier weight class), a Kugelfischer mechanical injection setup, single-piece bucket seats, and lightweight panels — claimed unladen weight around 960 kg. Five of the twenty production cars went to Rothmans/Prodrive for the 1984 European and Middle East rally programmes. One additional car was retained by Porsche for the museum, which is why some sources cite 21 cars and others 20 — record this in the conflicts log.

The Speedster is best understood as a body option on the Carrera 3.2 Cabriolet, not a separate model. The mechanicals are pure 1989 Carrera. What you are paying for is the cut-down windshield (more steeply raked, the cars often described as having "letterbox" proportions) and the twin "humps" of the body-coloured tonneau cover behind the seats. The hood was a single-layer canvas, described in factory documentation as for emergency use rather than regular weather protection. The narrow-body Speedster is much rarer than the Turbo-Look variant.

The 1988 Commemorative Edition celebrates the production of the 250,000th 911 (a Commemorative Edition Coupe was the actual milestone car). It is a colour-and-trim package — Diamond Blue Metallic paint with colour-matched Fuchs centres, Ferry Porsche's signature stitched into the headrest area, silver-blue interior — applied to all three body styles. The 1989 25th Anniversary Special Edition is a US-market trim package marking 25 years of 911 production: silver or satin black metallic paint, silk grey interior. Note that the user-supplied scope refers to a "25th Anniversary" car for 1988, but Porsche references confirm the 25th Anniversary nameplate belonged to the 1989 model — in 1988 the parallel commemorative was the CE for 250,000 cars built. Both should be treated as separate variants for valuation purposes.

The Club Sport (option M637) is the most performance-focused of the Carrera 3.2 family. The engine is mechanically standard 3.2 but blueprinted, with hollow intake valves and a higher rev limit (raised from 6,520 to 6,840 rpm). What distinguishes it is what's missing: no rear seats, no sound deadening (most), no electric windows, no central locking, no air-conditioning (with two factory exceptions), no rear wiper, no door pocket lids, no headlight washers, no hood lock, and a corrosion warranty halved from ten to two years because of the reduced underbody sealant. Approximately 70 kg saved over the standard Carrera. Most CS cars wear "Carrera CS" decals on the front fender or rocker, and all carry an "SP" stamp on the crankcase and cylinder head. UK cars were uniformly Grand Prix White with red side decals and red Fuchs centres; other markets received a wider colour palette. Despite the price premium, sales were modest — 340 cars over three model years — making this a halo variant in valuation terms.

### Sub-Generation Splits

The G50 transmission introduction at MY 1987 is the dominant split. Diagnostic: shift pattern (915 has a longer throw, "dogleg" first below reverse on RoW pre-1986; G50 has a conventional H-pattern with reverse to the right and forward), and the hydraulic clutch reservoir visible under the front trunk on G50 cars. The split materially affects valuation across all Carrera 3.2 body styles.

The MY 1986 dashboard redesign (larger HVAC vents, lower front seats) is a smaller but notable split. Pre-1986 interiors carry the older SC-style dashboard; from 1986 onward the dashboard moves to what becomes the canonical late-G-body design.

For the SC, the 1981 power bump from 188 to 204 PS in RoW spec is occasionally cited but is less material to valuation than to spec-sheet completeness.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 1980-1985 absence of the 911 Turbo from the US market is the most consequential commercial fact about this generation. Porsche could not certify the engine for US emissions during this period (the Turbo's tighter packaging made adding the catalyst hardware difficult), and so the only 1980-1985 model year 930s in North America entered through grey-market channels — many converted by independent shops to a level that more or less satisfied the EPA "Show or Display" exception. For valuation, US-market grey-market 1980-1985 Turbos sit in a separate category from the 1986-1989 federalised cars, and original German-market sales documentation can be required to prove provenance.

When the Turbo returned to the US for MY 1986, it did so with the Type 930/68 catalysed engine producing 282 hp — a meaningful drop from the 300 hp RoW spec — and at a list price of approximately $48,000. Porsche held this price flat in nominal terms through MY 1986; subsequent years saw aggressive escalation as the dollar weakened, with the 1989 list price reaching approximately $71,000 for the Coupe.

The Targa and Cabriolet variants were available as Sonderwunsch special orders in RoW markets earlier in the decade, but became standardised dealer-orderable options for all markets from MY 1987 onward. Targa production was the lowest of the three body styles by a wide margin — under 200 cars per model year worldwide — making the Turbo Targa a halo variant in valuation terms.

The 1989 model year is unique in being the only year of 930 production with the Getrag G50 five-speed transmission (specifically the G50/50 variant, with a stronger gear set than the Carrera's G50). This is universally regarded as the most desirable specification of the 3.3 Turbo and commands a meaningful market premium over identical 1987 or 1988 four-speed cars.

The Slantnose / Flachbau warrants careful treatment because the term covers three distinct factory generations plus an unknown number of dealer and aftermarket conversions. Generation 1 (1981-1983) is the "hammerhead" — a smooth front fender with no pop-up headlights, twin lamps recessed in the front valance below the bumper line. Sources put production at approximately 58 cars; all were Sonderwunsch special orders. Generation 2 (1983-1987) added pop-up headlights in flat fenders and a deeper front valance with a centrally mounted oil cooler; production figures for this generation are difficult to pin down but appear to be on the order of 250 cars, all still Sonderwunsch. Generation 3 (1987-1989) saw the Slantnose move to a dealer-orderable production option, M505 for the US (with the standard 930 valance and oil cooler relocated to the rear right fender to satisfy state safety regulations) and M506 for RoW (deeper valance, central oil cooler, typically with the 330 hp Performance Kit). The total factory Slantnose production across all three generations is most often cited at 948 cars, but the figure is not officially published by Porsche, and dealer and aftermarket conversions are common — diligence on individual cars is necessary.

Generation 3 cars from MY 1987 are easily identified by the M505 or M506 option code on the factory sticker on the underside of the front trunk lid. Earlier Sonderwunsch cars do not carry an option code on the sticker; authentication for these cars typically requires a Porsche Certificate of Authenticity (now Certificate of Production) drawn from the factory archive. Dealer-converted cars (notably those built by Alan Johnson Racing in the US from factory-supplied kits) can be authentic in mechanical content but lack original-VIN provenance — record this distinction for valuation.

The 1988 "Turbo S" terminology is a source of significant confusion. There is no factory model line called "911 Turbo S" in this period (the name is later — 1992-1994). What sources sometimes catalogue under "1988 US Turbo S" appears to be a small number of Slantnose-feature cars fitted with normal 911 headlights rather than the M505 hidden lamps, often with the 330 hp Performance Kit. Whether this constitutes a distinct variant or a variation within M505 is disputed — flag in conflicts log.

The 930 LE (Limited Edition) / Sondermodell is a 50-car UK-only run for MY 1989 with conventional 930 bodywork (no Slantnose), full leather interior, and special trim. Documentation references confirm 50 cars; this is the cleanest and best-documented limited variant of the Turbo line for the decade.

### Sub-Generation Splits

The MY 1986 US reintroduction is the dominant US-market split. Pre-1986 US-titled cars are grey-market imports and command a different valuation profile from federalised 1986-1989 cars.

The MY 1989 G50 transmission introduction is the dominant late-generation split. 1989 G50 cars command a premium over otherwise comparable 1987-1988 four-speed cars — this is more material than the equivalent split on the Carrera 3.2 because the 930's four-speed gearbox was widely regarded as inadequate for the engine's torque output.

The MY 1987 standardisation of Targa and Cabriolet body styles, and the standardisation of the M505/M506 Slantnose option, are minor splits in volume but significant in valuation for non-Coupe and Slantnose variants.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 928's nomenclature is unusually inconsistent and has tripped up valuation databases for years. The engineering reality and the marketing language do not always match. There is no factory designation "S2"; that name was applied to the MY 1984-1986 928 S only by the UK importer (the underlying engine was the 4.7L 16V running higher compression and Bosch LH-Jetronic / EZF). Likewise, "S3" is an unofficial enthusiast term for the MY 1985-1986 US-only 5.0L 32V cars; Porsche internally and on the rear badge simply called these "928 S" as well. "S4" was the first official rear-badge variant designation since the 928 S itself, and it covered the worldwide 5.0L 32V cars from 1987 forward.

The 1986/87 facelift accompanying the S4 is the single most identifiable visual cue across the 928 line. Pre-facelift cars (all 4.7L cars and the US-only "S3" cars) carry separate front spoiler, more upright nose with less integrated headlamp surrounds, and a hatch-integrated rear wing. S4 and later cars carry a smoother monolithic nose, integrated tail wing on a separate plinth, and side skirts. Drag coefficient improved from 0.39 to 0.34.

The Club Sport (CS), SE, and GT form a related family of enthusiast variants. The CS (option M637, like the 911 Carrera CS) is the most stripped — manual-only, deleted radio antenna integrated into windshield, no leather on the dash, no rear sun visors, manual seat adjustment. Approximately 19 production CS cars were built across MY 1988 and MY 1989, in addition to five prototypes built earlier as factory drivers' company cars (Bell, Mass, Stuck, Wollek, Ickx). The SE was a UK-market parallel option for MY 1988 sometimes called "S4 Sport"; 42 cars are documented. The GT replaced both the CS and SE for MY 1989 and was a more equipped enthusiast variant — manual-only, ZF 40% LSD standard, Club Sport wheels — but with normal interior trim. 1990 model year GTs added Porsche's PSD limited-slip and made RDK tyre pressure monitoring standard, but these changes are out of 1980s scope.

GT production figures across the 1989-1991 lifespan vary materially across sources: 1,620 (Rennlist), 2,070 (conceptcarz). The lower figure may exclude an estimated 70-80 US-market and 320-400 RoW MY 1989 GTs that some sources count under late S4 production. Record range. The MY 1989 portion of GT production is not cleanly publicly documented and is flagged in the conflicts log.

### Sub-Generation Splits

The 1986/87 S4 introduction (5.0L 32V worldwide, integrated nose, tail wing on plinth) is the most material split in the 928 line. Pre-S4 4.7L cars and the US-only "S3" cars sit at one valuation tier; S4 and later cars sit at a higher tier. Diagnostic features: rear-wing styling (integrated vs. separate plinth), front-bumper integration, side-skirt presence.

The 1989 GT/CS/SE break out is a smaller volume split but creates a halo tier above S4. Diagnostic: dogleg manual transmission only, "GT" or "CS" or "SE" rear badge, 16-inch Club Sport wheels.

For US-market valuation specifically, the 1985-1986 "S3" 32-valve cars are sometimes grouped with 4.7L S cars and sometimes treated separately. They were the first US 928s with the 5.0L DOHC engine and command modest premiums over 4.7L US cars on that basis.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 944 line is the most variant-rich Porsche family of the decade and warrants the most careful treatment. The naming becomes confusing because Porsche used different rear badges and different markets used different names for the same mechanical configuration.

The base 944 evolved in three meaningful ways. The "85.5" mid-1985 update (sometimes called 1985B) added a redesigned dashboard, embedded windshield antenna, larger oil sump, upgraded 115-amp alternator, new front and rear cast alloy control arms, and "phone dial" wheels in place of the earlier "cookie cutter" design. The MY 1987 update added optional ABS and standard dual airbags on US cars. The MY 1989 update bored the engine from 2.5 to 2.7 litres and added 162 hp — the 2.7L existed only for that single model year as a bridge to the S2. For valuation purposes, the 1985.5 break and the 1989-only 2.7L are the most material splits within the base 944.

The 944 Turbo (the 951, or 952 in RHD guise) was a serious rework of the base car, not just a turbocharged engine. Body changes included an integrated front bumper with the widest indicators of any production car at the time, revised aerodynamics dropping drag to 0.33, a two-piece rear diffuser, and Brembo four-piston fixed-caliper brakes lifted from the 928. The engine carried a ceramic exhaust port liner — a world first in a production engine — to manage exhaust gas temperature, along with forged pistons and inconel headers. More than thirty internal engine components were revised to handle the increased loads.

The 1988 Turbo S is the highest-profile collector variant. The M44/52 engine with the larger KKK K26-8 turbine, revised mapping holding boost to higher rpm, and the M030 suspension package (Koni adjustable shocks, ride-height adjusters, larger anti-roll bars, harder durometer bushings, chassis stiffening brackets) make it materially faster than the standard Turbo. Front brakes were the larger Brembo 4-piston caliper / 12-inch disc setup from the 928 S4. Many Turbo S cars wear "Silver Rose" metallic paint (LM3Z) with a "Studio" cloth interior in burgundy plaid — but this is not universal: many cars left the factory in other colours and trim combinations. Sources differ materially on production:
- 944 Turbo S Registry research (cited via Rennlist): 1,635 worldwide Turbo S / SE; 715-720 to US
- Peter Morgan: 1,300-1,500 (planned 1,000)
- Barnebys: ~1,200 worldwide, 77 RHD UK
- Specifically Silver Rose colour-coded cars: ~775

For valuation, the cleaner approach is to record both "all 1988 Turbo S badged cars" and "Silver Rose trim subset" separately, since they command different prices.

For MY 1989, Porsche dropped the "S" suffix and made the M44/52 engine and most Turbo S equipment standard on the regular Turbo, except that M030 suspension and the Club Sport wheels remained options. As a result, a 1989 Turbo without M030 has different driving and resale character from a 1988 Turbo S. The 1989 cars are sometimes cataloguing-referenced as "Turbo S" because of the engine, but the rear badge says "Turbo" only.

The 944 S (1987-1988) introduced the DOHC 16-valve head, which carried over (enlarged to 3.0L) into the S2. The S looks externally like a base 944 (early bumper style, "phone dial" wheels) but mechanically belongs with the 16-valve cars; market valuation treats it as its own tier between base 944 and Turbo.

The 944 S2 (MY 1989+) used the 3.0L M44/41 engine in the Turbo's body (rounded integrated nose, side skirts, full diffuser). The Cabriolet body style is unique to the S2 within the 944 family — no base, S, or Turbo cabriolets exist for the 944 (a separately documented 944 Turbo Cabriolet appears for MY 1991 only, which is post-1989s scope, but is mentioned in the data sources for completeness). ASC partially assembled the S2 cabriolets.

The 944 Turbo Cup racing series produced 192 race cars across the championship's life (1986-1990), with championships running in five countries (Germany, France, USA, Canada, South Africa). These are race-only and outside the road-car scope, but cataloguing systems sometimes catch them.

The Porsche-Callaway 944 Turbo programme of 1983-1984 (twenty cars converted in the US by Callaway from naturally aspirated 944s) predates the factory 951 and has separate provenance — flag for completeness.

### Sub-Generation Splits

Within the base 944, the 1985.5 mid-year update is the dominant split. Diagnostic: dashboard design (separate gauge pods pre-85.5 vs. integrated panel post-), wheel design (cookie cutter vs. phone dial), antenna location (mast pre-85.5 vs. windshield-embedded post-).

For the Turbo, the 1988 Turbo S → 1989 standard-spec Turbo transition is the dominant split. Pre-1988 Turbo cars use M44/51 (217-220 hp); 1988 Turbo S and all 1989-1991 Turbos use M44/52 (247-250 hp).

The 1989 base 944 is its own split as the only 2.7L year and a single-MY production run.

For the S/S2, the 1989 transition from S (2.5L 16V) to S2 (3.0L 16V) is a step change in displacement, body style availability (cabriolet appears), and bodywork (S2 uses Turbo-spec body).

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 924 family is the most polarising and most variable in valuation across the 1980s Porsche lineup, largely because the base car carries an Audi-sourced engine (a fact that has dogged the 924's reputation since launch) while the Carrera GT family and the 924S use considerably more Porsche-developed mechanicals. The differentiation matters: a 924 base and a 924S can look almost identical externally but are mechanically half a generation apart.

The 924 base 1980-1985 is the cheapest Porsche of the decade, advertised in the US at $13,000-$16,000 range. The block was Volkswagen's cast-iron unit originally developed for the LT van and Audi 100, with a Porsche-designed aluminium head. The car was built at the VW/Audi Neckarsulm plant. US-spec cars produced 95 hp (early) to 110 hp (post-1977 catalyst). RoW cars produced 110-125 hp. After 1982, the US base 924 was dropped to make room for the 944; production continued in Europe through 1985.

Between 1980 and 1981 Porsche issued two large limited-edition runs of the 924 base: 1,030 "Le Mans" cars in 1980 (commemorating 924-platform Le Mans success) and 1,015 "50 Jahre Porsche / Weissach" cars in 1981 (50th anniversary of the company's founding). These appear in production totals for those calendar years and are historically significant but mechanically standard.

The 924 Turbo (internally 931) was Porsche's response to criticism of the base 924's pace. Visual giveaways are the NACA duct in the hood, four-slot air vents in the badge panel, alloy wheels, and a front spoiler. Two production series exist: Series 1 (M31/30 engine, 1979-1980) and Series 2 (M31/03 with Siemens-Hartig digital ignition, 1981-1982). Series 2 was the first Porsche to use digital ignition. The 924 Turbo was sold in the US for MY 1980 (approximately 3,440 cars) before emissions and tax issues drove it out; some 1981 cars reached US buyers through grey-market channels but were not federalised. Italian-market production continued into 1984 because Italy taxed engines above 2.0L into a much higher class — out of scope.

The 924 Carrera GT, GTS, and GTR are Porsche's Group 4 homologation programme and form a distinct halo subset within the 924 family. The Carrera GT (1980) was built in 406 examples (including six prototypes) with flared polyurethane fenders, an intercooler atop the engine fed by a hood scoop, pop-up lights, and 210 hp. The GT was never officially imported to the US — emissions and safety regulations of the time made it impractical. Of the 400 road-going GTs, 75 were RHD (Type 938) for the UK; the rest were Type 937 LHD. After Carrera GT production ended in December 1980, Porsche built a small evolution series of 59 Carrera GTS cars (1981) with 245 hp, fixed perspex headlight covers, a nose-mounted intercooler, and approximately 60 kg less weight. Of the 59, 15 were "Clubsport" cars with roll cage and further weight reduction. The Carrera GTR (1981) is race-only, with approximately 17 production cars plus 2 spare chassis (sometimes catalogued as 19 total, sometimes as 17). The GTR engine produced 375 hp from 2.0L using a dry-sump, a 935-derived cylinder head, and Bosch-Kugelfischer mechanical fuel injection.

The 924S (1986-1988) is the most commercially significant 924 variant for the 1980s but the easiest to misclassify. After VW discontinued the 2.0L block in 1985, Porsche fitted the 944's 2.5L M44 engine (slightly detuned for the narrower 924 cooling system) into the 924 body shell, added 944-derived brakes and 5-lug hubs, and reintroduced the car as the 924S. US list price launched at $19,995. For MY 1987, Porsche issued a special edition of 980 cars with regional naming: "Le Mans" (UK), "SE" Special Edition (US, M756, ~500 cars in black with sunroof and 944-spec equipment delete), "Spirit" (Spain, 30 cars), "Exclusiv" (Germany), "924CS" (Japan, 20 cars, with 944-spec hardware and a roof-mounted antenna). The Le Mans / SE cars carry stripes and decals and have an additional 10 hp from revised pistons and compression. For MY 1988 Porsche issued a final UK runout edition of 74 cars (37 white + 37 black, with "Le Mans" decals).

The 924S' MY 1988 power increase to 158-160 hp, achieved through different pistons that raised compression from 9.7:1 to 10.2:1 (and required 95 RON / 91 AKI fuel), made it slightly faster than the contemporary base 944 due to its lighter weight. This is one reason the 924S is sometimes preferred by enthusiasts to a same-year 944 base.

The 924S was dropped after MY 1988, making it Porsche's final entry-level model of the decade — by the end of 1989 the 944 base had also been cancelled and the 968 was being prepared.

### Sub-Generation Splits

The 1985-1986 transition from 924 base (VW/Audi engine) to 924S (Porsche M44 engine) is the dominant valuation split within the 924 line. Diagnostic: hood NACA duct (Turbo only), 5-lug vs. 4-lug wheels (924S vs. 924), engine bay layout. The 924S sits at materially higher valuation than the same-year 924 base would (had the 924 base remained in US production).

Within the 924 Turbo, the 1980/81 Series 1 / Series 2 split is well-documented but not particularly material to valuation in most markets — both cars are rare enough that condition and provenance dominate.

The Carrera GT / GTS / GTR group sits in its own valuation tier far above the base and Turbo cars; the GT is more available than the GTS or GTR but all three are halo cars.

## 959

### Generation Overview

- **Years of production:** 1985 prototypes / Vorserie. 1986-1988 production (customer deliveries from May 1987). 1992-1993 reassembly batch (out of 1980s scope, cross-reference to 1990s file).
- **Chassis code:** 959.
- **Engine family:** 2,849 cc (2,850 cc rounded) flat-6 derived from the 956 / 962 race engines, with sequential KKK twin-turbocharging, water-cooled cylinder heads on an air-cooled block, four valves per cylinder, dual overhead camshafts. 450 PS / 444 hp (Komfort). 515 PS / 508 hp (S / Sport with larger turbos).
- **Transmission options:** Six-speed manual standard (the lowest gear is a "Gelände" off-road first); no automatic offered.
- **Drivetrain:** Permanent all-wheel drive with PSK (Porsche-Steuer Kupplung) electronically controlled multi-plate clutch in the transfer case, varying torque split between front and rear axles dynamically.
- **Sub-generation splits:** Komfort (luxury-equipped) vs. Sport (stripped, larger turbos, lighter; AKA 959 S). Pre-production / Vorserie cars are mechanically prototype-spec and treated separately by collectors.
- **Notable mid-cycle changes:** None within the 1986-1988 production run; the 1992-1993 reassembly cars added a speed-sensitive damper system and were sold at a higher price point.

### Variant Differentiation Notes

The 959 is unique in 1980s Porsche valuation because it sits genuinely above the rest of the lineup — including the 911 Turbo S and the 928 GT — and because it was never officially federalised for the US market. Customer deliveries were limited to RoW markets in 1987-1988, and US-resident buyers either left their cars in Europe, imported under "Show or Display" exception once that became available, or waited 25 years for the federal exemption. Bill Gates and Paul Allen famously imported 959s through congressional earmarks as "museum cars."

The Komfort versus Sport (S) distinction is the most material valuation split. Komfort cars carry full luxury equipment, hydraulic ride-height adjustment, and the standard 444 hp engine. Sport (S) cars delete the hydraulic suspension, much of the interior trim, and add larger turbochargers for 508 hp; these were originally intended in part as a "race car" workaround for US importation that did not succeed. Sport production is most often cited at 29 cars; the more inclusive figure of 37 in some sources may include prototype and press cars. The Sport commands a substantial premium over Komfort — the figure most frequently cited in market commentary is 50-80% over Komfort comparable.

The Vorserie / pre-production cars (1985-1986) are mechanically prototype-spec and were used for engineering, press, and display purposes. Wikipedia counts 37 such cars within a stated "337 total" (337 = 292 production + 8 reassembly + 37 prototypes, though arithmetic does not always reconcile across sources). Most prototypes were dismantled; a small number survived. Sports Car Market documents 16 prototypes and 21 pre-production cars (total 37) separately from production. These are highly variable in condition and provenance.

The 1992-1993 reassembly batch (8 cars total, all Komfort, four in red and four in silver) are technically continuation cars built from spare parts inventory. They incorporate a speed-sensitive damper system not fitted to the 1986-1988 production cars, and were sold at DM 747,500 (more than 75% above the 1987 list price). These are out of 1980s scope but are noted here for cross-reference to the 1990s file.

### Sub-Generation Splits

There are no within-generation splits during the 1986-1988 production run — the 959's engineering was finalised before customer cars began delivery, and the run was too short for mid-cycle changes.

The Komfort / Sport split is the only material valuation distinction. Diagnostic: hydraulic ride-height adjusters (Komfort only — visible at the suspension uprights), interior leather and equipment level (Komfort: full; Sport: stripped), turbocharger size (visible only with engine inspection).

The 1986-1988 production / 1992-1993 reassembly distinction is material — the reassembly cars, despite being in some ways "newer" (speed-sensitive dampers), are typically valued comparably to production Komfort cars and are recorded in the 1990s file. They are flagged here because contemporary auction listings sometimes describe them as "1988 model year" cars based on title documentation, when they are physically 1992-1993 build.

## Cross-Generation Notes

### G50 transmission (1987) and its effect on 911 valuations

The Getrag G50 five-speed replaced the long-running Type 915 transmission across the 911 line — Carrera 3.2 from MY 1987, 911 Turbo from MY 1989. The 915 dated to 1972 and was widely criticised for notchy shifting and lack of selector centring. The G50 introduced a hydraulically operated clutch, BorgWarner synchros, and a more conventional H-pattern; the Turbo's G50/50 used a stronger gear set than the Carrera's G50. For valuation, this is the most consequential split in the 911 line during the 1980s. Pre-G50 1986 Carreras typically sit at a 10-25% discount to MY 1987-1988 G50 cars in like-for-like condition; for the Turbo, the 1989 G50 cars are universally regarded as the desirable spec and command a premium of similar magnitude over MY 1987-1988 four-speed cars.

### 911 SC vs Carrera 3.2 distinction

The 1983-1984 transition from SC to Carrera 3.2 is the larger-displacement engine, Bosch Motronic injection (replacing K-Jetronic), redesigned dashboard (delayed to MY 1986), front fog lights integrated into the lower bumper, and improved chain tensioners. The Carrera 3.2 is materially more reliable as a daily-usable air-cooled 911. The SC retains a price discount across most condition tiers, with the 1983 Cabriolet (single-MY production) being a partial exception due to first-cabriolet status.

### Slantnose / Flachbau across the 911 Turbo years

Three factory generations span the decade: the 58-car Generation 1 hammerhead (1981-1983, Sonderwunsch), an estimated 250-car Generation 2 with pop-up lights (1983-1987, Sonderwunsch), and the 686-car Generation 3 (1987-1989, dealer-orderable as M505 in the US and M506 in RoW). The total most commonly cited is 948 across all generations, but Porsche has not officially published the figure and the Generation 2 count is the weakest data point. Generation 3 cars are easy to authenticate by the M505 / M506 option code on the underhood factory sticker; earlier Sonderwunsch cars require Porsche Certificate of Authenticity / Production from the factory archive. Aftermarket and dealer (notably Alan Johnson Racing) conversions exist in significant numbers and command different valuations from factory cars.

### 944 vs 924S engine sharing

From MY 1986 the 924S used the 944's 2.5L M44 engine (slightly detuned for the narrower 924 cooling system), creating an unusual situation where the 924-bodied entry car shared its engine with the more upmarket 944. The 924S used 944-derived 5-lug hubs and brakes but retained the 924's narrower body. For valuation, the 924S sits between the 924 Turbo (above) and the 924 base (below) and roughly competes with the contemporary base 944 — the 924S being slightly faster than the base 944 in late-1988 spec due to lower curb weight, despite less power on paper.

### 928 S/S2/S4/GT progression

The 928 progressed through the decade with two main engine generations and multiple intermediate label variants. Engineering simplification:
- 4.5L 16V SOHC: pre-1980 only (out of scope)
- 4.7L 16V SOHC: 928 S worldwide MY 1980-1986; UK marketing called this "S2" from MY 1984
- 5.0L 32V DOHC: introduced for US-only "S" (informally "S3") MY 1985-1986; standardised worldwide as S4 from MY 1987; uprated for GT (MY 1989+, 326-330 PS); ultimate 5.4L variant in GTS is post-1989s scope
- The 1986/1987 facelift to integrated bumpers and tail wing on plinth is the single most identifiable 928 visual generation marker. Pre-facelift cars (all 4.7L cars and US "S3" cars) and post-facelift S4/GT cars sit at distinct valuation tiers.

### 959 program and its halo position

The 959 sits at a distinct halo tier above all other 1980s Porsche road cars, including the 928 GT and the 911 Turbo S equivalents. It was never federalised for US sale and reached US ownership only through grey-market, Show-and-Display, or 25-year-import channels. The 959 also functioned as the engineering halo for the company — many 959 technologies (sequential twin-turbocharging, all-wheel drive control, advanced electronics) influenced the subsequent 964 generation and the all-wheel-drive 911 Turbo line from the 993 forward. For valuation, the 959 is its own market and tends not to follow the rest of the 1980s air-cooled 911 market closely.

### Currency / exchange-rate notes affecting US sticker prices

The early 1980s saw an exceptionally strong US dollar against the Deutschmark, which kept US Porsche list prices low in nominal terms. The 1984 Carrera 3.2 launched in the US at $32,000 — comparable in real terms to the 911 SC the year before. The Plaza Accord of September 1985 began a multi-year dollar weakening that drove German-import prices upward sharply through the rest of the decade. By MY 1989 the equivalent Carrera coupe was listed at $52,000+; the 911 Turbo had risen from $48,000 (1986 reintroduction) to $71,000+ (MY 1989); the 928 S4 from $58,000 (MY 1987) to $74,000+ (MY 1989); and the 944 Turbo from $30,000 (MY 1986) to $45,000+ (MY 1989). For valuation comparison purposes, MY 1986 cars and MY 1989 cars have very different nominal-dollar new-car relationships, even when mechanically similar. This affects depreciation curves and present-day relative pricing.
