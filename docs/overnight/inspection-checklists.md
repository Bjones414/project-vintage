# Pre-Purchase Inspection Checklists — Collector Porsche 911
## Generations: 964 · 993 · 996.2 · 997.1

> **How to use:** These checklists supplement a formal PPI from a Porsche-specialist shop — they are not a replacement. Bring this document to your in-person walk-around before you authorize a shop inspection. Items tagged `[VERIFY]` are based on strong consensus but should be confirmed against marque-specific sources before use in product copy.
>
> **Scope:** Applies to street cars. Excludes RS/GT variants and factory-turbocharged cars, which have generation-specific additional inspection points.

---

## 964 (1989–1994)

### 1. Body / Paint

- **Front trunk (frunk) battery box** — lift carpet and inspect metal floor for rust; electrolytic corrosion here is near-universal on unrestored cars and indicates whether the car has been maintained from the bottom up.
- **Front windscreen surround** — run a finger under the lower rubber seal; bubbling paint or soft metal means moisture has been sitting there for years and structural rust may be below the surface.
- **Rear windscreen lower channel** — press gently at the corners of the rubber seal; any flex or sponginess signals rust in the pinch weld underneath.
- **Rear quarter gusset plates** — behind each rear light cluster, where the quarter panel meets the engine bay; bubbling paint here is common and repair costs vary widely based on depth of corrosion.
- **Engine lid drip rail** — inspect the horizontal channel at the top of the engine lid; standing water collects here and rusts from the inside.
- **Door sill end caps** — remove end cap trim to expose the inner sill cavity; rust works outward and will be invisible from outside until structurally advanced.
- **Front wheel arch lip inner seams** — look up and inward with a flashlight; trapped road grit holds moisture against the seam.
- **Paint depth gauge** — run a gauge across all panels; factory steel averages 90–130 µm; readings above 200 µm indicate filler; readings below 80 µm indicate prior bare-metal work or repaint.
- **Panel gaps** — 964 gaps are slightly wider than modern standards; uneven gaps (particularly at doors or front lid) indicate accident repair or replacement panels.
- **Paint code verification** — paint code is on a sticker inside the driver's door jamb (e.g., L041 Grand Prix White, G1G1 Midnight Blue); confirm the code matches visible color and option sticker in the frunk.

### 2. Drivetrain

- **Compression or leak-down test** — mandatory on the 3.6L flat-six; a healthy engine shows even compression across all six cylinders; any cylinder more than 10% below the others warrants further investigation.
- **Valve cover gaskets** — inspect underside of engine lid for oil staining; weeping valve covers are routine but heavy oil fouling on the exhaust shrouds means the car has been running that way for a long time.
- **Oil cooler lines** — the braided lines that route oil to the front cooler crack with age; any seeping is a fire risk and a negotiating point.
- **Rear engine seals** — look for oil staining at the base of the engine behind the fan; the 964 has a rear crankshaft seal and engine case seals that are expensive to address with the engine in the car.
- **Chain tensioners** — have the shop verify hydraulic tensioner function; a rattling cold-start that clears within 5–10 seconds suggests worn tensioners.
- **Dual mass flywheel (DMF)** — the 964's DMF is a wear item most buyers overlook; at idle, a light shudder on throttle application or rattle on deceleration suggests DMF wear; replacement requires gearbox removal and costs $2,000–$4,000 in labor alone. `[VERIFY cost range by market]`
- **Clutch feel** — engage the clutch at low speed in 1st gear; slipping, a high bite point, or chattering on engagement indicates a clutch near end of life.
- **5-speed gearbox synchros** — 2nd gear synchro wear is the most common gearbox issue; if 2nd gear resists or crunches on fast downshifts, a gearbox rebuild is likely.

### 3. Suspension

- **Front control arm bushings** — grasp the front wheel at 9 and 3 o'clock and push/pull; any perceptible play indicates worn control arm bushings, which affect handling and tire wear.
- **Front torsion bar tubes** — the 964 uses front torsion bars, not springs; check for oil seeping from the torsion bar tubes at the trailing arm; a leaking torsion tube seal is a labor-intensive repair.
- **Rear trailing arm bushings** — the rear suspension uses trailing arms with rubber bushings; worn bushings cause imprecise rear-end feel and accelerated tire wear.
- **Shock absorbers** — inspect all four dampers for oil seeping down the shaft; any weeping shock is at or near end of life.
- **Tire wear pattern** — feathering or camber wear on rears suggests either a used-up alignment or worn suspension bushings that prevent holding alignment.

### 4. Interior

- **Dashboard top** — 964 dash pads crack from UV exposure; surface cracks are cosmetic, deep cracks reaching the foam indicate the pad needs replacement ($400–$800 new, more for correct-spec).
- **Carpet moisture** — press down on the floor carpet in both footwells; any dampness indicates either a leaking heater box, windscreen seal failure, or sunroof drain blockage.
- **Heater boxes** — the 964 uses exhaust-warmed heat exchangers routed to the cabin; cracked heat exchangers can introduce exhaust fumes into the interior (CO risk); insist on a CO sniff test with the heat running, or verify the heat exchangers have been replaced.
- **Sunroof drain channels** (if equipped) — pour a small amount of water into the front corner drains and verify it exits at the lower sill; blocked drains cause floor flooding and carpet mold.
- **Seat condition** — check driver's seat bolster for wear and seam separation; correct original Porsche seat fabric or leather is expensive to source correctly.
- **Steering wheel airbag** — if the car has the later airbag wheel, confirm it's intact and undeploy; replaced airbag covers are available but should match the specific color code.
- **Door panel warping** — early 964 door cards warp from heat and moisture; warped panels are a cosmetic issue but indicate the car has seen moisture inside.

### 5. Electrical

- **DME relay** — the DME relay (frunk relay box, position 6) has solder joint failures that cause intermittent no-start conditions; if the car hesitates to start or occasionally cranks without firing, this is the first suspect; a replacement relay is cheap ($20–$40) but the car should not leave on your risk without it being addressed.
- **Headlight pod motors** — cycle both headlights up and down multiple times; any grinding, hesitation, or off-sync movement means the motor or mechanism is worn; pods that stop mid-travel can cause an MOT/safety fail.
- **Heater blower** — test all blower speeds; the resistor pack fails on 964s, often leaving only high speed functional.
- **Central locking** — test all four doors from both the key and driver-side lock button; sticking actuators are common.
- **Cruise control** — engage on a highway-speed stretch of road; 964 cruise systems fail silently and parts availability is moderate.
- **Alarm system** — if present, verify the system does not false-alarm (drain battery); many 964 owners disable aftermarket alarms that were retrofitted.

### 6. Documentation

- **Option sticker** — a white sticker is in the frunk listing every factory-fitted option; verify this matches what the seller represents (sport seats, limited slip, color, transmission type).
- **Porsche factory build record (Kardex)** — request this from Porsche Classic; the Kardex lists every factory option and the delivery date; if the seller cannot provide it, order one yourself before purchase.
- **Service history** — demand invoices, not just a stamped book; look for chain tensioner service, valve cover gaskets, and oil cooler line replacement in the records.
- **Accident history** — ask for any bodywork invoices; have the VIN run through CARFAX or a European equivalent to check for declared accidents.
- **VIN plate verification** — VIN is on a plate in the front trunk floor and stamped into the left door sill; both should match the title.
- **Numbers matching** — the engine stamp on the crankcase and the gearbox stamp should correspond to the VIN; mismatches significantly affect collector value.

### 7. Test Drive

- **Cold start observation** — arrive early and request a cold start; listen for any chain rattle that should clear within 5–10 seconds; watch for oil smoke from exhaust (valve seals, rings).
- **DMF shudder** — at 10–20 mph in 2nd gear at low rpm, note any vibration through the drivetrain; a dual mass flywheel on the way out creates a distinct shudder.
- **Gearbox feel** — the 5-speed G50 gearbox should engage each gear with a positive, slightly notchy feel; avoid if 2nd gear resists on quick downshifts.
- **Braking** — strong brake pedal with no pulsation; brake servo boost is vacuum-assisted via the engine; if the pedal is hard without boost feel, inspect the servo.
- **Engine note at highway rpm** — the 3.6L should pull cleanly to redline with no misfires; any flat spot or stumble under load warrants a fuel system/ignition check.
- **Exhaust smell in cabin** — run the heater on full with windows up for five minutes; any exhaust smell means a cracked heat exchanger and is a deal-breaker or a significant negotiating point.

## 993 (1994–1998)

### 1. Body / Paint

- **Windscreen lower seal** — the 993 windscreen seal traps water against the lower pinch weld; press gently along the bottom edge of the glass; soft metal is rust.
- **Rear quarter panel corners** — check at the tail light cluster seams; the rear valance-to-quarter junction is a water trap.
- **Rear chassis legs** — behind the rear bumper cover (removable at clips), two longitudinal chassis rails run toward the rear; corrosion here is structural and expensive; insist on a lift inspection.
- **Battery box** — same location and issue as 964; check for corrosion under the front carpet.
- **Sill end caps** — pop the end caps at front and rear of each sill; rust migrates from inside out and will be invisible until late stage.
- **Paint code** — on the driver's door jamb sticker; confirm against the option sticker in the frunk and the actual color.
- **Panel gaps and alignment** — the 993 has tight-tolerance panel fits; uneven gaps at the front lid, doors, or bumper covers indicate prior accident repair.
- **Engine lid** — inspect the drip rail along the top edge; standing water rusts the lid from inside, same as 964.

### 2. Drivetrain

- **Chain tensioner condition** — the air-cooled flat-six uses hydraulic chain tensioners; have the shop remove the tensioner caps and measure the ratchet position; fully extended tensioners mean the chains are worn and the tensioners have compensated to their limit. `[VERIFY specific measurement threshold]`
- **Cold-start chain rattle** — a brief rattle at cold start that clears within a few seconds is a strong indicator of worn chain tensioners or stretched chains.
- **VarioRam system** (1996+ MY only) — the variable intake system uses vacuum-actuated flaps at approximately 5,000 rpm; on the test drive, note a distinct power surge above 5,000 rpm; if there is no surge, the system is stuck or disabled; a failed VarioRam is a significant repair.
- **Oil leaks** — inspect the engine bay for valve cover seeping, oil cooler line seeping, and any drips at the base of the engine; the 993 M64 engine leaks in the same places as the 964 but age-related leaks are more prevalent now.
- **Compression/leak-down** — mandatory on all 993s; any significant variation across cylinders (>10%) or overall low compression flags ring/valve wear.
- **Clutch operation** — engage in 1st at low rpm; slipping under load means it's due; a high biting point on an unmodified 993 indicates the clutch is near the end.
- **Gearbox** — early 993s have the G50 5-speed; 1995+ can have the G50/20 6-speed; confirm which is fitted; synchro wear on 3rd gear is noted on higher-mileage examples. `[VERIFY typical mileage threshold]`

### 3. Suspension

- **Front control arm bushings** — wheel rock test at 9 and 3 o'clock; any play means the front control arm bushings or ball joints need replacement before safe use.
- **Rear trailing arms** — at the rear wheels, push/pull at top and bottom (12 and 6 o'clock) to check for bearing wear; side-to-side play at 9 and 3 tests trailing arm bushing condition.
- **Shock absorbers** — all four shocks should be clean and dry; oily film on the shaft means the seal is failing.
- **Steering rack boots** — inspect both ends of the steering rack for torn or missing boots; a torn boot exposes the rack to water and accelerates wear.
- **Front lower ball joints** — pry bar test while car is on a lift; any movement means replacement.
- **Tire wear** — even wear front and rear is the target; excessive inner or outer edge wear indicates either alignment is out or bushings have allowed the geometry to wander.

### 4. Interior

- **Door retaining straps** — open each door to full extension; the fabric strap that limits travel is a known weak point on 993s; a failed strap means the door will swing past its intended stop and can damage the hinge.
- **Dashboard top** — same UV cracking issue as 964; a cracked pad indicates the car has lived in the sun.
- **A/C fan speed controller** — test all fan speeds; the rheostat resistor behind the dashboard fails and typically kills the lower fan speeds while leaving high speed operational.
- **Seat condition** — the factory Sport seats are desirable; verify they are original to the car; aftermarket seats reduce collector value.
- **Headliner** — the 993 headliner sags at the rear with age; sagging headliner is cosmetic but indicates deferred maintenance.
- **Carpet and door seals** — check footwells for moisture as on the 964; door seals deteriorate and allow water ingress.
- **OEM radio and telephone prep** — if the car has factory Becker radio or factory telephone prep, verify functionality; replacements that do not fit the DIN housing correctly indicate the interior has been modified.

### 5. Electrical

- **DME relay** — same failure point as the 964; intermittent no-start is the symptom; relay is cheap and should be replaced if no record of prior replacement.
- **Wiring harness condition** — Porsche issued a recall on 993 wiring harnesses (early cars); verify whether the replacement harness has been fitted; a brittle, cracked harness is a serious fire and reliability risk. `[VERIFY exact recall scope and years]`
- **Headlights** — unlike the 964's pop-up units, the 993 has fixed projector headlights; verify both function and that adjustment screws are intact.
- **Window regulators** — cycle all four windows; worn motor brushes and regulator rails are common; replacement requires door panel removal.
- **OBD1/OBD2 diagnostics** — the 993 uses Bosch Motronic 2.10/5.2; use a Durametric or PIWIS equivalent to pull fault codes before purchase; look especially for lambda (O2) sensor faults, which suggest the car has been running lean or rich.
- **Alarm and immobilizer** — confirm the standard factory alarm works without false triggering; the Porsche immobilizer on 993s is integrated with the DME.

### 6. Documentation

- **Option sticker** — frunk sticker lists every factory option; Turbo-look bodywork, LSD, sport seats, and limited-slip are value-critical options; verify each.
- **Kardex** — order from Porsche Classic or request from seller; confirms factory specification and delivery market.
- **Chain tensioner service records** — specifically ask whether tensioners have been replaced and when; this is the single most critical 993 drivetrain service item.
- **Service book** — look for consistent intervals and stamps from Porsche dealers or recognized independents; large gaps in records are a red flag.
- **Accident history** — run the VIN; the 993's value makes prior undisclosed accident damage a common issue.
- **Numbers matching** — verify engine stamp and gearbox code against Kardex; mismatches are disqualifying for serious collectors.

### 7. Test Drive

- **Cold start chain rattle** — the single most important sound test on a 993; chain tensioner rattle on a cold start that does not clear within 10 seconds suggests significant chain/tensioner wear.
- **VarioRam surge** (if equipped) — in the mid-rpm range (4,500–5,500 rpm), you should feel a distinct step in power delivery as the VarioRam flaps open; no surge means the system is non-functional.
- **Gearbox engagement** — the G50 6-speed has a long-throw, notchy feel that is normal; what is not normal is grinding or blocking on any gear.
- **Rear axle feel** — in tight corners, any vagueness, clunking, or unexpected oversteer suggests worn rear trailing arm bushings.
- **Engine tone at high rpm** — the 993's 3.6L (272 hp) or 3.8L VarioRam (285 hp) should pull to 6,800 rpm with a clean, even note; any stumble or flat spot under hard load warrants investigation.
- **Exhaust smell in cabin** — same protocol as 964; run heater full with windows up; any exhaust smell means a cracked heat exchanger.

## 996.2 (2002–2004 US model years)

> **Generation note:** The 996.2 is the facelifted water-cooled 911, distinguished from the 996.1 (1999–2001) by a revised front bumper, fried-egg headlights replaced with a 993-influenced design, and minor interior updates. All 996.2 Carrera/Carrera S models use the M96 engine with a **single-row IMS bearing** — the higher-failure-rate configuration. The 996.1 dual-row IMS bearing was statistically more reliable. Treat IMS status as the primary valuation input.

### 1. Body / Paint

- **Headlight moisture** — inspect behind the headlight lenses for condensation or a water mark tide line; water intrusion corrodes the reflector housing and is expensive to resolve correctly.
- **Front bumper cover alignment** — the 996.2 front bumper is a large single piece; any lateral misalignment, paint overspray at the edges, or color mismatch indicates prior front-end impact.
- **Sunroof drain paths** (if equipped) — pour water into front drain channels and verify exit at the lower sill; blocked drains cause moisture in the headliner and floor.
- **Door sill lower edge** — inspect the painted lower sill edge along the rocker for stone chips that have rusted; the 996 body is zinc-coated but exposed chips eventually rust.
- **Rear valance** — check the lower rear bumper underside for abrasion damage; grounding the rear valance on steep exits is common.
- **Paint depth** — run a gauge on all panels; the 996 is a modern enough body that any large-area repaints should be detectable; factory paint reads ~110–150 µm.

### 2. Drivetrain

- **IMS bearing retrofit status** — this is the most important single item on a 996.2; ask for documentation of an LN Engineering IMS Retrofit or IMS Solution installation; if no record exists, budget $2,500–$4,000 for the service (requires gearbox removal); the single-row bearing in 996.2 has an estimated failure rate of 8% `[VERIFY]` and failure destroys the engine.
- **IMS bearing oil sampling** — before buying any un-retrofitted 996.2, request an oil analysis for ferrous metal particles; metallic contamination is the earliest indicator of IMS bearing degradation.
- **Rear main seal (RMS)** — inspect the bell housing/transmission junction from underneath; any oil seeping at this joint means the RMS is leaking; the seal is replaced at the same time as the IMS bearing, so the service is typically combined.
- **Air-oil separator (AOS)** — observe exhaust on cold start; a puff of blue smoke that clears quickly is a warm-weather false positive; sustained blue smoke at startup or an oily film inside the intake pipe indicates a failing AOS; replacement is ~$300–$600 in parts and labor. `[VERIFY]`
- **Coolant pipes** — the M96 engine has plastic coolant pipes near the engine that become brittle with heat cycles; have the shop flex these; cracks cause sudden coolant loss; if not already replaced with aluminum, they should be.
- **Water pump** — should be replaced preventatively at or before 60,000 miles; ask for documentation; a failed water pump causes overheating that destroys the M96.
- **Compression/leak-down test** — mandatory; any cylinder more than 10% below average warrants a bore scope.
- **Throttle body carbon** — idle quality check; a rough or surging idle with no fault codes often traces to carbon buildup in the throttle body; cleaning is straightforward but indicates service gaps.

### 3. Suspension

- **PASM (if equipped)** — if the car has Porsche Active Suspension Management, engage both Normal and Sport modes while driving; any fault light or audible clunk in one mode means a damper or control unit is failing.
- **Front lower ball joints** — standard wear item on 996; test on a lift with a pry bar; any measurable play means replacement before the car is safely driveable.
- **Control arm bushings** — press on the wheel at 9 and 3 o'clock; any lateral movement beyond the normal specification indicates worn control arm bushings.
- **Rear multi-link geometry** — the 996 rear suspension has multiple trailing links; wheel play in multiple directions on a lift suggests bushing wear throughout the rear suspension.
- **Shock absorber seals** — inspect all four struts/shocks for oil seeping; PASM-equipped cars use active dampers that are more expensive to replace.

### 4. Interior

- **Instrument cluster pixel failure** — the 996 instrument cluster uses a graphic display for trip information that develops vertical line dropouts; if present, note as a negotiating point; OEM replacement clusters are available used but correct coding is required.
- **Seat leather and bolsters** — the driver's seat bolster compresses with use; check for foam collapse and stitching separation; correct Porsche leather is expensive to re-upholster.
- **Door mirror folding mechanism** — if power-folding mirrors are fitted, cycle them; the folding mechanism motor wears and can strip.
- **Sunroof wind deflector** (if equipped) — the fabric or rigid deflector should pop up cleanly when the sunroof opens; broken clips are a common cosmetic failure.
- **HVAC controls** — test all temperature, fan speed, and mode settings; the 996 climate control electronics are generally reliable but any non-responsive button indicates a failing control head.

### 5. Electrical

- **PSM/ABS warning lights** — scan for wheel speed sensor faults (common, sensor replacement is straightforward); verify lights extinguish after normal driving; persistent PSM lights can also trace to the brake light switch or MAF sensor.
- **OBD2 full scan (mandatory)** — connect a PIWIS or Durametric and pull all module codes, not just powertrain; look specifically for P0300-series misfires, oil pressure faults, and any active fault in the ABS/PSM module.
- **MAF sensor** — a failing mass air flow sensor causes rough idle, hesitation under load, and can trigger spurious PSM faults; cleaning is a first step, replacement is ~$200.
- **Ignition coil packs** — the M96 uses six coil-on-plug units; failed coils cause individual cylinder misfires; a scan will identify which, if any, are throwing misfire codes.
- **Window regulator motors** — cycle all four windows; worn motors or plastic regulator guides are common; rear quarter windows on Cabriolet variants are the most failure-prone.

### 6. Documentation

- **IMS/RMS/AOS service record** — the single most important document; if these three items cannot be demonstrated to have been addressed, adjust your offer to account for the service cost.
- **Water pump and coolant pipe records** — confirm these have been addressed; both are time-sensitive failures on M96 engines.
- **Oil analysis history** — if the seller has oil sample results, review for trend data; a clean trend with no metal particles is a strong positive signal.
- **Service history continuity** — look for consistent oil change intervals; M96 engines that run hot and are driven hard on degraded oil are the ones that fail earliest.
- **Accident and title history** — run a full VIN history; 996.2s are at an age where deferred accident repair is common.

### 7. Test Drive

- **Cold-start exhaust check** — stand behind the car at cold start; any blue smoke that lasts more than a few seconds is an AOS flag.
- **IMS symptom scan at idle** — let the car idle for 10 minutes post cold-start and listen for any deep bearing tone from the engine; this is a late-stage IMS symptom, but silence is still reassuring.
- **PSM engagement test** — on a clear road, make a firm brake application; the ABS should modulate cleanly with no harsh chattering; any shudder suggests rotor thickness variation or a failing wheel speed sensor.
- **Throttle response** — from a rolling 30 mph, apply throttle firmly to 60 mph; a hesitation or stumble in the mid-range suggests a throttle body or MAF issue.
- **Gearbox feel** (manual) — the 6-speed G96 should engage all gears cleanly; 2nd and 3rd gear synchros are the first to wear; test fast 3-2 downshifts.
- **Tiptronic function** (if equipped) — exercise both manual steering wheel paddles and the center console gate; missed shifts or hesitation in sport mode can indicate a worn transmission.

## 997.1 (2005–2008)

> **Generation note:** The 997.1 uses the M97 engine (3.6L base, 3.8L Carrera S). It carries over the water-cooled architecture from the 996 but with revised styling, improved interior, and new suspension geometry. The M97 retains the single-row IMS bearing of the late M96 (now in a larger diameter) **and** introduces a second major risk: cylinder bore scoring on Bank 2 (cylinders 4–6). Both must be evaluated on every 997.1 example. A borescope inspection is mandatory — not optional.

### 1. Body / Paint

- **Paint depth gauge across all panels** — the 997.1 has better corrosion protection than the 996 but is now old enough for accident repair to have been completed and forgotten; factory readings are approximately 110–150 µm.
- **Front bumper/hood gap** — check the gap between the front bumper cover and the hood on both sides; the 997's front is a common minor-impact zone; any asymmetry means the front has been disturbed.
- **Door-to-quarter alignment** — shut the door and sight down the car from the front; any surface wave in the quarter panel or door misalignment indicates accident repair.
- **Rear bumper cover fit** — the lower rear bumper valance scrapes easily; verify it sits flush and the paint matches.
- **Sunroof drain paths** (if equipped) — same protocol as 996.2; pour water into drain channels and confirm exit at sill.
- **Convertible top condition** (Cabriolet) — inspect the fabric for UV fading, seam separation, and delamination at the rear window; a failing top is $3,000–$5,000 to replace correctly. `[VERIFY]`

### 2. Drivetrain

- **IMS bearing status** — the 997.1 M97 uses a single-row IMS bearing (larger diameter than 996); the IMS Solution (permanently oil-fed plain bearing) or IMS Retrofit are the accepted fixes; ask for documentation; if no record, budget for replacement at the next clutch service; bearing failure destroys the engine as on the 996.
- **Bore scoring — borescope inspection mandatory** — the M97 engine (specifically cylinders 4–6 on the right bank) is prone to aluminum bore scoring caused by piston slap in nikasil-lined cylinders; scoring appears as longitudinal scratches on the cylinder wall; a borescope through the spark plug holes is the only reliable pre-purchase check; do not waive this on any 997.1.
- **Bore scoring symptoms** — sooty right-side exhaust tip relative to left, a light tick that increases with revs from cold, increased oil consumption, or P0300 misfire codes are all indicators; if any are present, treat the borescope as disqualifying confirmation.
- **RMS leak** — inspect the clutch bell housing from underneath; any oil seepage at the transmission-to-engine joint is the rear main seal; budget the IMS/RMS service if present.
- **AOS (air-oil separator)** — blue smoke at cold start, oily intake pipe, or elevated oil consumption (>1 quart per 1,000 miles) `[VERIFY threshold]` indicates a failing AOS; replacement is routine but should be in service history by 60,000 miles.
- **Compression and leak-down** — mandatory; if any cylinder is significantly low, immediately correlate with borescope results.
- **Clutch** — engage in 1st at low rpm; any slip or high bite point means replacement is overdue; budget clutch replacement together with IMS service if not documented.

### 3. Suspension

- **Coil spring condition** — the 997.1 is known for coil spring fractures, particularly the rear springs; a clunk from a stationary car (no obvious cause), ride height asymmetry, or reported "loud crack" noise while parked indicate a fractured spring; inspect visually on a lift.
- **Upper strut mounts** — the upper strut mount (top of strut tower) wears before the strut itself; a clunking noise over bumps that comes from the front corners is characteristically upper strut mount failure on the 997.
- **PASM condition** (if equipped) — engage Normal and Sport modes; any fault light or clunk in one mode indicates a failing active damper.
- **Front lower control arm ball joints** — pry bar test on a lift; any movement means replacement.
- **Rear multi-link bushings** — check for play in multiple directions at the rear wheel on a lift; worn rear bushings cause vagueness and accelerated tire wear.
- **Brake discs** — inspect for lip on the outer edge (deep groove means the disc has worn past spec); also look for heat cracking in a radial pattern from the hat, which indicates hard driving without proper cool-down.

### 4. Interior

- **Center console lid hinge** — the armrest lid hinge on 997.1 is a known failure; lift the lid and check that it opens and closes without the spring detent failing; replacement requires sourcing the correct hinge hardware.
- **PCM screen** (if Gen 2 PCM equipped) — verify the touchscreen responds across the entire surface; dead zones indicate a failing digitizer; PCM replacement or screen repair is $500–$1,500. `[VERIFY]`
- **Sport seat bolster condition** — the driver's bolster on sport seats compresses heavily; check foam collapse and leather separation at the side seam.
- **Headliner and A-pillar trim** — check for staining from sunroof drain backup or windscreen seal failure.
- **Steering column controls** — verify cruise control, audio, and phone controls on the steering wheel all function; stalk contact failures are common at this age.

### 5. Electrical

- **OBD2 full scan — mandatory** — connect PIWIS or Durametric and pull all module codes; specifically look for: P0300–P0306 misfires (bore scoring indicator), oil pressure faults, TPMS sensor faults, ABS/PSM wheel speed sensor faults, and any stored codes in the DME.
- **Ignition switch/lock cylinder** — 997.1 ignition switches fail and cause intermittent no-start; test by wiggling the key gently in the lock while running; any stumble indicates switch wear.
- **TPMS sensors** — the original TPMS sensors in 2005–2008 cars have batteries that fail after 7–10 years; expect the system to show fault codes; replacement sensors are straightforward but confirm none are physically broken (wheel mounting bolts crack the valve stems if impacted).
- **Ignition coil packs** — same six coil-on-plug setup as 996; check for any cylinder-specific misfire codes; a single coil is cheap to replace but multiple misfires suggest a more systemic engine issue.
- **PDK not available** — note that PDK (dual-clutch) was not introduced until the 997.2; the 997.1 is available with a 6-speed manual or Tiptronic 5-speed automatic; verify which the car has and that the Tiptronic functions cleanly through all five ranges if present.

### 6. Documentation

- **IMS and bore scoring service history** — if the car has documentation of IMS Solution/Retrofit AND a clean borescope, these are the two most value-protective pieces of paperwork possible; their absence does not necessarily mean failure has occurred, but their presence is a material positive.
- **AOS replacement** — ask for documentation; this should have been done by 60,000 miles on most examples.
- **Porsche CoA and option list** — verify Sport Chrono, PASM, sport exhaust, limited slip differential, and any other value-driving options are documented and present.
- **Service history continuity** — look for consistent 10,000-mile or annual oil changes; the M97 needs frequent oil changes to protect both the IMS bearing and the cylinder bores.
- **Accident and title history** — mandatory; run full VIN history.

### 7. Test Drive

- **Cold start — bore scoring listen** — this is the most important test drive moment; start the car cold and listen immediately for a light metallic ticking that increases with rpm; this is the earliest audible sign of bore scoring and may be inaudible at advanced warm idle.
- **Sooty exhaust check** — after a 10-minute cold drive, inspect both exhaust tips; the right exhaust tip (Bank 2, cylinders 4–6) will be noticeably blacker than the left if bore scoring is present on the affected bank.
- **Gearbox synchros** — the 997.1 6-speed has the same G97 architecture as the 996; test fast 3-2 and 4-3 downshifts; any resistance or crunch is synchro wear.
- **Brake pedal feel** — firm and progressive; any sponginess means air in the hydraulic system; a shudder under moderate braking is warped discs (common on cars used on track without ceramic brakes).
- **Rear spring clunk** — drive over a single speed bump at low speed; any single metallic clunk from the rear is a fractured coil spring until proven otherwise.
- **High-rpm pull** — the 3.6L should pull cleanly to 7,200 rpm; the 3.8L to 7,300 rpm `[VERIFY redlines]`; any stumble, flat spot, or hesitation under full load warrants a cylinder-specific diagnostics check.
- **Oil consumption baseline** — if possible, check the oil level before the test drive and again at the end; any measurable drop over a 30-minute drive indicates a significant consumption issue.
