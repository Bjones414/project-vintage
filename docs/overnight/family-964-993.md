## Summary

The 964 (1989–1994) and 993 (1995–1998) are the apex of air-cooled 911 development. Both generations use evolved forms of the air/oil-cooled flat-six originating in 1963; the 993 was the last air-cooled 911 before the M96-powered 996 arrived for 1999.

**964 quick facts.** First 911 with coil-spring suspension (replacing torsion bars), first with standard ABS, first with Tiptronic automatic, and the first production AWD 911 (Carrera 4). The 3.6L M64 engine introduced here carries through the 993. Primary failure risk: Nikasil bore scoring on early M64 engines exposed to high-sulfur fuel; timing chain tensioner wear; rear-main and valve-cover oil leaks; early ABS actuator failure.

**993 quick facts.** Multilink rear suspension (redesigned from 964 semi-trailing arms), Varioram variable-intake on the 3.6L (272 HP DIN), twin-turbo architecture on Turbo and GT2. Final air-cooled 911. Primary failure risk: hydraulic chain tensioner fatigue; Varioram solenoid/diaphragm failure; rear main seal; power-steering rack leaks; brake servo degradation.

**CRITICAL — IMS bearing does NOT apply to 964 or 993.** The intermediate shaft bearing (IMS) failure mode is exclusive to the M96/M97 water-cooled engine family used in the 996 (1999+), 997, and Boxster 986/987. Air-cooled M64 engines in 964/993 have no IMS. Any catalog entry, comp-engine heuristic, or condition flag attributing IMS risk to a 964 or 993 is incorrect and must be removed.

## Section C — Gaps and Uncertainties

### Production figures

- **964 C4 variants (Targa, Cabriolet):** Worldwide counts sourced from Porsche AG model-year summaries as republished in Excellence and Ludvigsen. US-split data is absent from any open-source registry. Resolution requires PCNA allocation records or VIN-decode analysis.
- **964 Carrera RS (Euro):** ~1,992 is the most frequently cited figure; some sources say 2,051. Authoritative resolution is the Porsche AG Zuffenhausen production database. Grey-market US imports exist but cannot be treated as a US allocation.
- **964 Turbo 3.3 / Turbo 3.6 production split:** Varies across Excellence, Ludvigsen, and Porsche AG summaries. Working estimates (~3,660 / ~1,437) are reasonable but should be cross-checked with factory records.
- **964 Turbo S unit count:** 82 and 86 both appear in literature. Sonderwunsch order records are the only resolution. ~80 is a safe working estimate.
- **964 RS 3.8:** ~55 widely cited; some sources as low as 50. Registry verification required per chassis.
- **993 per-variant production:** Aggregate 993 production is approximately 68,000 units over the run. Per-variant breakdowns differ between Porsche AG published data, Excellence archives, and marque registries. Figures in Sections A and B are working estimates with moderate confidence.
- **993 Turbo S US allocation:** ~177 is frequently cited; validate against PCNA import records or PCA Porsche Report data before using in valuation.
- **993 GT2 (street) worldwide production:** Literature ranges from 57 to 172. The 172 figure appears to represent total road-legal GT2 production; 57 may reflect a subset. Requires Porsche AG confirmation. Do not conflate with GT2 RS (~200).
- **993 GT2 RS (Clubsport):** ~200 for FIA homologation is commonly cited but unconfirmed; not a retail car.
- **993 Carrera RS Touring + Clubsport:** Combined ~1,124 is the working figure; individual Touring/Clubsport split is uncertain across sources.
- **993 Speedster:** Year of manufacture needs confirmation (1998 cited most often); both VINs should be traceable in Sonderwunsch order records.

### MSRP figures

- All MSRP figures are approximations drawn from period Excellence advertisements and period reports. None are sourced from original PCNA dealer price sheets. All should be treated as estimates pending access to 1989–1998 PCNA model-year price lists.
- **964 RS America MSRP (~$53,900):** This car was notable for being priced below the base Carrera 2 in some configurations due to stripped content. Verify with original PCNA pricing documents.
- **993 GT2 and 993 Speedster:** No standard retail MSRP exists for either. Both were special-order/Sonderwunsch; pricing was bespoke.

### Powertrain

- **964 Turbo 3.3 engine designation:** Commonly attributed to M30/69S lineage (30-series engine from 930 era); confirm whether Porsche AG used a distinct designation for the 964 application.
- **964 Turbo S HP (~381 DIN):** Some sources quote 360 DIN (same as Turbo 3.6) plus tuning package. Sonderwunsch documentation is the authority.
- **964 Carrera Cup HP (~265):** Racing-spec HP varied by year and preparation. Factory documentation may not be publicly available for competition-spec power output.
- **993 Carrera RS engine displacement:** Multiple published sources disagree. The RS engine is described as 3.8L in some Excellence articles and Porsche Klassik references; other sources describe a tuned 3.6L. Resolution requires Porsche AG engine code registry cross-reference against known RS VINs.
- **993 Turbo HP:** 408 DIN / 400 SAE. Some US documentation uses 400 HP throughout. Both figures circulate; include both in any US-market valuation reference.
- **993 Turbo S HP:** 450 DIN for European-spec cars; US-market cars may have been rated at 424 HP SAE due to emissions calibration. Verify with PCNA type approval documents.
- **993 GT2 HP (~430 DIN):** Some period sources quote 424 HP SAE for US market. Confirm with type approval.

### IMS attribution — catalog safety check

Confirm no existing catalog entry, comp-engine heuristic, or condition-flag template assigns IMS failure risk to any 964 or 993 trim. The intermediate shaft bearing (IMS) failure mode is exclusive to M96/M97 water-cooled engines in the 996 (1999+), 997, Boxster 986, and Boxster 987. Air-cooled M64 engines in 964/993 have no IMS component. The 993's hydraulic chain tensioners are a distinct and unrelated failure mode — do not label them "IMS-related" in any user-facing copy.

### Generation and body boundary flags

- **964 C2 Turbo Look package:** A 964 Carrera 2 ordered with the Turbo Look body package has wide-body fenders and Turbo-style wheels but retains the C2 engine and RWD drivetrain. It must not be matched against actual Turbo comps. Comp engine must distinguish Turbo Look option from Turbo drivetrain.
- **993 Targa body type:** The 993 Targa uses a full-width electrically sliding glass roof panel that retracts under the rear glass — it is architecturally unrelated to the 964/911 Targa bar (removable steel center section with fixed B-pillar). Do not apply the same body classification or comp pool to both.
- **964 / 993 engine family:** Both generations share the M64 air-cooled flat-six family. The 993 M64/05 adds Varioram and revised heads. The comp engine should treat these as variant codes within the same engine family, not as separate lineages. Engine generation should be a factor in comp weighting, not a hard comp exclusion.
- **993 Carrera 4S body vs 993 Carrera 4:** The 4S uses the full Turbo-width body; the standard C4 uses the narrow Carrera body. These are meaningfully different for comp matching — body width is a relevant filter.

### Sourcing gaps

- US allocation data for most 964 and 993 variants is not publicly cataloged in aggregate form. Best available sources: PCA Panorama historic registry articles, PCNA internal import records (not public), Excellence marque production summaries (print archive). The comp engine should use worldwide production as the primary figure and treat US allocation as supplementary context for rarity scoring.
- 964 Carrera RS (Euro) had no official US allocation. All US examples are grey-market or parallel imports; they exist and transact but are not PCNA-documented.
- 993 Carrera RS (Touring and Clubsport): negligible US presence. Any US examples are grey-market or NHTSA show-and-display imports (25-year rule). Do not include a US allocation figure; treat US comps for this trim as anecdotal.
